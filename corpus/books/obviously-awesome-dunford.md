## About the Book

*Obviously Awesome* is the definitive guide to product positioning for startups and growth-stage companies. April Dunford, a former VP of Marketing who led marketing at 7 successful startups (and the 6 big companies that acquired them), developed a repeatable methodology for positioning products so customers immediately understand their value.

The book's central thesis: most products fail not because they're bad, but because they're mispositioned. When a product is correctly positioned, selling it becomes easier — customers get it, they buy it, and they love it.

## Core Story: The SQL Database That Wasn't

Dunford's first job out of engineering school was as a product marketer at a startup building a "Microsoft Access killer" that supported SQL. After a huge marketing effort, they'd barely sold 200 copies. Before killing the product, they called buyers.

94 of 100 customers didn't even know they had it. But 6 had transformed their business with it — using the product on laptops and mobile devices to sync field sales orders and field service data with SQL databases at headquarters.

"Your product made me the hero of the sales team!" one customer shouted. Another: "We've increased service capacity by 60%."

The company repositioned from "Microsoft Access Killer" to "Embeddable Database for Mobile Devices." The product took off. A year of massive growth later, they were acquired by a big database company. Over 20 years later, this "failed" product still runs on mobile devices worldwide.

**The lesson:** Positioning isn't about the product. It's about understanding what problem you actually solve, for who, and against what alternatives.

## What Positioning Is (and Isn't)

### What positioning IS NOT:
- It's not messaging
- It's not a tagline
- It's not your brand story
- It's not your vision or mission
- It's not "everything you marketers cook up over there" (as one CEO told Dunford)

### Dunford's Definition:
> **"Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."**

### Positioning as Context-Setting

Positioning is like the opening scene of a movie. In *Apocalypse Now*, you're 4 minutes and 45 seconds in and you already know: Vietnam War, PTSD soldier, intense film. The opening scene answers the big questions (Where? When? Who? How should I feel?) so you can pay attention to the story.

Similarly, positioning your product in a market conveys a powerful set of assumptions:
- Who your product competes with
- What features your product should have
- Who the product is intended for
- What the product should cost

**Good positioning** sets off assumptions that are true.
**Bad positioning** sets off assumptions that aren't true — forcing sales and marketing to undo the damage.

Example: If you position a product as "email" vs "chat," buyers have completely different expectations even if features overlap. A shift in positioning can be the difference between success and failure.

## Why the Traditional Positioning Statement Fails

The classic "Mad Libs" positioning statement exercise:
- "For [target customer] who [need], [product name] is a [market category] that [key benefit], unlike [primary competitive alternative], [product] [primary differentiation]."

Dunford argues this is **not only pointless but potentially dangerous** because:
1. It assumes there's only ONE answer for each blank
2. Most products could be positioned in multiple market categories
3. It provides no methodology for *finding* the right positioning
4. It would never have revealed that "Microsoft Access Killer" = "Embeddable Database for Mobile Devices"

## The 5 Components of Positioning

Great positioning requires finding the best answer for each of these five components *in the right order*:

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if your product didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated Features/Capabilities** — What do you have that the alternatives don't?
3. **Value for Customers** — What is the value those capabilities enable?
4. **Target Customer Segmentation** — Who are the customers that care a lot about that value?
5. **Market Category** — What context positions your product so your value is obvious to your target customers?

**Critical insight:** Each component depends on the others. The flow must be sequential, starting with competitive alternatives.

## The Positioning Workflow (Customer-Centric Methodology)

Clayton Christensen's "Jobs To Be Done" theory helped Dunford crack the starting point: **always start with Competitive Alternatives**.

```
Competitive Alternatives
        ↓
Differentiated Features
        ↓
Value for Customers
        ↓
Target Customer Segmentation
        ↓
Market Category
```

**Step 1 — Competitive Alternatives:** What would customers do if your solution didn't exist? (Often: spreadsheets, manual processes, "do nothing")

**Step 2 — Differentiated Features:** What do we have that the alternatives don't? List them.

**Step 3 — Value for Customers:** For each differentiated feature, ask "So what for customers?" What is the value those capabilities enable?

**Step 4 — Target Segmentation:** Which customers care the *most* about this value? What characteristics make a customer care a lot?

**Step 5 — Market Category:** What context positions your product so your value is obvious to your target customers?

## Case Study: Janna Systems — From $2M to $80M with Repositioning

Early in Dunford's career she ran marketing for a company positioned as "Enterprise CRM." Their competitor was Siebel Systems ($2B revenue, 8,000 employees). Every sales call led to: "How are you better than Siebel?" — a losing question when you're a 24-person company doing <$2M revenue.

They had two differentiators, but didn't know how to articulate the value.

**The breakthrough:** A deal with an investment bank revealed the real value. Their unique relationship-modeling feature gave sales teams insight into *interpersonal relationships* — who knows who, who influences deals. For companies that relied heavily on personal relationships (investment banking, private client services), this was a game-changer.

**The repositioning:**
- Old: "Enterprise CRM" (competing against Siebel)
- New: "CRM for Investment Banks" (where they had a distinct advantage)

**Result:** Grew from <$2M to ~$80M in 18 months. Siebel acquired them for $1.3B.

## Positioning and Competition: Competitive Alternatives vs. Competitors

One of the most common positioning mistakes is defining "competition" too narrowly.

### Status Quo is Your First Competitor
Customers had the problem before you showed up. How were they solving it? Often:
- Spreadsheets and documents
- Manual processes
- Making do with limited functionality in their existing platform (CRM, ERP)

> **"In enterprise software, we typically lose between 20% and 30% of deals to 'no decision.'"**

Your positioning must position you as clearly superior to the status quo.

### Phantom Competitors
Many startups list every company that could *theoretically* compete as an alternative. Wrong approach.

**Phantom competitors** = companies that theoretically could compete with you, but you never actually see them or lose to them in deals.

Positioning against phantom competitors waters down your message. Until they show up in actual deals, ignore them.

### The Right Question
> "What would a customer do if your offering didn't exist?"

That answer — not your investor pitch deck's competitive landscape — is where positioning starts.

## Market Category Choices: Existing vs. New

When selecting a market category, you have two options:

### Option A: Existing Market Category
Leverage what customers already know. "Embeddable database for mobile devices" uses their existing understanding of databases, then narrows to where you win.

Benefits:
- Customers have pre-existing context
- No education cost
- You can compete on differentiation immediately

### Option B: Create a New Category
Invent a new frame of reference. You become the leader of a category you define.

The downside: You first have to make the category mean something before it serves as meaningful context. This is expensive and time-consuming.

**Dunford's counterintuitive finding:** 90% of tech companies that have gone public over the past five years were positioned in *existing* markets rather than creating new ones. Even famous "category creators" often started in existing markets first:
- Salesforce: niche CRM play until hundreds of millions in revenue
- Qualtrics: survey software until hundreds of millions in revenue

**Don't assume you need a new category to win.**

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### Trap 1: Defining competitive alternatives as "every possible competitor"
Ask who customers actually buy from or what they actually do instead. Don't add phantom competitors.

### Trap 2: Assuming you need to create a new market category to grow
New categories are expensive and time-consuming. Most successful tech companies started in existing categories.

### Trap 3: Describing features without articulating value
"We can model relationships in a different way. What can you do with it? Anything you want!" — this is how you lose. Features need to be translated into customer value.

### Trap 4: Internal positioning
Positioning feels right in the office but doesn't resonate with customers because it's not differentiated. Always test with actual prospects.

## Key Takeaways for Marketers

1. **Positioning is the foundation** — messaging, taglines, branding all come *after* positioning. Positioning is the input for everything.

2. **Start with competitive alternatives** — not features, not market category. What would customers do if you didn't exist?

3. **Differentiation only exists in context** — your features are only "differentiated" relative to what the alternatives offer.

4. **Best-fit customers** = customers who care the most about your differentiated value. Find their characteristics.

5. **Market category = the context that makes your value obvious** — choose the category where you can win, not where the big players are.

6. **Test your positioning with prospects** — positioning that feels true internally may set off wrong assumptions externally.

7. **Repositioning can be transformational** — one repositioning can take a "failed" product to acquisition-level success.

## The Methodology in Practice

April Dunford's positioning exercise (from the book):
1. Gather your positioning team (sales, marketing, product, founders)
2. Identify competitive alternatives as customers see them
3. List your differentiated capabilities vs. each alternative
4. Translate each capability into customer value ("so what?")
5. Identify customers who care the MOST about those values — their characteristics
6. Choose the market category that makes your value obvious to those customers
7. Test your positioning with actual prospects
8. Socialize positioning internally so all teams align
