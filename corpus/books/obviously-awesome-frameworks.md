## Overview

"Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning So Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It" (2019) is April Dunford's definitive guide to product positioning. Dunford spent decades as a startup executive repositioning 8 companies before distilling the process into a repeatable methodology.

**The Core Argument:** Most companies default to a product positioning statement template (the Geoffrey Moore "For X who Y, our product Z unlike A does B" formula) without understanding *why* positioning works. Good positioning makes your product's value obvious in context — so customers immediately understand why it's the best choice for them. Bad positioning forces salespeople to educate buyers from scratch on every call.

**The Central Positioning Insight:** Positioning is not a tagline or a statement. It's the context you set for your product. Context determines how everything else (value, pricing, competitive comparisons) is perceived.

## The 10-Step Positioning Process

### Step 1: Understand the Customers Who Love You
Before positioning, you need to understand your best existing customers — not your ideal target customers, but the ones who already love the product. These customers self-selected for a reason. Interview them. Find out:
- Why did they buy?
- What alternatives did they consider?
- What would they use if your product didn't exist?
- What do they value most?

Your best customers are living proof of a positioning that works. Start there.

### Step 2: Form a Positioning Team
Positioning is cross-functional. It affects how product is built, how sales sells, how marketing communicates, and how success is defined. The team needs representation from:
- Marketing (messaging and positioning ownership)
- Sales (on-the-ground customer feedback)
- Product (what the product actually does)
- Customer Success (what customers actually value post-purchase)

Without buy-in from all functions, positioning falls apart in execution. The VP of Marketing can write the positioning document, but if sales doesn't use it, it's worthless.

### Step 3: Align on a Positioning Vocabulary
Before running a positioning exercise, align the team on what words mean. "Positioning," "messaging," "value proposition," and "differentiation" mean different things to different people. Create shared definitions. Dunford's definitions:
- **Positioning:** The act of deliberately defining how you are the best at delivering something a specific set of customers cares a lot about
- **Messaging:** The specific words you use to communicate positioning
- **Value proposition:** The value you deliver to customers
- **Differentiation:** The unique features or attributes that make you distinct from alternatives

### Step 4: List Your True Competitive Alternatives
Ask customers what they would use if your product didn't exist. The answer is often not a direct competitor — it could be spreadsheets, manual processes, a different category of software, or hiring a person.

This step is critical because your positioning is always relative to alternatives. You can only claim to be "better" in the context of "better than what?" The alternatives your customers consider define the competitive landscape you need to position against.

Common mistake: companies list the competitors they fear, not the alternatives customers actually consider.

### Step 5: Isolate Your Unique Attributes
List every feature and capability your product has. Then cross off everything that competitors or alternatives also offer. What remains is your list of potentially differentiating attributes.

Push further: which of these unique attributes do customers actually value? Features that are unique but irrelevant don't help your positioning. You're looking for unique attributes that translate to customer value.

### Step 6: Map Attributes to Value Themes
Group your unique attributes into 2-4 value themes. A value theme is a cluster of features that together deliver a specific type of value to customers.

Example: "real-time collaboration," "version history," and "comment threads" might all map to a value theme of "team alignment" — the ability to keep distributed teams working from a single source of truth.

Value themes become the pillars of your positioning. They're what marketing communicates, what sales leads with, and what customer success reinforces.

### Step 7: Determine Who Cares a Lot
Not every customer values your differentiators equally. Some customer segments care deeply about your value themes; others are indifferent.

The goal is to identify the segment(s) for whom your unique value themes are highly relevant and important — not a nice-to-have but a must-have. This is your best-fit customer profile.

This step often requires narrowing focus. Companies resist targeting a smaller segment, but narrow positioning to a passionate segment beats broad positioning to an indifferent market every time.

### Step 8: Determine the Best Market Frame of Reference
The market category you position into sets the context for everything else. It tells buyers:
- What alternatives to compare you against
- What kind of buyers need you
- What price to expect
- What the product should/shouldn't do

Choose the wrong category and buyers have the wrong expectations from the start. Three category choices:
1. **Position in an existing category** — Buyers already understand the category. You just need to explain why you're better than the alternatives.
2. **Reframe an existing category** — Create a subcategory. You're "CRM for sales teams doing outbound" rather than "CRM."
3. **Create a new category** — Requires the most marketing investment (educating the market) but creates the strongest moat if you win. Best for products that are genuinely different from anything that exists.

### Step 9: Layer in Trends (Optional)
If there's a macro trend making your solution more relevant or urgent, you can layer it into your positioning. Trends create urgency — "why now" is easier to answer when there's an external force driving change.

Example: A data security product can layer in the trend of increasing regulation (GDPR, CCPA) to explain why this problem is more urgent than it used to be.

Caution: trends can become clichés quickly. If every competitor is invoking the same trend (e.g., "AI-powered"), the trend no longer differentiates you.

### Step 10: Capture Your Positioning in a Positioning Document
Write it down. The positioning document is not a marketing brief or a tagline. It captures:
- Competitive alternatives
- Unique attributes
- Value themes
- Target customers
- Market category
- Relevant trends (if applicable)

The document becomes the single source of truth that sales, marketing, and product align to. Review and update it as the market changes, competitors evolve, and the product adds capabilities.

## The Competitive Alternatives Framework

The most important insight in the book: **you don't compete against your competitors — you compete against whatever the customer would do instead of buying your product.**

The competitive alternatives framework forces you to see competition from the customer's perspective:
- Alternatives often include "do nothing" or "keep doing it manually"
- The most dangerous competitor is the one customers don't think of as a competitor
- Your positioning needs to explain why you're better than the alternative they're actually considering — not the competitor you're afraid of

**Practical exercise:** In every sales call, ask "What would you do if we didn't exist?" The answers will reshape your understanding of your competitive landscape.

## The Positioning Canvas

A one-page tool that captures the key elements of positioning in a structured format:

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| Best-fit customers | The specific segment who values your differentiators most |
| Market category | The frame of reference you want buyers to use |
| Unique attributes | Features/capabilities that competitors don't have |
| Value themes | Clusters of attributes that deliver distinct value |
| Competitive alternatives | What customers would use instead |
| Relevant trends | External forces making the problem more urgent |

The canvas should be a living document, not a one-time exercise.

## Running a Positioning Sprint

Dunford recommends a focused workshop (typically 1-2 days) to establish initial positioning:

**Pre-work:**
- Interview 6-10 best customers using the competitive alternatives and value questions
- Compile a full feature/capability list
- Identify 3-4 potential target segments

**Workshop agenda:**
1. Share customer interview findings (60 min)
2. List competitive alternatives (30 min)
3. Brainstorm unique attributes — everyone contributes (45 min)
4. Map attributes to value themes (45 min)
5. Identify who cares most about each value theme (30 min)
6. Debate market category options (60 min)
7. Draft the positioning document (60 min)

**Post-sprint:**
- Test positioning with 3-5 existing customers: does it resonate?
- Test with 3-5 sales calls: does it help close deals?
- Revise based on feedback, then lock and communicate across the team

## Why This Book Matters for B2B Marketing

Obviously Awesome is essential for B2B marketers because:
1. Most B2B products are undifferentiated to buyers even when they're genuinely unique — positioning is what fixes this
2. Sales productivity dramatically improves when reps can communicate positioning clearly
3. Marketing efficiency improves because every channel can use consistent messaging
4. Pricing power increases when value is clearly communicated
5. Customer retention improves when customers chose you for the right reasons
