# Framework Summary — "Product-Led Growth" by Wes Bush
**Synthesized from public knowledge about Wes Bush's book, the ProductLed.com blog, talks, and podcast appearances. Not a copy of the book — a structured framework extraction.**

---

## Overview

"Product-Led Growth: How to Build a Product That Sells Itself" (2019) by Wes Bush is the definitive guide to the go-to-market strategy where the product itself is the primary driver of acquisition, conversion, and expansion. Published through ProductLed.com, this book codified a movement that Slack, Dropbox, Calendly, Notion, and dozens of other breakout SaaS companies had been executing without naming it.

**The Core Argument:** Traditional SaaS go-to-market is sales-led — you hire salespeople who convince buyers to try your product. Product-led growth flips this: you let buyers try your product first, then convert them based on experienced value. This creates faster growth at lower CAC, because the product does the selling.

---

## The PLG Definition and Core Principles

**Official definition (from the book):**
> "Product-led growth is a go-to-market strategy that relies on using your product as the main vehicle to acquire, activate, and retain customers."

**The three PLG principles:**
1. **Value before money** — Users experience real value before they pay. This could be through freemium (free forever) or free trial (time-limited free access).
2. **Upgrade through experience** — The decision to pay is driven by experiencing the product's value, not by a salesperson's pitch.
3. **Viral by design** — PLG products are built so that usage naturally spreads to new users (collaboration features, sharing, "powered by" branding, notifications that non-users receive).

**Why PLG works:**
- Buyers increasingly resist talking to salespeople before they've tried a product
- The consumerization of enterprise software has created buyer expectations of self-serve evaluation
- A product that sells itself scales without proportionally scaling headcount
- PLG products create natural network effects — users invite colleagues, creating organic growth

## The MOAT Framework

The MOAT framework helps companies determine whether PLG is the right strategy and what type of free model to use.

**M — Market Strategy**
Are you going after a top-down (executive decision) or bottom-up (end-user adoption) market strategy?
- PLG works best with bottom-up strategies where individual users or small teams can adopt the product without IT or procurement approval
- Top-down enterprise sales rarely pairs well with PLG because buyers aren't end-users

**O — Ocean Conditions**
What's the competitive environment?
- **Red ocean** (saturated, commoditized): PLG helps you differentiate by letting buyers experience your product without risk — removes a barrier to switching
- **Blue ocean** (new category, little competition): PLG helps you educate the market cheaply — early adopters self-serve and spread the product

**A — Audience**
Who is your target user?
- Is the user technically savvy enough to self-serve onboarding?
- Does the user have the authority to try and adopt the product without organizational approval?
- Is the user already aware of the problem your product solves?

**T — Time-to-Value**
How quickly can a new user experience meaningful value?
- PLG requires fast time-to-value — if it takes days or weeks for a user to see what the product does, PLG doesn't work
- The faster you can get users to their "aha moment," the better your PLG conversion rates

## Freemium vs. Free Trial Decision Framework

One of the most practical sections of the book. The question isn't "should we offer free?" but "which type of free fits our product and business?"

**Freemium:**
- Free forever with limitations (features, usage, seats, etc.)
- Best when: viral/network effects exist; product value is clear with limited features; you can monetize a small % of free users; acquisition cost via product is very low
- Risk: you need huge free user volume to generate meaningful paid conversions; support costs for free users can be high

**Free Trial:**
- Full product free for a limited time (7, 14, 30 days)
- Best when: product value requires using the full feature set; time pressure creates urgency; you have enough onboarding infrastructure to get users to value quickly
- Risk: if users don't reach value before the trial ends, they churn and never convert

**The Decision Matrix (from ProductLed.com):**

| Question | Points to Freemium | Points to Free Trial |
|---|---|---|
| Core value clear with limited access? | Yes → Freemium | No → Free Trial |
| Viral/sharing features? | Yes → Freemium | No → Free Trial |
| Short time-to-value? | Either works | Required for Free Trial |
| High support cost? | Risky for Freemium | Manageable |
| High-touch onboarding needed? | Freemium struggles | Free Trial better |

**The hybrid model:** Many successful PLG companies use both — freemium for individual/team adoption, free trial for enterprise buyers who want a time-limited evaluation of full enterprise features.

## How to Redesign for PLG

Bush outlines a fundamental shift in how teams need to think and work when transitioning to PLG.

**The three organizational shifts:**

1. **From sales-led to product-led onboarding**
   - Remove all friction from signup
   - Redesign onboarding to get users to value without human involvement
   - Build in-app guidance, contextual tips, and empty states that show what's possible
   - Measure time-to-value as a primary product metric

2. **From feature-centric to outcome-centric development**
   - PLG products are built around user goals, not feature lists
   - Every new feature must answer: "Does this help users get to value faster? Does this create sharing/viral moments?"
   - Kill features that users don't use and that add onboarding complexity

3. **From lead-gen to product-qualified leads**
   - In PLG, the product generates leads (PQLs) not just marketing
   - Sales team's job shifts from cold outreach to converting engaged free users
   - Marketing's job shifts from top-of-funnel volume to activation and expansion

**The PLG redesign checklist:**
- Can a new user sign up without talking to a salesperson?
- Can they reach the core value proposition within their first session?
- Does the product have natural sharing or invitation moments?
- Can users upgrade without leaving the product?
- Is pricing clearly visible in-product?

## Product Qualified Leads (PQLs)

PQLs are the central concept of PLG pipeline. A PQL is a user who has taken specific actions in the product that indicate readiness to pay.

**PQLs vs. MQLs (Marketing Qualified Leads):**
- MQL: "This person downloaded our ebook and attended a webinar" → expressed marketing interest
- PQL: "This person created 5 projects, invited 3 colleagues, and hit the free tier limit" → demonstrated product value and expansion intent

**How to define your PQLs:**
1. Analyze what your best customers did in their first 30 days before converting
2. Identify the 2-3 actions most correlated with paid conversion
3. Set thresholds: when a user crosses these thresholds, flag them as a PQL for sales or automated expansion

**PQL examples by product type:**
- Collaboration tool: invited 3+ team members AND created 5+ shared documents
- Analytics product: ran 10+ queries AND viewed dashboards 3+ times in a week
- Developer tool: made 50+ API calls AND viewed pricing page

**Why PQLs matter:** Sales teams working PQLs have dramatically higher conversion rates than cold outreach because they're talking to users who already see value. CAC drops, win rates rise.

## The PLG Flywheel

The PLG flywheel shows how each stage of the customer journey feeds the next:

```
Acquisition (users discover product via sharing, word of mouth, SEO)
    ↓
Activation (users reach their first value moment — "aha moment")
    ↓
Retention (users keep returning because value compounds)
    ↓
Revenue (users convert to paid or expand usage)
    ↓
Referral (users share, invite colleagues, become advocates)
    ↓ (back to Acquisition)
```

**The critical flywheel stage:** Activation is the most underinvested and highest-leverage part of PLG. If users don't reach their aha moment in the first session or two, the flywheel stops. Most companies over-invest in acquisition and under-invest in activation.

**Aha moment definition:** The moment a user first understands the core value of the product through direct experience. For Slack: sending your first message and getting an instant reply. For Dropbox: accessing a file from a different device. For Calendly: sharing a booking link and having someone book instantly.

## Pricing for PLG Products

Pricing in PLG serves two functions: it determines who converts, and it creates natural expansion revenue.

**PLG pricing principles:**
1. **Make pricing transparent and self-serve** — Hidden pricing or "contact sales for pricing" kills PLG conversion. Buyers should be able to upgrade without a conversation.
2. **Align pricing to value** — Price based on the unit of value the product delivers (seats, usage, outcomes), not arbitrary tiers.
3. **Design for expansion** — Start low and expand as usage grows. This creates natural net revenue retention > 100%.
4. **Free tier should convert, not just exist** — Freemium works only if the free tier creates genuine value AND creates obvious reasons to upgrade.

**Common PLG pricing models:**
- **Per seat:** Most common. Free 1-3 seats; pay for 4+. Works well for collaboration tools.
- **Usage-based:** Free up to X uses/month; pay for more. Works well for API products, analytics tools.
- **Feature-gated:** Core features free; advanced features paid. Works when there's a clear distinction between basic and advanced user needs.
