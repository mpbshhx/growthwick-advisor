# Revenue Architecture for Coaches and Consultants
**Source: Tara McMullin — whatworks.fyi | whatworks.fyi/blankslate | whatworks.fyi/work-with-tara**

---

## McMullin's Revenue Philosophy

Tara McMullin approaches revenue architecture from a needs-first perspective rather than a revenue-maximization frame. The key question isn't "How can I make the most money?" — it's "What revenue structure would give me stability, allow me to do great work, and not extract more than I can sustain?"

This produces different answers than standard coaching industry advice. McMullin doesn't chase "passive income" as an end goal. She doesn't prescribe a specific price point. She designs revenue structures to fit the owner's actual life, then checks whether the math works.

---

## The Two Revenue Questions That Come First

Before designing any revenue structure, McMullin establishes two numbers:

**1. Your Revenue Floor**
The minimum monthly income you need to meet your actual obligations, cover business expenses, and feel financially stable (not wealthy — stable). This is not aspirational. It's operational.

If your floor is $8,000/month and your current model can't reliably produce $8,000/month at a pace you can sustain, you have a structural problem — not a marketing problem.

**2. Your Revenue Ceiling**
The maximum income your current model can produce given your time, capacity, and client relationships. This is determined by: (number of clients you can serve well) × (average client revenue).

If your ceiling is only slightly above your floor, you have no margin for growth, mistakes, or downtime. The model needs redesign before you can grow sustainably.

**The gap between floor and ceiling is your strategic constraint.** A healthy business has a ceiling that's 2-3x the floor, giving room to run at partial capacity without hitting financial stress.

## Revenue Structure Options McMullin Has Personally Used

McMullin's own bio lists the revenue models she's operated directly. Here's how she frames each:

### 1. Coaching and Consulting (1:1)
**The classic service model.** You trade time and expertise for a fee, delivered through sessions, retainers, or project engagements.

**McMullin's take:**
- Ceiling is hard because it's time-bound
- High margin, but only if priced correctly (most coaches underprice dramatically)
- Best for owners who have strong relational energy and enjoy deep 1:1 work
- Requires explicit scope management or it bleeds into unlimited service

**Structural variations:**
- **Hourly**: Maximum flexibility, minimum predictability. Client controls the pace.
- **Retainer**: Fixed monthly fee for defined access. Predictable income, requires clear scope.
- **Package**: Fixed deliverable, fixed fee. Predictable scope, but income is episodic.
- **Project-based**: Scoped engagement with a defined endpoint. Clean transitions, but creates income gaps between projects.

**McMullin's framework for choosing**: What's your risk tolerance for income volatility? Retainers reduce it. Projects increase it. Packages are mid. Match this to your financial stability and personal stress tolerance.

### 2. Group Coaching and Mastermind Programs
**One-to-many structure** that multiplies leverage. You serve multiple clients through a shared experience.

**The economic logic**: Instead of one client at $2,000/month, you run a group of 8 at $500/month = $4,000/month for similar delivery time.

**McMullin's caveats:**
- Group programs require more marketing than 1:1 (you need a cohort, not just one client)
- The experience is different — peer learning and community dynamics become part of the value
- Cohort-based programs create income cliffs (all revenue arrives at launch; none between)
- Ongoing groups (mastermind model) have better revenue predictability but require keeping members engaged month-to-month

**Structural variations:**
- **Cohort-based program**: Fixed start/end dates, group goes through curriculum together
- **Rolling enrollment group**: Members join anytime, content is evergreen
- **Mastermind/Peer group**: Primarily peer-to-peer with facilitation, lighter content delivery
- **Group + 1:1 hybrid**: Group experience with individual coaching calls included

### 3. Training and Licensing
McMullin has operated a training and licensing program — essentially teaching others her methods and allowing them to use her frameworks in their own work.

**Revenue logic**: One-to-many at the delivery level, but also leverages IP rather than just time. Revenue isn't purely capped by McMullin's hours.

**Use case for Growthwick clients**: Coaches and consultants who have developed genuine methodology and want to multiply impact without multiplying their personal hours. Requires confidence in the framework's teachability and a customer base ready to become practitioners.

### 4. Membership Community
McMullin ran CoCommercial — a paid community platform for small business owners — for several years before transitioning.

**The membership model economics**: Monthly recurring revenue, relatively low price per member, value is in the community itself (peer connections, access to content, and facilitated learning).

**McMullin's honest assessment** (based on her eventual pivot): Membership communities require significant ongoing facilitation energy to maintain value. The "passive recurring income" framing undersells the activation and retention work required. Works best when community interaction itself is genuinely valuable independent of content.

**Structural elements of a revenue-viable membership:**
- Price must be high enough to sustain delivery but low enough for easy renewal decisions
- Value must be clear and felt regularly (not just theoretically)
- Community needs critical mass to self-sustain; below that mass, the operator is doing all the work
- Exit/pause policies matter — how you handle churn affects cash flow and community dynamics

### 5. Micro Media Business (Newsletter + Podcast)
**Not a direct revenue model** — but McMullin's newsletter and podcast generate business through audience development, brand authority, and downstream conversion to paid services.

**McMullin's economics**: The media business is the marketing engine that makes her consulting and programs available without paid advertising. She builds audience trust through free content, then offers paid products and services to that audience.

**Monetization paths from media**:
- Paid programs and guides (Blank Slate — $99)
- Coaching and consulting (Insight Intensive at $500; project coaching at custom rates)
- Sponsorships (not McMullin's current model, but common in this space)
- Premium membership or paid newsletter tier

## The Operational Sustainability Calculator

McMullin includes an "Operational Sustainability Calculator" with the Blank Slate guide. The calculator models:
- Revenue floor vs. current revenue
- Client capacity vs. current client load
- Hours required vs. hours available
- Margin between floor and ceiling

The output is a concrete assessment of whether the current or proposed model is structurally sustainable — not based on aspirations, but on actual operational math.

## Pricing Philosophy

McMullin's pricing framework is not "charge your worth" (which she considers vague and unhelpful). It's:

**1. Start with what you need**
What monthly revenue do you need, and at what client volume are you comfortable? Divide the target by the number — that's your minimum price. Then price above that to give yourself margin.

**2. Test recognition, not just willingness to pay**
The right price is one that clients accept without long deliberation — because the offer matches something they already wanted. If you're fighting objections on price constantly, often the problem is offer-recognition, not price level.

**3. Raise prices to control volume**
If you're at capacity and burning out, raising prices is a capacity management tool. Fewer clients at higher fees = same revenue with more operational headroom.

**4. Avoid the escalation trap**
Many coaches build revenue by adding more clients at the same price. McMullin's preferred path: fewer clients at higher prices, with better delivery. This maintains quality and reduces the sustainability risk of over-extension.

## Revenue Architecture for Boutique Service Businesses: The McMullin Stack

If designing from scratch for a coach/consultant, McMullin's approach produces something like:

**Layer 1 (Floor)**: 3-6 ongoing 1:1 retainer clients at a fee that covers the revenue floor with room to spare. These are the stable anchor clients.

**Layer 2 (Growth)**: One group program per year (cohort-based) at a lower per-person price point, serving a larger audience and creating a proof-of-concept for scalable delivery.

**Layer 3 (Leverage)**: A media asset (newsletter, podcast, or both) that generates ongoing audience trust without ongoing client acquisition cost.

**Layer 4 (Products)**: One or more evergreen guides/frameworks (like Blank Slate) that generate revenue without delivery time.

Not every business needs all four layers. The point is to build toward multiple income streams that don't all require equal amounts of your time and energy.
