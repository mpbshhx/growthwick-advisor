# Business Model Design for Service Businesses
**Source: Tara McMullin — whatworks.fyi/blankslate | whatworks.fyi/work-with-tara**

## The Core Problem McMullin Solves

Most service business owners inherit their business model rather than design it. They copy what others in their niche do, or they follow a course template, or they just do "what makes sense" for their trade. The result: a business that sort of works but was never actually designed for the specific person running it.

McMullin's business model design work is explicitly about **starting from your constraints** rather than from an industry template. The model is a hypothesis about how your specific business can create and capture value, given your specific needs, capacities, and client base.

## The Five-Layer Business Model Framework

McMullin's Blank Slate guide structures business model design around five interconnected elements:

### 1. Theory of Business (the "Why it works" layer)
Before designing any model component, the owner needs a clear **theory of business** — the underlying logic that explains why people will pay, what transformation or result they're buying, and how the business creates value that others can't easily replicate.

This is Drucker's concept applied to micro-businesses: "what a company is, what it represents, and what its basic concepts, values, policies, and beliefs are."

A strong theory of business:
- Survives market changes (you know what to hold onto)
- Gives clarity on what to add, cut, or change
- Connects the brand story to the operational reality

**Diagnostic question:** If someone asked "why does your business exist and why does it work?", could you answer in 3 sentences without using the words "passion" or "help"?

### 2. The Offer (the "What clients buy" layer)
McMullin draws a sharp distinction between:
- **A product people want to buy** — clients immediately recognize it as what they've been looking for
- **A product people can be convinced to buy** — requires an elaborate sales argument

The second type is exhausting to sell, often has high refund rates, and creates misaligned client expectations. The first type sells itself.

**Recognition** is McMullin's operative concept. The offer should match something the client is already actively or latently looking for. Three paths:
1. Solve a **readily apparent problem** (the client can name it)
2. Tap a **deeply felt desire** (the client can feel it even if they can't articulate it)
3. **Jump on an emerging trend** (the client is already paying attention to the category)

**Practical test:** Can your ideal client explain what they get from your offer in one sentence, without you coaching them? If not, the recognition factor is too low.

### 3. Delivery Structure (the "How it happens" layer)
This is where capacity meets service. McMullin identifies the key variables that determine whether a service model is sustainable:

**Time-for-money ratio**: How many hours does delivery require per client or cohort? What's the actual hourly rate when you account for prep, admin, and client communication — not just "session time"?

**One-to-one vs. one-to-many**: Pure 1:1 services hit a capacity ceiling fast. Group or community structures multiply leverage but require different client relationships and marketing approaches.

**Synchronous vs. asynchronous**: Live delivery (calls, cohort sessions) is high-touch but schedule-constrained. Async delivery (courses, recorded programs, email-based) is scalable but lower-touch. Hybrid models blend both.

**Ongoing vs. project-based**: Retainers/ongoing engagements create revenue predictability but require continuous delivery energy. Project-based work is episodic and allows breathing room but creates income volatility.

**McMullin's framework for choosing**: Map your preferred work style (do you like sustained relationships or episodic deep dives?), your capacity constraints (how many clients can you hold well?), and your revenue needs (what monthly floor do you need to feel safe?) — then design the delivery structure that fits all three.

### 4. Capacity Design (the "How much" layer)
This is perhaps the most neglected layer in service business design. McMullin asks owners to think explicitly about:

**Maximum sustainable clients**: Not how many clients you *could* technically serve, but how many you can serve well without degrading quality or burning out.

**Revenue floor vs. revenue ceiling**: What's the minimum monthly income needed for stability? What's the maximum achievable with your current capacity? Is there a gap between the two that requires a model change?

**Operational leverage**: What parts of delivery can be systematized, delegated, or templated without losing quality? Where does the "you" have to be fully present?

**The sustainability test**: If you ran at full capacity for 12 months straight, what would you have to give up? If the answer includes your health, relationships, or creative energy — the model is not actually sustainable.

### 5. Operations (the "How it keeps running" layer)
McMullin argues that operational design is as creative as any other part of business strategy. The question "How do we make this happen?" opens into process design, tooling choices, workflow structure, and (eventually) team structure.

Key operational decisions for service businesses:
- **Client onboarding**: What does a client experience in their first 30 days? Is it systematized or improvised?
- **Communication protocols**: How and when do clients reach you? What's in-scope vs. out-of-scope?
- **Delivery documentation**: Are your frameworks and processes documented, or entirely in your head?
- **Referral systems**: Does your business generate its own leads through satisfied client networks, or is it entirely marketing-dependent?

## Common Business Model Failure Patterns

McMullin identifies these as the most common structural problems:

**1. Model-by-default** — you're doing what your peers do without asking whether it fits you. "I'm a coach so I do 1:1 sessions" without questioning whether 1:1 is the right structure.

**2. Capacity-blind growth** — adding clients without modeling what "full" looks like. You reach full capacity and discover the model is financially thin or operationally unsustainable.

**3. Recognition gap** — the offer is designed around what the owner loves to deliver, not what clients immediately recognize as valuable. Results in good service but hard sales.

**4. No revenue floor** — business has a ceiling (you can't take more clients) but no floor (income is too variable to feel safe). This creates anxiety that drives bad decisions.

**5. Operational dependence** — the business can't run without the owner being "on" at all times. No systems, no delegation, no breathing room. This is sustainability's nemesis.

## The Blank Slate Redesign Trigger

McMullin recommends stepping back for a Blank Slate redesign when:
- You're on a plateau that doesn't feel like progress
- You're adding new ideas without removing old ones
- You're working more but earning about the same
- The work feels fine but the business feels like a trap
- You realize you built the business for an earlier version of yourself

The core question she poses: *"Are you working with what you've got rather than imagining what you could create?"*
