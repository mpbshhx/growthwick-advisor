## The Core Premise of "What Works"

The central question McMullin has chased for 15+ years: **What actually works for small, owner-operated service businesses — not theoretically, but in practice?**

Her answer is deliberately anti-formula. She doesn't sell a playbook. Instead, she teaches business owners to think rigorously about their own situation, apply systems thinking, and build businesses that are *durable* rather than just initially profitable.

Her philosophy rests on four commitments:
1. **Fundamentals over tactics** — most "growth hacks" are noise; durable businesses are built on sound fundamentals
2. **Nuanced analysis over easy answers** — every business is different; cookie-cutter advice creates cookie-cutter problems
3. **Sustainability over profit maximization** — a business that burns you out is not a success
4. **Systems thinking over one-off fixes** — problems recur when you treat symptoms rather than causes

## The Blank Slate Business Design Process

McMullin's most complete framework is the **Blank Slate** process — a step-by-step guide for service business owners to redesign their business from the ground up. It's structured as six chapters, each building on the last:

### Chapter 1: Needs & Priorities
*"Strategy is an architecture for decision-making."*

The starting point is radically owner-centric. Before you design an offer, before you think about marketing — what do YOU need?

McMullin asks clients:
- How much do you want to earn, and how important is that number to you?
- How flexible do you want your schedule to be?
- Do you prefer to fill your time with people or with projects?

This is not fluffy self-reflection. It's **strategic constraint setting**. By clarifying your needs and priorities first, you eliminate a huge chunk of options that would never actually work for your life. Every subsequent decision is filtered through this foundation.

Her framing: there are two ways to build a business — (1) as a money-making venture, and (2) as a needs-meeting pursuit. When you optimize purely for money-making, you often build a business that works financially but hollows you out. Needs-meeting framing asks: whose needs are we meeting, and are mine included?

**Key principle:** "The only way to achieve long-term sustainability is by making sure your business takes care of you."

### Chapter 2: Theory of Business
*"A company's theory of business communicates what it is, what it represents, and what its basic concepts, values, policies, and beliefs are."*

A "theory of business" (borrowed from Drucker) is the underlying logic of why your business works. It's not your mission statement — it's the actual operational thesis. What problem are you solving? For whom? Why will they pay? What do you believe about how value gets created?

A strong theory of business:
- Helps you face novel challenges without losing focus
- Creates clarity on what to say yes or no to
- Connects your brand narrative to your operational reality

### Chapter 3: Model & Structure
McMullin treats business models as **hypotheses**, not blueprints. A blank-slate business model is a strong guess about how things could work — not a prescription for how they should.

This framing frees owners from copying industry templates and instead asks: given my needs, my clients' needs, and my theory of business, what model would actually work?

### Chapter 4: Your Offer
*"The fastest path to business success is to make a product people want to buy — not a product that people can be easily convinced to buy."*

**Recognition** is the key concept here. The best offer is one clients can immediately recognize as the thing they've been looking for (even if they didn't know they were looking for it). This is different from a product that requires an elaborate sales argument.

Three paths to recognition: solve a readily apparent problem, tap into a deeply felt desire, or jump on an emerging trend.

### Chapter 5: Operations
Operations is where strategy meets reality. McMullin argues that operational design has the same creative potential as any other part of business strategy. The question "How do we make this happen?" has enormous room for novel, clever, and even playful answers.

This is where capacity planning, workflow design, and team structure live.

### Chapter 6: Marketing
McMullin's model: any effective marketing ecosystem fulfills three core objectives:
1. **Discovery** — getting found by the right people
2. **Nurturing** — deepening the relationship over time
3. **Evaluation** — helping prospective clients decide

Note the absence of "conversion" as the primary objective. Marketing's job is connection, not persuasion.

## Practical Application for Growthwick Clients

For service business owners (coaches, consultants, freelancers):

**Start here, not with tactics:**
- Before asking "how should I market?" ask "what do I actually need from this business?"
- Before designing an offer, ask "will my ideal clients immediately recognize this as something they want?"
- Before scaling, ask "does my current model have the structural capacity to scale without breaking me?"

**The What Works diagnostic questions:**
1. Is your business built around your needs, or despite them?
2. Do you have a clear theory of business — not just a mission statement, but an operational thesis?
3. Is your offer one clients recognize immediately, or one you have to sell hard?
4. Does your marketing ecosystem create discovery, nurturing, AND evaluation — or just one?

**The anti-pattern McMullin identifies repeatedly:** business owners who add new ideas without removing old ones, who assume current constraints are permanent, and who optimize for tactics rather than fundamentals. The result is a business that's technically running but structurally unsound — held together by effort rather than design.

## The "What Works" Podcast Frame

The podcast uses the same core lens: "What's working (and what's not) in the 21st-century economy for knowledge workers, service businesses, and small operators?" Topics include money, management, culture, media, philosophy — always filtered through the question of sustainable, humane work.

McMullin's politics are explicitly anti-hustle, anti-maximization, and oriented toward what she calls "reducing harm rather than maximizing output." This is a distinctive positioning in a market full of growth-at-all-costs coaching.
