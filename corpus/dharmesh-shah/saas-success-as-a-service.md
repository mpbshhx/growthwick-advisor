# Secret To SaaS Success: Recognize That You're Not Selling Software

By Dharmesh Shah | March 7, 2017

## The Core Reframe

In the old days of software, you shipped bits to a customer. They installed them. If they succeeded, great. If they didn't, you'd already been paid — such was life, and you moved on.

Today, in SaaS, if the customer doesn't succeed, they cancel. The math doesn't work.

**SaaS = Success as a Service**

"If you're in the SaaS business, the only way to survive in the long-term is not to just deliver software. It's to deliver success. You have to actually deliver the benefit that the software is promised to provide. And, if the customer fails to get that benefit then you have failed."

This isn't just a positioning shift. It's a structural one. Revenue is recurring, which means your business success is permanently downstream of your customer's success.

## What This Looks Like at HubSpot

HubSpot invests heavily in three areas because of this insight:

### 1. Onboarding
If you help customers get started, they're more likely to actually succeed. Especially for broad or sophisticated products, customers often need help. Onboarding works.

### 2. Education
HubSpot Academy educates the broader marketing industry — not just HubSpot customers. Why spend millions educating people who aren't even paying you?

Because HubSpot's success depends on the success of their customers. And customers succeed faster when they understand inbound marketing, not just HubSpot the product.

### 3. Community
HubSpot hosts an online community for marketers (inbound.org — 200,000+ members at time of writing). It allows marketers to find the best content, discuss topics of interest, post jobs, find jobs.

Not a product feature. A success infrastructure investment.

## The Paradox of SaaS

The higher the purchase price in traditional software, the lower the chances of seeing success. Multi-million dollar software purchases that never saw the light of day are everywhere in enterprise history.

SaaS flips this: your revenue model forces you to care about adoption, usage, and outcomes — not just signing contracts.

**The lesson:** You can't sell software anymore. You sell success. Build the onboarding, education, and community infrastructure that makes success the default outcome for your customers.
