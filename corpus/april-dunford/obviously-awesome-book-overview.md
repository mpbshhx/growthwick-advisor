## The Dunford Positioning Framework (Core Concepts)

### 5 Components of Positioning

1. **Competitive alternatives:** What would customers do if your product didn't exist? (Not "competitors" — actual alternatives including spreadsheets, doing nothing)
2. **Unique attributes:** What features/capabilities do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value:** What value do those unique attributes enable for customers? (The "so what")
4. **Customer segments:** Who cares a LOT about that specific value?
5. **Market category:** What context makes the value obvious and credible?

### Why This Order Matters

Most companies start at features and work forward. Dunford's insight: you must start at competitive alternatives and work backward. The competitive context determines what's actually unique, which determines what's actually valuable.

### The Market Category Trap

Most companies define their market category by analogy to what already exists ("It's like Salesforce but for small business" / "It's an AI content tool"). Dunford argues: sometimes the right category doesn't exist yet, and creating a new category can be the most powerful positioning move — but only if you have the resources to actually educate the market.

### "Positioning is not taglines."

Strong positioning isn't a slogan. It's a strategic decision about which market you're competing in and what makes you the obvious best choice for specific customers in that market. Everything downstream (messaging, sales pitch, content, pricing) flows from this.
