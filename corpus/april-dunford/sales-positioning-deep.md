## Why Positioning Fails in the Sales Process

April Dunford's most practically valuable contribution may not be the positioning frameworks themselves — it's her diagnosis of why positioning breaks down between marketing and sales.

**The core problem:** Marketing creates positioning. Sales doesn't use it.

Reasons sales doesn't use marketing's positioning:
1. The positioning is written for ads and websites — not for 1:1 conversations
2. Salespeople discover their own language through trial and error on calls
3. The positioning doesn't help salespeople handle the objections they actually face
4. Sales doesn't understand why the positioning choices were made

The result: marketing messages one thing; sales says something else; customer success sees something else again. Positioning fragmentation destroys trust with buyers who encounter the company across multiple channels.

## The Positioning → Sales Process

Dunford argues for a direct connection between positioning decisions and sales conversation design:

### Step 1: Context-Setting (Not Discovery)
Traditional sales training says: "lead with discovery questions." Dunford challenges this.

Her framework: **You already know more about the buyer's problem than they do.** You built a product to solve it. Leading with discovery when you already know the answer wastes everyone's time and makes you look uncertain.

Instead, lead with **context-setting**: briefly frame the problem in the way your positioning has defined it.

"We work with B2B SaaS companies that are growing fast but finding that their outbound sales team is spending 60% of their time manually researching prospects instead of selling. Does that sound like your situation?"

This:
- Quickly identifies whether the buyer is in your ICP
- Demonstrates expertise and understanding
- Gets the buyer nodding before you've asked any questions
- Sets up the conversation on your terms

### Step 2: Competitive Alternative Acknowledgment
Before presenting your solution, acknowledge what the buyer is currently doing instead.

"Most companies we talk to are dealing with this in one of three ways: they're either using LinkedIn Sales Navigator manually, they've hired a research analyst, or they've tried to build something internally. Which of those is closest to what you're doing?"

This:
- Shows you understand the competitive landscape from the buyer's perspective (not yours)
- Creates an opening to explain why your solution beats the alternative they're actually using
- Avoids the awkward moment of proposing a solution before understanding the context

**Dunford's key insight on competitive alternatives in sales:** Every buyer has an alternative — even if it's "do nothing" or "keep using a spreadsheet." Salespeople who don't ask about alternatives can't position effectively against them.

### Step 3: Unique Value Statement (Not Feature List)
After establishing the problem and the alternative, deliver the unique value statement — not a feature list.

Poor: "Our platform has 47 features including real-time data sync, AI-powered lead scoring, and 200+ integrations."

Strong: "What we've found is that companies using [your product] cut prospect research time by 80% in the first 30 days. That means your SDRs spend almost all their time on outreach instead of research — which is what they're actually paid to do."

The difference:
- Features describe what the product does
- Value statements describe what the buyer gets
- Buyers buy value; they don't buy features

### Step 4: The Positioned Demo
For products with demos, Dunford advocates for a "positioned demo" — a demo specifically designed around the buyer's context and competitive alternative.

Poor demo: "Let me show you all the features of our platform." (feature walk)

Positioned demo: "Based on what you told me — you're using LinkedIn Sales Navigator manually and it takes your team about 3 hours per prospect — I want to show you specifically how we solve that, and then you can tell me if the rest of the platform is relevant."

The positioned demo:
- References the buyer's specific situation (established in Step 1-2)
- Shows only the features most relevant to their problem
- Creates a natural comparison to their current alternative
- Builds to a specific, relevant "aha moment"

### Step 5: Objection Handling with Positioning
Every objection has a positioning-informed response. Dunford's framework for objection handling:

**The objection root cause model:**
Most sales objections come from one of three causes:
1. **Positioning mismatch** — The buyer isn't actually in your ICP; you're talking to the wrong person
2. **Value not communicated** — The buyer understands the product but not why it's worth the price
3. **Competitive confusion** — The buyer has a wrong mental model about how you compare to an alternative

Each cause requires a different response:
- Positioning mismatch → Requalify or redirect to the right stakeholder
- Value not communicated → Go back to the specific outcome they care about and connect features to that outcome
- Competitive confusion → Explicitly address the comparison they're making, on your terms

## The Sales Positioning Playbook

Dunford's recommended artifact for sales teams:

**Section 1: Our Best-Fit Customer**
- Company profile: size, industry, growth stage, technology stack
- Buyer persona: role, title, goals, day-to-day challenges
- The trigger: what event or pain makes them open to a new solution now?

**Section 2: The Alternatives They're Considering**
- What are they currently using?
- What other products are they evaluating?
- Why do they often choose not to buy at all?

**Section 3: Our Unique Value (by Alternative)**
For each alternative, a specific explanation of why we're better:
- vs. Manual process: "We save X hours per week per rep"
- vs. [Competitor A]: "Unlike them, we do [specific thing]; they can't because [reason]"
- vs. Do nothing: "The cost of not solving this is [specific outcome]"

**Section 4: The Proof**
- Customer stories organized by alternative and use case
- Specific ROI data points
- Third-party validation (analyst reports, review sites)

**Section 5: Objection-to-Response Map**
Every common objection with the positioning-informed response.

## The "Whole Product" Positioning Concept

Dunford draws on Geoffrey Moore's "whole product" concept in her sales positioning work:

The "whole product" is everything a buyer actually needs to achieve the value you're promising — not just the software itself.

Components of the whole product:
- **Core product** — The software
- **Implementation and onboarding** — How hard is it to get started?
- **Integrations** — Does it work with what they already use?
- **Training and support** — How does the customer learn to get value?
- **Customer community** — Can they learn from other users?
- **Partner ecosystem** — Can they extend the product with certified partners?

**Why this matters for sales positioning:** Buyers evaluate the whole product, not just the features. A sales positioning strategy that only addresses features misses half the evaluation.

Salespeople who explicitly walk through the whole product in the sales process — including implementation, integrations, and support — address objections before they become objections.

## Positioning Ownership: Who Is Responsible?

One of Dunford's most important (and controversial) positions:

**Positioning is not owned by marketing. It is owned by the CEO.**

Reason: Positioning affects every function. Product builds to the positioning. Sales sells to it. Customer success onboards to it. Marketing communicates it. If the CEO doesn't own the positioning decision and enforce alignment, each function develops its own version.

**The practical implication for sales:**
- Sales leadership must be in the room when positioning decisions are made
- Sales has veto power on positioning that doesn't work in conversations
- Marketing has veto power on positioning that creates unrealistic expectations
