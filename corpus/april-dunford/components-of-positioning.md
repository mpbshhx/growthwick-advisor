# An Introduction to Positioning — April Dunford

## What Positioning Is NOT

When April talks about positioning at conferences, she starts by defining what it's NOT:
- Not the same as messaging
- Not a tagline
- Not your brand story
- Not your vision or mission
- Not "everything you marketers cook up over there"

## Defining Positioning

**"Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."**

## Positioning as Context Setting

Positioning = context-setting for products. Like the opening scene of a movie.

The opening scene answers the big questions: Where are we? What year? What's happening? How should I feel? Who are these people?

Until those big questions are answered, it's hard to follow the details.

**Apocalypse Now example:**
- Shot of beach with palm trees → smoke → helicopter → WHOOSH, whole scene on fire
- Shift to Martin Sheen's face → drinking, smoking, messy room
- "Saigon. Shit. I'm still only in Saigon."
- 4 minutes 45 seconds in: Vietnam war, PTSD, intense film — you know everything you need to settle into the story

## Providing a Frame of Reference

Positioning a product in a market sets off powerful assumptions about:
- Who it competes with
- What features it should have
- Who the product is intended for
- What the product should cost

**Email vs. Chat example:**
- "Email" triggers: spam filter, organize/store conversations, calendar integration
- "Chat" triggers: instant delivery, read receipts, real-time communication
- Large feature overlap, but completely different buyer expectations
- Positioning in either category transforms how the product is perceived

## Why The Positioning Statement Can't Help You

The traditional "Mad Libs" positioning statement:
- Assumes only one answer for each blank
- You simply "know" what it is
- **Dangerous:** Most products could be positioned in multiple market categories, with different competitors, providing different value for different customers

## The 5 Components of Positioning

These are the components of positioning (the blanks in the statement, used correctly):

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if your offering didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated Features/Capabilities** — What unique attributes do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value for Customers** — What is the value those capabilities enable for buyers?
4. **Target Customer Segmentation** — Who are the customers that care a lot about your value?
5. **Market Category** — The context where you position your product such that your unique value is obvious to target customers

**Great positioning = all five components defined in the best way possible for the offering.**

## What Comes After Positioning

All of these things require positioning as input:
- Storytelling
- Messaging
- Taglines
- Vision
- Branding

**"Positioning defines what market you intend to win and why you deserve to win it."**
