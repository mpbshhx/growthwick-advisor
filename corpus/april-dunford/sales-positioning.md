# Positioning Is Not A Marketing Exercise — April Dunford

## Why Positioning Has Been Misunderstood

Anyone who has read Ries and Trout's "Positioning — the Battle for Your Mind" would understandably think positioning is a marketing department exercise. The examples: marketing teams coming up with creative slogans, taglines, ad campaigns.

But the real world has dramatically changed.

- Madison Avenue no longer controls how customers perceive products
- Buyers now research products themselves — 81% do online research before purchase
- Buyers are adept at ignoring most ads and heavily skeptical of the few that break through
- Brands have been caught in blatant lies — trust in brand claims is at a low

## Today, Positioning Must Be Embodied Across the Entire Organization

Unlike the 70s, when you could get away with simply saying "We try harder" (Avis), brands today have to PROVE their positioning to a skeptical audience.

Positioning is now done through every interaction a prospect has with a company and its offerings.

## How Positioning Shows Up in Every Function

### Product
- If you say "easiest to use" — customers judge that the moment they use it
- If you claim "fastest" — they better feel it on login
- Key features, onboarding, roadmap, UX are all signals about: what you are, what you're good at, who you're for, what your value is
- **Product is often your best way to demonstrate positioning**

### Sales
- Goes far beyond message consistency
- If you're positioned around customer service — your sales reps better be courteous and calm
- The sales process itself can reflect positioning (how you handle returns, billing issues)
- Tone and demeanour of sales people: informal/fun signals focus on indie consultants; formal/stiff signals enterprise security

### Content
- Beyond saying "outperforms competition" — there's a new burden of proof
- "Your opinion, although interesting, is irrelevant"
- Claims must be backed by: data, 3rd-party validation, independent reviews, customer endorsements
- Days of prospects trusting your claims without proof are long gone

### Support
- Sometimes support IS your differentiation (Zappos)
- Support response times, escalation processes, key account coverage must reflect positioning
- A $20/month product shouldn't need phone support; mission-critical enterprise software's support must reflect that seriousness
- Pricing and support expectations are tightly linked

### Pricing
- People underestimate pricing's influence on positioning
- High prices = expectations of quality, service, performance
- Low prices = signal who it's appropriate for (small biz, individuals)
- Some startups have had to raise prices to be taken seriously by enterprise customers

### Channels and Partnerships
- The channels you sell through signal who your ideal customer is and who your competitors are
- Example: new fitness tracker that takes photos — placed in Best Buy's camera dept vs. fitness tracker dept changes perception entirely
- Partnerships signal complementary vs. competitive positioning (IBM partnership signals enterprise alignment)

## The Takeaway

David Packard (the P in HP) quote: "Marketing is too important to be left to the marketing department."

**Positioning is closely tied to overall business strategy — implementing it crosses every department, not just marketing.**

Positioning is not a tagline exercise. It's a strategic business function that must be lived across product, sales, content, support, pricing, and channels.
