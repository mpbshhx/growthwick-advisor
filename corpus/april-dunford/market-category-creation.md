## When to Create a New Category vs. Enter an Existing One

April Dunford is one of the most cited experts on market category strategy in B2B. Her core position: creating a new market category is a high-risk, high-reward bet that most companies shouldn't take — and many companies that attempt it do so for the wrong reasons.

### The Three Category Options

**Option 1: Position in an existing category**
- Easiest for buyers to understand ("it's like X but better at Y")
- You compete directly against established players
- Best when your product has clear, demonstrable superiority on dimensions the market already values
- Risk: commoditization pressure and price competition

**Option 2: Reframe an existing category (subcategory)**
- Create a subcategory where your differentiators become the definition of the segment
- Example: "CRM" → "Sales engagement platform for outbound SDR teams"
- Buyers understand the broader category; you educate them on why the subcategory matters
- Lower investment than full category creation; higher defensibility than generic category positioning

**Option 3: Create an entirely new category**
- Maximum upside: you define the category, set the terms of competition, and become the default comparison point
- Maximum risk: requires educating the entire market about a problem they may not know they have
- Best when the product does something genuinely unprecedented that no existing category captures

## The Conditions Required for Successful Category Creation

Dunford's documented position: you only attempt to create a new category when ALL of the following are true:

**Condition 1: No existing category fits**
If you can position your product as a subcategory of something that exists, do that instead. Category creation is expensive. The bar for attempting it is "no existing frame of reference works."

**Condition 2: The problem is real but unnamed**
Buyers are experiencing the problem — they just don't have a name for it or a category of solution to look for. If they don't recognize the problem, category creation fails.

**Condition 3: You have significant resources**
Creating a category requires sustained marketing investment in education, not just product marketing. You're not just explaining your product — you're explaining a new way of thinking about a problem. This takes years and significant spend.

**Condition 4: You can own and defend the category**
If you create a category and then a larger, better-resourced competitor defines it away from you, you've done the category creation work for them. You need enough product differentiation and brand speed to own the category before competition arrives.

## The Category Creation Process (Documented from "Obviously Awesome" and Public Talks)

### Phase 1: Name the Problem
Before you can create a category, you need to articulate the problem in terms that resonate with buyers.

A good category name:
- Captures the core problem buyers face
- Is intuitive enough to understand without explanation
- Is distinct enough to be ownable
- Doesn't pigeonhole the company into too narrow a niche

Bad category naming: too technical, too internally focused, or too similar to existing category names.

Example from Dunford's talks: Drift didn't create "AI chatbot software" (too technical, too narrow). They created "conversational marketing" — a name that captures the underlying shift in how businesses should engage buyers.

### Phase 2: Make the Problem Visible
Category creation requires making buyers see a problem they may not have been consciously aware of. Tactics:
- Research that quantifies the cost of the status quo ("companies lose X% of revenue because they can't...")
- Case studies that show what success looks like with the new approach
- Thought leadership content that reframes the problem in the buyer's mind

The goal: buyers start describing their problem using your category's language.

### Phase 3: Define the Category Requirements
A category has requirements — minimum standards a product must meet to qualify. By defining the requirements, the category creator subtly shapes the market in their favor.

Example: HubSpot defining "inbound marketing" created a category where the requirements (integrated content, CRM, email, and analytics) perfectly described HubSpot's product.

### Phase 4: Establish Your Leadership
Category kings don't just exist — they actively demonstrate and claim leadership:
- Industry conferences that define the category conversation
- Research reports that quantify the category
- Analyst briefings that position you as the category leader
- Awards programs for category practitioners
- A community of practitioners who identify with the category

## Why Category Creation Fails

From Dunford's talks and writing, common failure modes:

**1. Creating a category for a problem buyers don't care about**
"We're the leaders in AI-powered predictive behavioral micro-segmentation." If buyers don't recognize the problem you're solving, the category never takes hold.

**2. Creating a category you can't defend**
Salesforce created CRM category; Siebel was displaced. When Salesforce had better resources, they redefined the category. Small companies that create categories often lose them to better-funded followers.

**3. Confusing category creation with product naming**
Category creation is about defining a new way of thinking about a problem, not just inventing a new term for your product.

**4. Under-investing in category education**
Category creation requires years of sustained education investment. Companies that try to create a category with a 90-day content sprint fail.

**5. Creating the category alone**
The strongest categories are co-created with a coalition — analyst firms, influencers, early customers, and complementary vendors all speaking the same language.

## The Category Positioning Statement vs. Product Positioning Statement

Dunford distinguishes between two types of positioning:

**Product positioning:** "Our product is the best at X for customer segment Y"
**Category positioning:** "This type of product exists, solves this type of problem, and here's why every company like you needs it"

Category positioning must precede product positioning when creating a new category. You can't explain why your product is best before buyers understand the category.

## Key Quotes from April Dunford on Category Creation

- "Most companies try to create categories when they should be creating subcategories. The ROI on category creation is great if you win — the risk of losing is losing everything you invested in education."
- "Category creation is not a marketing tactic. It's a company strategy that takes years and requires the entire organization's commitment."
- "You know category creation is working when buyers start using your language without prompting."
- "The goal of category creation is to make alternatives look obviously inferior — not by attacking them, but by defining requirements they can't meet."
