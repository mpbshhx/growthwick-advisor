## Overview

April Dunford's "Only 5 Ways to Win" framework addresses a fundamental question in positioning: how do you claim a position that is both differentiated AND defensible? Her answer is that there are only five sustainable competitive strategies — every winning product uses one (or occasionally two) of them.

This framework is deeply rooted in Michael Porter's competitive strategy work, applied specifically to software and SaaS products, filtered through Dunford's 20+ years of repositioning companies.

---

## The 5 Ways to Win

### Way 1: Be the Best Product in an Existing Market
Win by having the objectively best product for a defined set of buyers in an existing category.

**What "best" means here:**
- Best performance on the features buyers already care about
- Best UX and reliability
- Strongest brand and social proof
- Best customer success and support ecosystem

**When this works:**
- Your product has genuine technical superiority that's hard to match
- Buyers have well-formed preferences and comparison criteria
- You can win head-to-head on the dimensions buyers already evaluate

**When this fails:**
- "Best" is subjective and you're comparing against something that's "good enough"
- Larger competitors can match features with resources
- You're in a winner-take-all market dominated by an entrenched leader

**Example pattern:** A new entrant in a mature category (project management, CRM, etc.) that builds objectively better UX and performance — and takes share from the legacy player through sheer product quality.

---

### Way 2: Niche Down — Be the Best for a Specific Segment
Win by being clearly and specifically built for a buyer segment that is underserved by generic alternatives.

**The mechanism:**
- General-purpose products make compromises to serve everyone
- A product built specifically for one segment can optimize without compromise
- Niche-specific products command premium pricing because they eliminate "doesn't quite fit" tax

**What "niche" means in practice:**
- Vertical specialization: "CRM for commercial real estate brokers"
- Workflow specialization: "Project management for creative agencies"
- Persona specialization: "Analytics for solo content creators"
- Scale specialization: "Enterprise contract management for Fortune 500 legal teams"

**When this works:**
- The segment is large enough to build a business in
- The segment's needs are different enough from the mainstream that a general product genuinely can't serve them well
- The segment has economic density (they pay well)

**When this fails:**
- The niche is too small
- The segment's needs aren't different enough to justify a specialized product
- General-purpose products add the specific features and eliminate the advantage

**Dunford's key insight on niching:** Most companies resist niching because it feels like leaving revenue on the table. In reality, focused niching wins more revenue because conversion rates are higher, churn is lower, and word-of-mouth within the segment is stronger.

---

### Way 3: Create a New Market Subcategory
Win by creating a subcategory where your specific combination of capabilities becomes the definition of a new solution type — and where alternatives are disqualified because they don't meet the new subcategory's requirements.

**The subcategory mechanism:**
- "Sales intelligence platform" is generic and competitive
- "Sales intelligence platform for outbound SDR teams using LinkedIn-first prospecting" is a subcategory where your specific strengths define the category
- Competitors can't easily enter the subcategory without your specific features

**How subcategories are created:**
1. Identify the 2-3 capabilities that make your product genuinely different
2. Find the customer segment where those capabilities matter most
3. Name the subcategory in a way that emphasizes those capabilities as requirements
4. Market the subcategory, not just your product — make buyers want "the subcategory" and then show that you're the only real option

**When this works:**
- You have genuine differentiated capabilities (not just feature parity)
- A specific segment values those capabilities above all else
- You can educate that segment on why the subcategory matters

---

### Way 4: Create a New Market Category
Win by defining an entirely new category and becoming the reference point that all future entrants are compared against.

*(See the full market-category-creation.md for detailed treatment of this way to win.)*

**The short version:**
- Highest risk, highest reward
- Requires the problem to be real but unnamed
- Requires significant marketing investment in education
- Category kings capture disproportionate market share — but getting there is expensive

**The payoff:** Salesforce didn't just win CRM — they defined it. Buyers who want CRM think of Salesforce first. That's a structural competitive advantage that compounds for decades.

---

### Way 5: Change the Value Metric — Price/Package Differently
Win not through a better product but through a better pricing and packaging model that removes friction for buyers.

**The mechanism:**
- Sometimes the market is priced in a way that excludes buyers who would use and value the product
- A different value metric (usage-based vs. seat-based; monthly vs. annual; free vs. paid) unlocks a new buyer segment
- This is how freemium disrupted enterprise software: the product wasn't better, but the buying model eliminated procurement friction

**Examples:**
- AWS vs. traditional hosting: same capability, but pay-per-use vs. annual commitment changed who could buy
- Dropbox vs. enterprise file storage: same capability, but freemium consumer entry vs. IT-led enterprise deployment
- Notion vs. Confluence: similar capability, but free for individuals + low-friction team pricing vs. per-seat enterprise

**When this works:**
- There's a large underserved market that wants your capability but can't access it due to pricing
- You can be economically viable at the new price point
- The new pricing model doesn't cannibalize your existing revenue

**Dunford's caution:** Competing on price alone is not a strategy — it's a race to the bottom. Changing the value metric must unlock a genuinely new buyer segment or a new use case, not just take existing business at lower margin.

## Choosing Your Way to Win

Dunford's process for selecting the right competitive strategy:

**Step 1: Inventory your differentiators**
What do you have that competitors don't? Features, data, integrations, expertise, pricing model, team?

**Step 2: Map differentiators to customer segments**
Which customer segments care most about each differentiator? Where do your differentiators create the highest relative value?

**Step 3: Evaluate each way to win**
For your best-fit customer segment and differentiators, which of the 5 ways to win is most achievable and most defensible?

**Step 4: Test with customers**
Present your chosen positioning to 5-10 target customers. Does the "way to win" resonate? Do they understand why you're the obvious choice?

**Step 5: Align the whole company**
The winning strategy must be embodied in product development (build what reinforces the position), pricing (match the value metric to the strategy), sales (message that matches the positioning), and marketing (campaigns that establish the position).

## The Positioning Trap: Trying to Win Multiple Ways

The most common positioning mistake: trying to be everything to everyone.

"We're the best product (Way 1) for enterprise teams (Way 2) in a new category we're creating (Way 4) with flexible usage-based pricing (Way 5)."

This isn't a strategy — it's a list of aspirations. Each "way to win" requires different product, marketing, and sales approaches. Trying to execute multiple ways simultaneously produces mediocre execution of all of them.

**Dunford's rule:** Pick one primary way to win. Be ruthlessly consistent. Add a secondary way only if it genuinely reinforces the first.

## Key Quotes

- "There are only five ways to win in a market. If you're not sure which one you're using, you're probably losing."
- "Trying to win all five ways simultaneously means you're winning none of them."
- "The best positioning makes the sale obvious — customers should think 'I'd be an idiot not to buy this.'"
- "Niche positioning feels like it limits opportunity. In practice, it creates more opportunity by making you the obvious choice for someone."
