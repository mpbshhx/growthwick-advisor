# Positioning and Competition: How to Think About Competitive Analysis

Source: https://www.aprildunford.com/post/positioning-and-competition

## The Most Common Positioning Mistake

The most common positioning mistake startups make is how they define "competition" — specifically, who they are positioning against.

We default to thinking about competitors as other solutions that look like ours. If we're a purpose-built software product, we see our competition as other purpose-built software solving more or less the same customer problems.

This view is often either too narrow or too broad — and it seriously weakens positioning.

---

## Positioning Against Competitive Alternatives (Not Just "Competitors")

When working on positioning, think about "Competitive Alternatives" rather than "competitors."

To win business, the first competitive alternative you need to beat is **the status quo**. Customers had the problem your product was designed to solve before you showed up — how were they solving it?

Often they're using:
- Spreadsheets and documents
- Manual processes
- Limited functionality in existing "monolith" platforms (CRM, ERP, Accounting)

These status quo solutions are chosen because they are easy to get started with, cheap or free, and frequently do the job "good enough."

---

## Status Quo Wins One Deal in Four

A better way to think about competitive alternatives: **"What would a customer do if your offering didn't exist?"**

Sometimes the answer is "Do nothing." That means the customer would stick with their current way of solving the problem — spreadsheet, manual process, or hiring an intern.

In enterprise software, companies typically lose **20-30% of deals to "no decision."**

You need to position yourself as clearly superior to the status quo if you want to convince customers to act.

---

## Not Every Competitor is a Competitive Alternative

Beyond the status quo, consider any competitors that your prospects actually look at when actively hunting for a new solution.

**The mistake:** Considering every solution on the market that *could possibly* compete as an "alternative."

**The reality:** Many of these solutions are not on the radar of your customers. They may target a very different customer type, or be too small/niche to be known outside their specific segment.

**Phantom competitors** should NOT be considered when working on positioning. You're diluting your positioning by trying to position against them.

Rules:
- Positioning shouldn't involve an internet research exercise
- Just because a company *could* compete doesn't mean they ever *will* in your deals
- The product team might monitor them as a future threat — but don't let them contaminate your positioning
- When you start seeing a competitor in actual deals, *then* adjust

**Your goal:** Strongly and clearly present your solution as a clear winner versus the other solutions actually on your prospect's shortlist.

---

## The Right Framework for Competitive Analysis

1. **Identify actual competitive alternatives** — not all competitors, only those prospects actually consider
2. **Include the status quo** as your first competitive alternative to beat
3. **Ask: what would customers do if we didn't exist?** — that answer reveals your real competition
4. **Map what you have that alternatives do not** — this is your differentiation starting point
5. **Ignore phantom competitors** — position only against what your customers actually evaluate

---

## Why This Matters for Positioning

Considering your product's competitive alternatives is the **starting point for understanding what differentiates your product in the market.**

The more deeply you understand:
- Your prospects' status quo
- Which other solutions land on a shortlist with you

...the stronger your positioning will be.

Competitive analysis for positioning is not about tracking all competitors. It's about deeply understanding the actual alternatives your buyers weigh — and making your value obvious by comparison.
