## Why Positioning Gets Harder as You Grow

Early-stage companies often stumble into their initial positioning — they found a segment that wanted their product and ran with it. Growth-stage companies face a different challenge: the positioning that got you here may not be the positioning that gets you to the next level.

As companies scale, they encounter:
- A broader range of customers, not all equally profitable or happy
- Competitive pressure from better-funded players
- Sales teams losing deals for unclear reasons
- Marketing messages that resonate with some prospects but not others

The fix is almost always the same: **sharpen the positioning**.

## The 5 Components Review for Growth-Stage

The same 5-component framework applies, but with growth-stage nuance:

### 1. Competitive Alternatives (Revisit These)
At early stage, you often knew exactly who you competed against. At growth stage:
- New competitors may have entered
- Your product may have expanded into adjacent use cases
- The "status quo" may have shifted

**Action:** Re-survey customers and recent wins/losses. Who was actually on the shortlist?

### 2. Differentiated Features (Prune and Focus)
Early-stage companies often win on one or two key differentiators. Growth-stage companies add features — but not all additions are differentiators.

**Action:** Return to first principles. Which capabilities do you have that the alternatives your target customers actually evaluate do NOT have? Ignore features you added for retention or edge cases.

### 3. Value for Customers (Quantify It)
Growth-stage companies can now quantify value claims with real customer data.

**Action:** Mine customer success stories for specific, measurable outcomes. "Saves time" → "Sales teams close deals 40% faster." Specificity is credibility.

### 4. Target Customer Segmentation (Narrow to Win)
The most common growth-stage mistake: trying to be everything to everyone.

**The fix:** Focus on the customers that care the most about your differentiated value.

Steps to find them:
1. Get a grip on your key differentiators
2. Understand the value those differentiators deliver
3. Identify which customers care MOST about that value
4. Ask: what makes them different from other customers?
   - Company size? Vertical? Geography? Tech stack? Behavior?
5. Narrow to the segment where you can win consistently

The smaller and tighter the initial segmentation, the easier it is to get traction. You can always expand later.

### 5. Market Category (Reassess as Market Evolves)
Markets change. Categories that didn't exist when you started may now be mainstream. Dominant players may have changed.

**Action:** Does your current market category still make your differentiated value obvious to your ideal customers? If not, it's time to reassess.

## How To Select a Target Market (Growth Stage)

### The Core Question
"What are the characteristics of prospects that love my unique stuff the most?"

### Six-Step Segmentation Process

**Step 1: Get clear on your key differentiators**
What can you do that no competitor can do?

**Step 2: Understand the value those differentiators deliver**
Features → "So what?" → measurable outcomes for customers

**Step 3: Identify prospects that care the most about your value**
Not "yeah, cool" but "I need that RIGHT NOW"

**Step 4: Map the characteristics of high-affinity prospects**
- Company size range?
- Vertical or industry?
- Geography?
- Existing tech stack?
- Specific buyer role?
- Behaviors or triggers that indicate they have the pain?

**Step 5: Verify segment size**
Can you realistically meet sales goals with just this segment?
Is it tight enough that awareness can actually build via word of mouth?

**Step 6: Verify ability to sell**
Can prospects afford your price? Do they have budget authority? Do they buy through channels you can access?

## The "Magical Weird Customer" Principle

For startups and growth-stage companies alike: you're going to close a certain kind of customer.

Not all SMBs. Not all enterprises.

A *special snowflake* sort of customer that:
- Gets what you do
- Loves what you do
- Will pay money for what you do
- Is willing to overlook your weaknesses because your key differentiators solve a critical problem

What makes them different from every other prospect? That answer is the key to your segmentation.

## Positioning Red Flags at Growth Stage

Signs your positioning needs work:
- Sales cycle getting longer without obvious cause
- Increased discounting to close deals
- High churn in a specific customer segment
- Marketing generating lots of leads but poor conversion to qualified pipeline
- Sales team frequently losing to "no decision"
- Different team members describing the product differently in pitches

Any of these → go back to competitive alternatives and work through the 5 components again.

## The Customer-Centric Positioning Methodology

Start with competitive alternatives (not features, not category). This is the non-negotiable starting point.

Flow:
1. **Competitive Alternatives** → What are they actually evaluating?
2. **Differentiated Capabilities** → What do you have that they don't?
3. **Value** → So what for customers?
4. **Best-Fit Customers** → Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market Category** → What context makes that value obvious?

This isn't a one-time exercise. Growth-stage companies should revisit positioning at each major inflection point: new competitor entry, expansion into a new segment, or product pivot.
