# Positioning and Competition — April Dunford

## The Most Common Positioning Mistake

The most common positioning mistake startups make is how they define "competition" or who they are positioning against.

We typically think about competitors as other solutions that look like ours. But this view of competition is often either too narrow or too broad — and can seriously weaken positioning.

## Competitive Alternatives vs. Competitors

When working on positioning, think "Competitive Alternatives" rather than "competitors."

**To win business, the first competitive alternative to beat is the status quo.**

Customers had the problem your product was designed to solve before you showed up — how were they solving it? Often:
- Spreadsheets and documents
- Manual processes
- Limited functionality of existing "monolith" platforms (CRM, ERP, Accounting)

Status quo solutions are chosen because they are:
- Easy to get started with
- Cheap or free
- Frequently "good enough in a pinch"

## Status Quo Wins One Deal in Four

"What would a customer do if your offering didn't exist?" — Sometimes: "Do nothing." Which means: stick with current way of solving the problem.

**In enterprise software, we typically lose between 20-30% of deals to "no decision."**

You need to position yourself as clearly superior to the status quo if you want to convince customers to act.

## Not Every Competitor is an Alternative

Beyond status quo, consider competitors that prospects actually look at when hunting for a new solution.

**The phantom competitor mistake:** Considering every solution on the market that could possibly be competitive as an "alternative." The reality: many of these solutions aren't on your customers' radar.

Reasons a competitor may not be relevant:
- Targets a very different type of customer
- Small, not known outside their specific niche
- Simply not considered by your buyer

**Phantom competitors** dilute your positioning. Positioning isn't a test of internet research skills. Just because a company *could* compete doesn't mean they ever *will*.

Product team can watch phantom competitors as future threats. When they start appearing in actual deals — then adjust positioning. Until then, ignore them.

## The Principle

Your goal: strongly and clearly present your solution as a clear winner for prospects versus the other solutions on their actual shortlist.

Considering your product's competitive alternatives is:
- A key component of positioning
- The **starting point** for understanding what differentiates your product in the market

The more deeply you understand:
1. Your prospects' status quo
2. Which other solutions land on a shortlist with you

...the stronger your positioning will be.
