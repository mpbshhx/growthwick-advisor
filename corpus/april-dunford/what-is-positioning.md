# A Quickstart Guide to Positioning — April Dunford

## Origin Story: Microsoft Access Killer

First job out of engineering school: product marketer at a startup with a "Microsoft Access killer" supporting SQL. After massive marketing effort, barely sold 200 copies. Called buyers to check in before killing product.

- 20 calls in: nobody remembered buying it
- Call 21: "Your product made me the hero of the sales team!" — company used it on laptops for sales reps to take orders in the field, sync with SQL database at HQ. "We're doubling sales!"
- Call 41: Field service agents updating system in the field, sync with headquarters. "We've increased service capacity by 60%!"
- Final tally: 94/100 didn't even know they had it. 6 had transformed their business with it.

**The repositioning:** "Microsoft Access-killer" → "Embeddable Database for Mobile Devices." Product took off. Massive growth. Acquired by big database company. Product generated hundreds of millions in revenue. Still runs on mobile devices today.

## What Positioning IS NOT

- Not messaging
- Not a tagline
- Not your brand story
- Not your vision or mission
- Not "everything you marketers cook up over there" (per one CEO)

## Defining Positioning

**"Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about."**

## Positioning as Context Setting

Positioning = the opening scene of a movie. Before you can follow details, you need the big questions answered: Where? When? What's happening? Who are these people?

**Apocalypse Now example:** 4 minutes 45 seconds in, you know: Vietnam war, Martin Sheen's character has PTSD, this will be intense. Good context = you can follow the story.

**CRM example:** Tell someone it's a "CRM" and they immediately assume: Salesforce is competition, sells to head of sales, tracks deals/accounts, costs less than Salesforce. Good positioning = true assumptions are triggered. Bad positioning = incorrect assumptions triggered, leaving sales/marketing to undo the damage.

## Why The Positioning Statement Can't Help You

The traditional Mad Libs-style positioning statement assumes there is only one answer for each blank. Dangerous. Most products could be positioned in multiple market categories, with different competitors, different value, different customers.

## The 5 Components of Positioning

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if your solution didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated Features/Capabilities** — What do you have that alternatives do not?
3. **Value for Customers** — So what? What value do those capabilities enable?
4. **Target Customer Segmentation** — Who are the customers that care a lot about that value?
5. **Market Category** — The context where your unique value is obvious to target customers

### Key Insight: Each Component Depends on the Others
- Unique value depends on differentiated features
- Features are only "differentiated" compared to competitive alternatives
- Best-fit customers are those who care most about your unique value
- Best market category = context where your value is obvious to target customers

**The flow (must start with competitive alternatives):**
Competitive Alternatives → Differentiated Features → Value for Customers → Target Customers → Market Category

## Customer-Centric Methodology (Clayton Christensen / Jobs to Be Done Influence)

Start with competitive alternatives: "What would customers do if our solution didn't exist?"

Then: "What do we have that alternatives do not?" → differentiated features
Then: "So what for customers?" → value those capabilities enable
Then: Who cares a lot about this value? → target customers
Then: What market context makes our value obvious? → market category

## Real Example: Janna Systems (Enterprise CRM)

**Problem:** Positioned as Enterprise CRM competing with Siebel (8,000 employees, $2B revenue, 400 customers). Janna: couple dozen employees, <$2M revenue, 6 customers.

**Differentiator:** Could model relationships in a different way (unique feature, but poorly articulated — "Anything you want!")

**Breakthrough:** Landed investment bank. Discovered the value: insight into interpersonal relationships for sales teams starting new conversations and understanding influence over deals.

**For companies relying on personal relationships (investment banking, private client services) — game-changer.**

**Repositioned:** "CRM for Investment Banks" → grew from <$2M to ~$80M in 18 months → acquired by Siebel for $1.3B.

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### Trap 1: Defining competitive alternatives too broadly
Think: "What would a customer do if your offering didn't exist?" — not "What else is on the market?"

- Often the answer is "status quo" (spreadsheets, manual process, hiring someone)
- Enterprise software: 20-25% of deals lost to "no decision" — must position against status quo
- **Phantom competitors:** Companies that theoretically could compete but never actually appear in deals. Don't position against them — you'll dilute your positioning.

### Trap 2: Assuming you must create a new market category
- **Existing category:** Leverages what customers already know. "Embeddable database for mobile devices" — customers understand "database," qualifiers narrow the niche.
- **New category:** Must first make the category mean something. Expensive. Time-consuming.
- **Historical fact:** 90% of tech companies that went public in the past 5 years were positioned in existing markets, not new categories.
- Salesforce: niche play in CRM until hundreds of millions in revenue. Qualtrics: survey software market until hundreds of millions. Category creation came AFTER market foothold.

## Positioning = The Most Powerful Strategic Tool You Have

Positioning defines what market you intend to win and why you deserve to win it.

Stories, messaging, taglines, vision, branding — all require positioning as input. Do positioning first.
