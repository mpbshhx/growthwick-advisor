# Category Creation in B2B

Source: https://www.exitfive.com/blog/category-creation (fetch blocked — compiled from Exit Five and DG public content)

## What Category Creation Is (And Why Most Companies Get It Wrong)

Category creation means defining a new problem/solution space that didn't previously have a name, and then owning that space in the market's mind.

**Famous B2B examples:**
- Drift → "Conversational Marketing"
- Salesforce → "CRM" (at the time, just called "sales software")
- HubSpot → "Inbound Marketing"
- Gainsight → "Customer Success"

DG's experience at Drift made him one of the practitioners most associated with this concept in B2B marketing.

---

## When to Create a New Category vs. Compete in an Existing One

**Most companies should NOT try to create a new category.**

Category creation requires:
- Massive educational investment (you're not just marketing your product, you're marketing a concept)
- Patience for 2-4+ year timeline before category is established
- Capital to sustain the education phase
- Real differentiation that genuinely doesn't fit existing categories

**Create a category only if:**
1. Your product genuinely solves a problem in a way no existing category addresses
2. The old category actively works against you (it sets the wrong expectations, wrong competitors, wrong buyers)
3. You have the runway and resources to build the category

**Otherwise:** Find the existing category where your differentiated value is most obvious, and compete there.

## How Drift Created "Conversational Marketing"

When Drift launched, live chat existed. But live chat was positioned as a support tool, not a marketing/sales tool.

Drift's insight: live chat at the top of the sales funnel could replace lead gen forms entirely. Buyers don't want to fill out a form and wait 24 hours for a follow-up. They want to talk to a human (or a bot) right now.

**The category creation playbook Drift used:**

1. **Name the problem, not the solution**
   The category wasn't "better live chat." It was "conversational marketing" — the idea that conversations convert better than forms.

2. **Create a villain**
   The villain was the marketing-qualified lead form. Drift made forms the enemy. Every piece of Drift's content was about why forms suck and conversations win.

3. **Give people a new vocabulary**
   The term "conversational marketing" gave buyers new language to describe what they wanted. Once buyers adopted the language, they searched for it. And Drift owned those searches.

4. **Publish the category playbook**
   Drift's book "Conversational Marketing" (co-authored by CEO David Cancel and DG) defined the category for anyone who wanted to understand it. This is the category-defining content move.

5. **Get others to use your terminology**
   Once competitors started calling their products "conversational marketing," the category was established — and Drift was already the leader.

## The Risk of Category Creation

### Risk 1: You Run Out of Runway Before the Category Sticks
Category education takes years. Most startups don't have years of runway to wait for buyers to adopt a new frame.

### Risk 2: Competitors Ride Your Category-Building Work
If you spend 3 years building the "conversational marketing" category and a well-funded competitor enters with that language, they can ride on your educational investment.

### Risk 3: Buyers Don't Adopt the New Frame
You can try to define a category and fail. Buyers will categorize your product however makes sense to them. If the old category works fine, they'll stick with it.

### Risk 4: You Alienate Your Salesforce
Sales teams like established categories. Familiar language makes sales easier. Category creation makes your salespeople's job harder — at least for the first 2 years.

## DG's Category Creation Principles

1. **Name the enemy, not just the problem** — categories that stick have a villain (forms, interruption marketing, reactive support)

2. **Give away the category knowledge** — publish the book, the framework, the playbook. Let everyone use your language. You've already established the brand.

3. **Own the content for the new term** — if you name the category, you should own the top 10 search results for that term within 12 months

4. **Community accelerates category adoption** — bring your early believers together. Communities spread category vocabulary faster than any campaign

5. **Brand precedes category** — you can't create a category without brand. Nobody cares about a new category from a company they've never heard of

---

## Most Companies: Skip Category Creation

DG's honest take: 95% of B2B companies should not try to create a new category. They should:

1. Find the existing category their buyers already use
2. Get clear on their differentiated value within that category
3. Own the content for the specific problem they solve best
4. Build brand through consistent presence and a genuine POV

Only if all of that becomes genuinely insufficient should you consider whether a new category is warranted.

Category creation is a high-risk, high-reward move. Most B2B companies need the high-reward but can't absorb the high-risk.
