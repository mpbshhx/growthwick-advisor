# Brand Marketing in B2B

Source: https://www.exitfive.com/blog/brand-marketing (fetch blocked — compiled from Exit Five and DG public content)

## The B2B Brand Problem

Most B2B marketers have been trained to prove ROI on everything. That means brand — which is long-cycle, hard-to-attribute, and takes years to compound — gets deprioritized in favor of campaigns that produce MQLs this quarter.

The result: a generation of B2B companies with interchangeable positioning, identical messaging, and no reason to be remembered.

---

## What Brand Actually Is in B2B

Brand = the sum of associations buyers have when they think about your company.

It includes:
- What problem you're known for solving
- Who you serve (and who you don't)
- How you make people feel
- The trust you've earned over time
- Your point of view on the market

**Brand is not:**
- Your logo
- Your color palette
- Your tagline
- Your "about us" page

Brand is built through repeated exposure and consistent experience — not declared in a rebrand.

---

## Why Brand Matters More in B2B Than Most People Think

### 1. The Buying Committee Problem
B2B purchases involve multiple stakeholders. When 6 people meet to decide on a vendor, the one they've heard of wins, all else being equal. Brand creates the home field advantage.

### 2. The Long Sales Cycle Problem
B2B sales cycles run 3-18 months. Over that period, the buyer interacts with dozens of content pieces, sales touchpoints, and peer conversations. Brand is the thread that makes all of that coherent and memorable.

### 3. The CAC Problem
Paid acquisition is increasingly expensive and increasingly noisy. Companies with strong brands have lower effective CAC because some buyers come to them already pre-sold — no ads required.

### 4. The Pricing Power Problem
Commoditized products compete on price. Branded products compete on trust. You cannot charge a premium if buyers see you as interchangeable with the competition.

## How to Build a B2B Brand (DG's Approach)

### Step 1: Get Clear on Your Point of View
A brand without a POV is just a logo.

Your POV is your answer to: **What do you believe about your market that others don't?**

Examples:
- Drift believed that gated forms were friction that destroyed buyer experience
- HubSpot believed that interruption marketing was dying and inbound was the future
- Basecamp believed that the startup hustle culture was toxic

Your POV should be polarizing enough that some people disagree. If everyone agrees with your POV, it's not differentiated enough to build a brand around.

### Step 2: Pick Your Primary Channel and Own It
Brand is built through repetition. You can't build a brand being present everywhere occasionally.

For B2B in 2024: **LinkedIn is the highest-leverage brand-building channel.**

Why:
- Your ICP is already there
- Professional mindset = receptive to business content
- Algorithmic reach still exists (unlike other platforms)
- Builds both company and personal brand simultaneously

### Step 3: Create Content With a Consistent Voice
Not content that sounds like every other B2B company. Content that sounds like you.

Elements of a distinctive brand voice:
- Specific word choices you always use (and always avoid)
- Consistent formatting and structure
- A recognizable level of formality (or deliberate informality)
- A repeated set of beliefs and themes
- Stories and examples that recur across content

### Step 4: Be Consistent Over Years, Not Weeks
Brand compounds like interest. The first 6 months build almost nothing visible. Months 12-18 start to show real results. Year 2-3 is when it becomes a genuine competitive moat.

Most companies quit in month 4. That's why most B2B brands don't exist.

## The Founder Brand in B2B

In early-stage B2B, the founder's personal brand is the company's best marketing asset.

**Why it works:**
- People trust people more than companies
- A founder's authentic voice is more credible than polished corporate messaging
- Followers of a founder become early customers, evangelists, and amplifiers
- Investor relationships are warmer when the founder has a recognized presence

**The Dave Gerhardt proof case:**
DG built his personal brand at Drift, writing and sharing publicly about what he was doing and learning. His audience followed him to Privy. His audience followed him to Exit Five. The audience became the asset — it transferred between companies and eventually became the business itself.

## What Brand Looks Like in Practice at Different Stages

### Seed Stage (1-20 employees)
- Founder brand = company brand
- 3-5 content pieces/week from the founder on LinkedIn
- Clear POV in every piece of content
- Small but highly engaged email list

### Series A/B (50-200 employees)
- Hire a marketing leader who prioritizes brand alongside demand gen
- Begin building company channels separate from founder
- Podcast, newsletter, community as owned distribution
- Consistent content calendar with brand voice guidelines

### Growth Stage (200+ employees)
- Dedicated brand team or brand function
- Brand measurement (aided/unaided awareness, NPS, share of voice)
- Brand campaigns alongside performance marketing
- Community as a brand moat

## Measuring Brand

The hard problem: most brand investment doesn't show up in last-click attribution.

**Proxy metrics to track:**
- Branded search volume (growing = people are looking for you by name)
- Organic/direct traffic growth
- Net Promoter Score trend
- Share of voice in category conversations
- Email list growth rate
- % of pipeline from "heard of us" vs. paid channels

**The survey method (DG's recommended approach):**
Ask every closed-won customer: "How did you first hear about us? What were you doing when you decided to reach out?"

The patterns in those answers tell you where your brand is actually working — regardless of what your attribution model says.
