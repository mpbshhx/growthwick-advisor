# Tribes: Building a Minimum Viable Audience

**Source:** Seth Godin, *Tribes: We Need You to Lead Us* (2008); seths.blog  
**Primary URL:** https://seths.blog  
**Corpus role:** Community and leadership philosophy — how small groups of true believers beat mass audiences every time

---

## What Is a Tribe?

A tribe is a group of people connected to one another, connected to a leader, and connected to an idea.

That's it. Three connections. All three required.

For millions of years, humans have been part of tribes. The tribe provided safety, meaning, and identity. We evolved to seek belonging, to look for leaders, to organize around shared ideas.

In marketing terms, this is extraordinarily good news for anyone who isn't Coca-Cola. You don't need to reach everyone. You need to find — or create — your tribe.

Godin wrote *Tribes* as a manifesto for the person who wants to lead a movement rather than run an ad campaign. The insight: **the internet has made it easier than ever to connect people with each other and with ideas. The bottleneck isn't technology. It's leaders willing to step up.**

---

## The Minimum Viable Audience

The most important strategic concept for small and mid-sized service businesses: **you don't need a big audience. You need the right audience.**

Godin's framework suggests that a small, cohesive tribe — people who share values, worldview, and goals — is worth far more than a large, passive audience.

The math for a solo coach or small firm:
- 1,000 true believers can sustain a thriving practice indefinitely
- 100,000 passive followers with no real connection produce nothing
- A tribe of 200 who genuinely trust you and refer others can fill your calendar for years

The "minimum viable audience" concept (developed further in *This Is Marketing*) flows directly from the tribes insight: **find the smallest group of people for whom your work is essential. Serve them brilliantly. Let them tell everyone else.**

This inverts the traditional funnel logic. Instead of starting with a big audience and converting a small fraction, you start with a small dedicated tribe and grow from there.

## Leadership Is the Product

The central provocation of *Tribes*: the internet doesn't need more products. It needs more leaders.

Anyone can be the leader of a tribe. You don't need permission, capital, credentials, or scale. You need:

1. **A shared idea worth rallying around**
2. **The courage to stand up and say "follow me"**
3. **The commitment to keep showing up**

For coaches and consultants, this reframes the entire positioning challenge. You're not selling a service. You're leading a movement. The movement is the philosophy behind your work — the worldview you hold about what creates success in your domain.

A business coach who leads a tribe doesn't just help individuals improve. She's saying something like: "The old model of grinding yourself into exhaustion is wrong. Sustainable high performance comes from a radically different approach to how you work. If you believe that too, follow me."

That's a tribe. That's leadership. That's far more compelling than "I offer executive coaching services."

## The Three Things Tribes Need from Leaders

Godin is clear that leadership in this context has a specific shape:

**1. A narrative** — a story about the world that your tribe can see themselves in. Not your story. Their story. What does the world look like from their perspective? What are they fighting against? What do they believe that most people don't?

**2. A way to connect** — tribes bond through shared identity, shared language, and shared experiences. Leaders create these. A newsletter creates a shared ritual. A community creates shared connection. A signature framework gives the tribe a shared language.

**3. A tension between the status quo and a better future** — the most powerful tribes are organized around a genuine disagreement with how things are. "We believe X, and most of the world does Y, and that's why Y doesn't work." That tension creates energy, urgency, and identity.

## The Problem with Mass Marketing (for Tribes)

Mass marketing tries to appeal to everyone. Tribes require that you appeal to someone specifically — and implicitly exclude others.

This is psychologically uncomfortable for most business owners. "What if I alienate potential clients?" is the fear.

But the tribe dynamic is clear: belonging requires contrast. A tribe that anyone can join is not a tribe — it's a demographic. The tribe of "people who want business success" has no identity, no culture, no bonding mechanism.

The tribe of "former corporate executives who left six-figure jobs to build businesses that matter and refuse to do it the old way" — that's a tribe. You know immediately if you're in it or not.

The exclusion isn't mean. It's clarifying. It tells the right people they've found home. It tells the wrong people to look elsewhere. Both outcomes are good.

## Heretics and Movements

*Tribes* is partly a love letter to heretics — people who see a better way and are willing to say so, even when it's uncomfortable.

Godin's argument: the world doesn't need more managers who maintain the status quo. It needs heretics who challenge it.

For a service business, the heretic move is taking a public position on something your industry takes for granted. Examples:

- "Most business coaches focus on tactics. I think that's the wrong starting point."
- "The advice to 'niche down' is right, but everyone niches down to demographics. You should niche down to a worldview."
- "Accountability programs don't work for most people. Here's what actually does."

A position like this will alienate some people and magnetize others. The people it magnetizes are your tribe.

## Practical Tribe-Building for Coaches

Building a tribe as a coaching or consulting practice means:

**Step 1: Choose a belief.** What do you believe about your domain that is true, important, and underappreciated? This becomes your philosophical north star.

**Step 2: Name the enemy.** Not a person — a bad idea, a failing system, a wrong approach that the market takes for granted. Your tribe bonds partly through shared opposition to this.

**Step 3: Create consistent rituals.** Regular touchpoints — weekly newsletter, monthly call, annual event, daily post — that give tribe members a reason to stay engaged and something to share.

**Step 4: Give them language.** Signature frameworks, memorable phrases, and specific vocabulary that tribe members can use to recognize each other and communicate the ideas to others.

**Step 5: Celebrate the members.** The leader's job isn't self-promotion. It's elevating the tribe members. When members succeed, the leader amplifies it. When members struggle, the leader supports. The tribe is the product.

## Trust Is the Currency

From Seth's blog, June 2026:

> "Trust isn't something a brand builds with an ad campaign. It's what's left if the marketers don't ruin it."

This is the deeper truth about tribes: trust is the medium through which tribe leadership works. The tribe follows the leader because they trust the leader's worldview, judgment, and commitment.

Trust is fragile and non-transferable. It can't be bought with advertising. It can only be built through consistent, reliable, genuine engagement over time. The coach with 500 people who deeply trust them has an unassailable competitive position. The one with 50,000 followers who barely remember who they are has nothing.
