# Permission Marketing: Earning the Right to Communicate

**Source:** Seth Godin, *Permission Marketing* (1999); seths.blog  
**Primary URL:** https://seths.blog/2008/01/permission-mark/  
**Corpus role:** Foundational philosophy — the ethical and strategic case for building an audience that wants to hear from you

---

## The Core Idea

Seth Godin's definition, pulled directly from his blog:

> "Permission marketing is the privilege (not the right) of delivering anticipated, personal and relevant messages to people who actually want to get them."

Three words matter more than anything else in that sentence: **anticipated, personal, and relevant.**

- **Anticipated** — the person expects to hear from you. They signed up, opted in, said yes.
- **Personal** — the message speaks to them specifically, not to a mass audience.
- **Relevant** — it connects to something they actually care about.

If your marketing fails any one of these three tests, it's interruption marketing — and you're paying for attention that isn't really yours.

---

## Interruption Marketing Is Dying

The old model: buy enough eyeballs, shout loud enough, repeat often enough, and some percentage of people will eventually buy. TV ads. Cold calls. Banner ads. Spam.

The problem isn't that interruption marketing doesn't work at all — it's that it's getting more expensive and less effective every year. The average person sees thousands of marketing messages per day. The brain has evolved to filter them out. Attention is the scarcest resource in a modern economy.

Godin wrote *Permission Marketing* in 1999, when this shift was just beginning. In 2026, it's a completed revolution. Cold outreach open rates are sub-5%. Organic reach on social platforms requires paying to play. Ad costs have inflated while conversion rates have dropped.

The coaches, consultants, and service businesses that are thriving aren't the ones running louder interruption campaigns. They're the ones who figured out how to build a permission asset — an audience that gave them the right to communicate.

---

## What "Real Permission" Actually Means

Godin makes a critical distinction: **real permission vs. presumed or legalistic permission.**

> "Just because you somehow get my email address doesn't mean you have permission. Just because I don't complain doesn't mean you have permission. Just because it's in the fine print of your privacy policy doesn't mean it's permission either."

Real permission has a simple test: **If you stop showing up, people complain. They ask where you went.**

That's a radically different bar than "they haven't unsubscribed yet." Most marketing lists are filled with people who simply haven't gotten around to opting out. That's not a permission asset — that's a list of people who are tolerating you.

A real permission asset: people who would notice your absence.

---

## Permission Is Like Dating

Godin uses the dating analogy deliberately. You don't ask someone to marry you on the first date. You earn the right — over time, through small consistent actions — to be trusted with more.

In marketing terms: you offer something valuable upfront (a useful email, a free resource, a genuinely helpful piece of content). In exchange, the person gives you their attention and contact information. You honor the implicit contract. You deliver what you promised. You don't escalate faster than the relationship warrants.

Permission is built in stages:
1. **First touch** — they encounter you and something resonates
2. **Opt-in** — they choose to hear more
3. **Consistent value delivery** — you keep the promise you made
4. **Trust deepens** — they open your emails, engage with your content, refer others
5. **Permission to sell** — they're now genuinely interested in what you offer

Most businesses try to skip from step 1 to step 5. That's why their conversion rates are terrible.

## The Economics of Permission

Once you have permission, the economics change dramatically. Godin's insight from 2008 is even more true today:

> "One of the key drivers of permission marketing, in addition to the scarcity of attention, is the extraordinarily low cost of dripping to people who want to hear from you. RSS and email and other techniques mean you don't have to worry about stamps or network ad buys every time you have something to say."

Email, newsletters, podcasts, YouTube channels — these are all permission delivery systems. Once someone subscribes, reaching them costs almost nothing. The marginal cost of sending your newsletter to 1,000 people vs. 10,000 people is approximately zero.

This creates an enormous compounding advantage for service businesses that build permission assets early. The coach with 5,000 engaged email subscribers has an asset worth far more than the one running paid ads to cold traffic.

---

## The Promise Is Sacred

Here's where most businesses break down: they earn permission, then abuse it.

Godin's rule:

> "You say, 'I will do x, y and z, I hope you will give me permission by listening.' And then, this is the hard part, that's all you do. You don't assume you can do more. You don't sell the list or rent the list or demand more attention."

The promise you make when someone subscribes is a contract. Violate it — by changing the frequency, shifting the topic, or suddenly pivoting to daily sales pitches — and you don't just lose subscribers. You lose permission. Trust, once broken, doesn't easily rebuild.

Godin himself demonstrates this: when he published *Permission Marketing*, he offered readers a third of the book free in exchange for their email address. Then he never emailed those people again. That wasn't the deal. A deal is a deal.

## Application for Coaching/Service Businesses

For a coaching or service business, permission marketing translates to:

**Build before you need it.** Your email list, your newsletter, your podcast audience — these take time to grow. Start before you need clients, not when you're desperate for them.

**Make a specific promise.** "Every Tuesday, I send one framework for growing your consulting revenue" is a real promise. "Subscribe for updates" is not.

**Deliver before you ask.** The default mode should be giving. Offers and pitches should be occasional and earned, not the default content.

**Treat your list like a relationship, not a channel.** Real permission feels like a friendship with someone genuinely helpful. Transactional permission feels like a company's marketing department.

The coaching businesses that dominate their markets — the ones with waitlists and premium pricing power — almost universally have strong permission assets. That's not a coincidence.
