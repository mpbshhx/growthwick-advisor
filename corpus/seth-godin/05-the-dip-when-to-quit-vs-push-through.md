# The Dip: Knowing When to Quit vs. When to Push Through

**Source:** Seth Godin, *The Dip: A Little Book That Teaches You When to Quit (and When to Stick)* (2007); seths.blog  
**Primary URL:** https://seths.blog  
**Corpus role:** Strategic resilience framework — distinguishing temporary setbacks from permanent dead ends

---

## The Dip in a Sentence

Winners quit all the time. They just quit the right things.

That's the core argument of *The Dip*, and it contradicts almost everything motivational culture tells us. "Never quit." "Winners never quit, quitters never win." All wrong, according to Godin.

The question isn't whether to quit. It's **what to quit and when.** Strategic quitting is the path to excellence. Stubborn persistence at the wrong things is the path to mediocrity.

---

## What the Dip Is

The Dip is the long, frustrating trough between starting something and mastering it.

You start a new business. First week, there's excitement. Month one, you're getting traction. Month three, it gets hard. The easy wins are gone. Real obstacles appear. Progress slows. It feels like nothing is working.

That's the Dip.

The Dip is not failure. The Dip is the mechanism that creates scarcity. Because most people quit during the Dip, the people who push through emerge into a marketplace with far fewer competitors. **The Dip is the price of admission to being the best in the world at something.**

This is why the best in any field command premium prices and disproportionate rewards. It's not just that they're better. It's that they paid the full Dip tax, and most people didn't.

---

## Three Curves

Godin identifies three situations, not just one:

**The Dip** — a temporary setback that rewards persistence. Hard now, better later. You're building something real, and the difficulty is the barrier that protects your eventual success. Push through.

**The Cul-de-Sac** — a dead end. No matter how hard you push, you go nowhere. Effort goes in; progress never comes out. This could be a career path with no advancement, a market segment that doesn't value what you offer, a relationship that fundamentally doesn't work. The trap: we don't recognize cul-de-sacs because they look like dips. We keep pushing, expecting the progress that never comes.

**The Cliff** — a situation where things are great right up until they're catastrophically not. Common in industries where the end comes suddenly: Kodak's film business, the Yellow Pages salesman, the taxi medallion owner. The cliff punishes those who don't quit early enough.

Strategic excellence means: **identify your Dips early, push through them. Identify your Cul-de-Sacs early, quit before you've wasted years. Never ride a Cliff.**

## Being Best in the World

The Dip's underlying premise: the rewards in most fields flow disproportionately to the person (or business) that is "best in the world" in that category.

"Best in the world" doesn't mean globally best at everything. It means:

- Best in your city at executive coaching for healthcare professionals
- Best newsletter writer in the accounting space
- Best consultant for early-stage tech companies navigating their first institutional raise
- Best career coach for former military officers transitioning to corporate

"World" is defined by your audience. Their world. The world they search in when they have your problem.

In a Google-mediated world, "best" gets the click. Second best gets almost nothing. This winner-take-most dynamic makes being best in a small category far more valuable than being mediocre in a large one.

The Dip is what separates you from the people who give up before becoming best. Embrace it. Name it. Track your progress through it.

## How to Know If You're in a Dip or a Cul-de-Sac

This is the hardest practical question, and Godin doesn't pretend it's simple. But he offers useful heuristics:

**Signs you're in a Dip (worth pushing through):**
- You can articulate what the end state looks like
- Progress is measurable, even if slow
- External validation suggests you're on the right track
- The difficulty is skill-based (you're just not good enough yet)
- Comparable people have pushed through this and emerged successfully

**Signs you're in a Cul-de-Sac (quit strategically):**
- There's no clear definition of what "made it" looks like
- You've been at it for years with no directional progress
- The market is consistently indifferent, not just temporarily slow
- You'd be embarrassed to tell peers you're still doing this in 5 years
- The environment has fundamentally changed and the thing you're building no longer fits

The dangerous middle ground: **being too comfortable.** Not thriving, but not suffering enough to quit. Enough stability to stay, not enough progress to know if you're in a Dip or a Cul-de-Sac. This is where most mediocre businesses live — perpetually in no man's land.

## The Role of Quitting in Building Excellence

Quitting the cul-de-sacs frees up the resources to push harder through the actual Dips.

Every business owner has a finite amount of energy, money, and attention. Spreading these across too many things — some Dips, some Cul-de-Sacs, some Cliffs — produces mediocre performance across the board.

Godin's prescription: ruthlessly quit everything that isn't a Dip worth pushing through. Redirect all resources to the one or two Dips where you have a real shot at "best in the world."

For a coaching or service business, this often means:
- Quitting the service line that has never gained traction (Cul-de-Sac)
- Quitting the audience segment that never converts (Cul-de-Sac)
- Doubling down on the client type where results are extraordinary (the real Dip)
- Accepting the difficulty of building a specialized reputation (pushing through the Dip)

## Quitting Strategically Requires Deciding Before You Start

The most useful Dip advice: **decide in advance what would cause you to quit, and decide in advance what progress through the Dip would look like.**

Because in the middle of the Dip, you're too demoralized to think clearly. Every obstacle feels permanent. Every setback feels like evidence that you're wrong. Making decisions while in the Dip produces bad strategy.

Decide before:
- "I'll give this market segment 12 months. If I don't have 3 paying clients by month 9, I'll pivot."
- "I'll build this audience for 18 months. If I don't reach 1,000 engaged subscribers, I'll revisit."
- "I'll push through the discomfort of public speaking until I've done 20 talks."

Set the benchmarks. Then push.

## The Dip for Coaching Practices

For coaches specifically, the Dip often looks like:

**Year 1:** Excitement, first clients, figuring it out. Everything is hard but energizing.

**Year 1-3:** The Dip. Referral momentum hasn't built yet. You're not yet "known" in your niche. Marketing feels like screaming into a void. Some early clients don't renew. The economic pressure mounts.

**Year 3-5:** Emergence. For those who push through, the reputation starts to compound. Referrals accelerate. You can be more selective with clients. Premium pricing becomes possible.

Most coaches quit during the Dip — usually between year 1 and year 3 — and conclude that coaching "doesn't work" as a business. What actually happened: they exited before the compound interest kicked in.

The coaches at the top of their field are not necessarily more talented than those who quit. They pushed through the Dip.
