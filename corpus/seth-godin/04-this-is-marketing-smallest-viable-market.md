# This Is Marketing: The Smallest Viable Market and Making Change

**Source:** Seth Godin, *This Is Marketing: You Can't Be Seen Until You Learn to See* (2018); seths.blog  
**Primary URL:** https://seths.blog/2019/01/what-is-marketing/  
**Corpus role:** Modern marketing philosophy — empathy, specificity, and the ethics of making change for the people who want it

---

## What Marketing Actually Is

From Seth's blog, January 2019:

> "If you need to persuade someone to take action, you're doing marketing. If you're looking for votes at the city council meeting, or looking for a promotion, you're marketing. If you're writing copy on your website, taking a selfie for your social media profile or trying to talk your way out of a speeding ticket, you're marketing."

Most people who say "I don't do marketing" mean "I don't spend money on ads." Those are very different things.

*This Is Marketing* is Godin's most complete synthesis of his philosophy — drawing together permission marketing, the Purple Cow, tribes, and a deeper ethical framework. The core argument: **marketing is making change happen for people who want it, in ways that benefit them and you.**

That's a fundamentally different definition than what most people learned.

## The Smallest Viable Market

The most operationally important concept in the book: **start with the smallest viable market, not the biggest possible one.**

Godin's instruction:

> "What's the minimum number of people you would need to reach to make this worth doing? Find that number. Ignore everyone else."

This is counterintuitive to almost every business instinct. We're trained to think bigger is better — larger addressable market, wider appeal, more potential customers.

Godin inverts this completely. The smaller your intended market, the more precisely you can serve them. The more precisely you serve them, the more remarkable you become to them. The more remarkable you are, the more they talk about you. The more they talk, the bigger you eventually grow.

The path to scale runs through specificity. The generalist never gets there.

For a coaching or service business:

- "I help business owners" = too broad to be remarkable to anyone
- "I help women-owned service businesses with $500K–$2M revenue who want to systematize without losing their culture" = small enough to be remarkable, specific enough to spread

The smallest viable market for most coaching practices is much smaller than the founder imagines. It could be 200 people. It could be 500. The question is: can you find enough of them, and are they reachable?

## Empathy Before Tactics

The subtitle — *You Can't Be Seen Until You Learn to See* — points to the book's deepest layer: before you can create effective marketing, you have to deeply understand the people you're trying to serve.

Godin calls this "seeing the world through the eyes of the people you seek to serve." Not just understanding their demographics (age, location, income) but their:

- **Dreams** — what they're hoping for, what their lives would look like if the problem was solved
- **Fears** — what keeps them up at night, what they're protecting against
- **Worldview** — how they see themselves, what they believe, what narrative they operate within
- **Status** — how your product/service makes them feel about themselves relative to others they care about

The marketer who skips this step and goes straight to tactics (what should my Instagram feed look like? what should my email subject lines say?) is building on sand.

The marketer who does this work first — who can say with genuine conviction "I know exactly who I'm serving and what they actually want" — has a durable advantage that tactics can't replicate.

## Marketing as an Act of Service

One of the most important reframes in the book: **good marketing is a generous act.**

The traditional view of marketing is adversarial: marketers try to separate people from their money, consumers try to resist. The whole game is manipulation vs. defense.

Godin rejects this entirely. His definition:

> "Marketing is the generous act of helping someone solve a problem. Their problem."

If you've found someone with a real problem, and you have a genuine solution, and you're telling them about it in a way that's relevant and useful — you're not imposing on them. You're helping them. The reluctance to market is often a failure of belief: you're not sure enough that your work genuinely helps people.

The coach who truly believes her coaching changes lives shouldn't have to be persuaded to market. She should be compelled to. Not marketing would be *withholding* something valuable from people who need it.

## Status, Affiliation, and the Invisible Forces

*This Is Marketing* goes deep into the psychology of why people buy. Two underappreciated drivers:

**Status** — where do I sit in my peer group? Does buying this make me feel more or less advanced, successful, respected? Status is rarely spoken aloud but almost always present. Luxury goods are pure status. But so is buying the "right" business coach (signals you're serious) or the "right" methodology (signals sophistication).

For coaches: your positioning signals something about the status of your clients. Working with you should feel like an upgrade, not a compromise. Are you positioned as the coach that serious people hire, or as the affordable option?

**Affiliation** — who do I want to be like? Tribes and affiliation are inseparable. We buy in ways that connect us to groups we aspire to join and distance us from groups we want to leave.

The coaching client isn't just buying results. They're buying membership in the group of people who "figured it out" or "took the leap" or "invested in themselves." Smart positioning activates these affiliation signals.

## Making Change Happen

The final frame of the book: **marketing is about making change happen.**

If you're trying to get someone to change their behavior — try a new approach, invest in coaching, leave a bad situation, step into a better one — you're in the change-making business.

Change is hard. People default to the status quo because the devil you know is less terrifying than the one you don't. The marketer's job is to lower the psychological cost of change and raise the perceived cost of staying the same.

This has implications for how coaches structure their message:

- **What is the "before" state?** Describe it in a way that resonates — the client should recognize themselves.
- **What's wrong with the before state?** Not just inconvenient — what is it actually costing them?
- **What does the "after" state look like?** Be specific and emotional, not abstract and rational.
- **What's the bridge?** How does working with you move someone from before to after?

Marketing as change-making is marketing as advocacy for the client's better self. That's a fundamentally more dignified way to think about what you do.

## Marketing as a Craft (Not Hustle)

From Seth's blog, June 2026, on teaching marketing:

> "There are at least three kinds of 'marketing' we ought to be teaching... Marketing as a craft. Strategic marketing. Telling stories that spread. Building an asset. Marketing as a service on behalf of your customers. Owning the responsibility that goes with the leverage that marketers have. Most organized marketing instruction is about the first or second... My best work is about the third kind, the one where it all began."

This is the *This Is Marketing* spirit: reject hustle, reject manipulation, embrace craft. Marketing as something you can be proud of. Marketing that serves before it sells.
