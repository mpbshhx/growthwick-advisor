# Purple Cow: Being Remarkable, Not "Very Good"

**Source:** Seth Godin, *Purple Cow: Transform Your Business by Being Remarkable* (2003); seths.blog  
**Primary URL:** https://seths.blog  
**Corpus role:** Differentiation philosophy — why "very good" is invisible and how to create something worth talking about

---

## The Metaphor That Changes Everything

Imagine driving through the French countryside. Fields, farmhouses, trees. And cows. Hundreds of cows. Brown cows, black cows, spotted cows. They're beautiful the first time you see them. Remarkable, even.

But by the time you've driven past the fourteenth field of cows, you're not seeing them at all. They're wallpaper.

Now imagine a purple cow. You'd pull over. You'd take a photo. You'd call someone. *You'd tell people.*

That's Seth Godin's entire argument in *Purple Cow*: in a world saturated with good products and competent services, only the remarkable survives. Not "better." Not "excellent." **Remarkable** — literally worth remarking about.

The word comes from "remark." Something worth talking about. Something that causes people to spread the word on your behalf.

---

## The Death of "Very Good"

The old formula for business success: make a good product, buy advertising, reach the masses, profit. This worked because:
1. There weren't many products competing for attention
2. Mass media was the primary distribution channel
3. "Good" was actually rare enough to be noteworthy

That formula is dead. Not ineffective — dead.

Today, every category is crowded with good options. The grocery store has 47 kinds of olive oil. There are thousands of business coaches. Every city has dozens of capable accountants. "Good" isn't remarkable anymore. "Very good" is ignored. Even "excellent" fails to spread.

Godin's observation: **the riskiest thing you can do is be safe.** Playing it safe produces average results, and average results are invisible. The businesses taking real risks — being weird, specific, opinionated, different — those are the ones getting talked about.

---

## Remarkable Is Not About Being Loud

A critical misunderstanding: people confuse "remarkable" with "loud," "flashy," or "outrageous." That's not it.

Remarkable means:
- **Doing something so specifically good for a specific person that they can't stop talking about it**
- **Making a choice that matters to the people you're trying to serve**
- **Being different in a way that's relevant to someone**

A remarkable financial advisor isn't the one with the catchiest tagline. It's the one who does something no other financial advisor does — serves only divorced women rebuilding wealth, or only second-generation immigrants navigating US tax law for the first time, or only early-career doctors managing student loan strategy with their income ramp.

The purple cow isn't purple for its own sake. It's purple because to the right person, that's exactly the cow they needed.

## The Idea Virus

Connected to Purple Cow is Godin's concept of the *ideavirus* — ideas that spread from person to person like a contagion.

The most powerful marketing isn't advertising. It's word of mouth. And word of mouth only happens when someone has a remarkable experience — something worth telling another person about.

The math on this is brutal: a satisfied customer tells nobody. A disappointed customer tells ten people. A genuinely wowed customer tells three to five people, repeatedly, over time.

Remarkable experiences generate ideaviruses. Competent experiences generate... silence.

For service businesses: your clients are either silently satisfied or loudly referring. There's no middle ground that produces growth. The only path to referral-driven growth is being remarkable enough that clients *have* to tell someone.

## The Otaku Concept

Godin borrows the Japanese concept of *otaku* — an obsessive, slightly fanatical interest — to describe the early adopters who drive word of mouth.

These are the people who care deeply about a specific thing. Car enthusiasts who spend weekends reading about cars. Coffee snobs who travel across town for the right espresso. Parents obsessive about their kid's education.

The purple cow doesn't have to appeal to everyone. It has to trigger the otaku — the people who care enough to tell others.

For a coaching business: who are your otaku? Who are the people so obsessed with the problem you solve that they're already talking about it, reading about it, and actively searching for the best solution? Those people will find your purple cow — but only if it's actually remarkable to *them*.

## What "Safe" Actually Costs You

The counterintuitive insight: most business owners think playing it safe minimizes risk. Godin argues the opposite.

Safe is expensive because:
- It doesn't spread (no word of mouth, no organic growth)
- It requires you to keep buying attention (paid ads, cold outreach, networking)
- It commoditizes you (you compete on price with everyone else in the category)
- It creates no defensible position (competitors can always copy "good")

Remarkable, by contrast:
- Generates its own awareness through word of mouth
- Earns premium pricing (there's only one purple cow)
- Creates a defensible position (hard to copy something genuinely specific)
- Attracts your best clients and repels the wrong ones

The coaching business that niches down aggressively — the one that specializes so specifically it feels risky — almost always outperforms the generalist who plays it safe.

## Remarkable for Service Businesses: Practical Framework

What makes a coaching or consulting practice remarkable?

**1. Specificity of audience** — "I help women returning to the workforce after caregiving gaps" is a purple cow. "I help professionals" is a brown cow.

**2. Specificity of outcome** — "I help first-time managers have the conversation they've been avoiding for six months" is a purple cow. "I help with leadership development" is wallpaper.

**3. Methodology that has a name** — Remarkable practices often have proprietary frameworks, signature programs, or named systems. The name isn't marketing fluff — it signals that you thought hard enough about your work to define it.

**4. A point of view that excludes** — Purple cows have opinions. A remarkable coach doesn't just have a niche; they have a perspective that some people will love and some will reject. That rejection is the cost of being remarkable.

**5. Something worth talking about** — Test: would a client spontaneously mention you to a friend? If so, what would they say? If you can't answer that clearly, the purple cow isn't fully formed yet.

## The Real Artists Parallel

From Seth's blog, June 2026:

> "Real artists do all the painting themselves, not like Rembrandt. Real artists use brushes, not technology like Cartier-Bresson... It turns out that real artists have always used technology. What they have in common is intent, responsibility, and the ability to create a feeling in the audience. 'Here, I made this.'"

The purple cow is the same thing. It's not about the gimmick. It's about the intent and the feeling you create. "Here, I made this" — for *you*, specifically.
