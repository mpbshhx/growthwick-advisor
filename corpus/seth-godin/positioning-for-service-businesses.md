# Seth Godin: Positioning for Service Businesses

*Source: Seth Godin — This Is Marketing, Purple Cow, The Practice, Seth's Blog*

---

## The Core Premise

Most service businesses position themselves by describing what they do. Seth Godin's argument is that this is exactly backwards. Positioning isn't about what you do — it's about who you're for and what change you make. The consultant who can answer both questions precisely has already outmaneuvered 90% of their competitors, who can only answer the first.

---

## Be For Someone Specific: The Smallest Viable Market

Godin's most counterintuitive principle, and the one most consultants resist: shrink your target market until it feels dangerously small.

The instinct is to go broad. Broader audience = more potential clients. More potential clients = more revenue. The logic seems airtight. It fails in practice because:

1. Broad positioning produces generic messaging that resonates with no one deeply
2. Specialists command premium prices; generalists compete on price
3. Referrals require that your clients can describe who you help in one sentence — broad positioning makes that sentence impossible

The **smallest viable market** is the smallest group of people for whom you can create something so relevant, so precisely fitted to their situation, that they would feel it was made for them. Not "small businesses" but "bootstrapped SaaS founders between $500K and $2M ARR who are trying to hire their first sales rep." Not "executives" but "newly promoted VPs of Marketing at B2B companies navigating their first 90 days."

The test: can a current client describe your ideal prospect to a colleague in a single sentence that would let that colleague immediately identify whether they qualify? If not, your market definition is still too broad.

## Category Creation vs. Category Entry

There are two strategies for positioning a service business. Most people try to enter an existing category and win. Godin's preference — and the higher-value play — is to create a new category.

**Category entry** means positioning yourself within a pre-existing frame. "I'm a business coach." "We're a digital marketing agency." The advantage: prospects already understand what you do. The disadvantage: you're in a crowded field with established competitors, and prospects have already developed price expectations and skepticism.

**Category creation** means defining a new frame that didn't exist before you named it. This is harder but creates a durable positioning advantage. When you create the category, you become the default leader of it — at least temporarily. Examples of category creation from the consulting world: "fractional CMO," "product-market fit coach," "AI readiness advisor."

To create a category, you need three things:
1. A name that immediately signals who it's for and what problem it solves
2. A point of view that explains why the old way of doing things is broken
3. A specific claim about what changes for the people you serve

Godin's test for a real category: can someone easily explain to their boss, spouse, or colleague why hiring you is different from hiring the other options? If they can, the category is real. If they'd stumble, you're still just another provider in a known category.

## Commodity vs. Specialist: The Positioning Gap

A commodity is something where the buyer assumes all options are roughly equivalent and therefore makes the decision on price. A specialist is someone the buyer is willing to pay a premium for because they believe this person understands their specific situation better than the alternatives.

The gap between commodity and specialist isn't skill. It's specificity.

A generalist copywriter is a commodity. A copywriter who only works with Series A SaaS companies on their first sales enablement deck is a specialist — even if their underlying skills are identical. The market doesn't reward generalist excellence. It rewards relevant expertise.

Godin's mechanism for the shift:
- **Pick a problem, not a service.** "I write copy" is a service. "I help SaaS companies reduce churn through better in-app messaging" is a problem. Problems command specialist fees; services command commodity prices.
- **Own a point of view.** A commodity provider does what they're asked. A specialist tells you what you should be asking for. The willingness to say "that approach won't work, here's why" is what separates trusted advisors from vendors.
- **Refuse some clients.** Nothing signals specialist positioning faster than saying no. When prospects see that you turn away people who aren't the right fit, they become more confident you're the right fit for them.

---

## Stop Trying to Appeal to Everyone

Godin is explicit: trying to appeal to everyone means mattering deeply to no one. Every word you add to make your positioning broader dilutes its power with the people it could have resonated with completely.

The fear behind broad positioning is usually: "What if my ideal client doesn't find me? At least if I'm broad, I can catch the others." Godin's response is that the others — the ones outside your ideal niche — will always find a provider who seems more relevant to them. You won't convert them. You'll just confuse your actual ideal clients in the process.

Practical implications:
- Your website headline should describe a specific person or situation, not a category of service
- Your case studies should feature clients who look like more of the clients you want
- Your content should take positions that some people will disagree with — because that's what attracts the people who agree deeply
- Your pricing should reflect specialist positioning — if your price doesn't cause some prospects to self-select out, you're positioned as a commodity

## The "Who's It For and What's It For" Positioning Test

Godin's simplest and most useful positioning diagnostic. Before publishing any piece of marketing, before proposing a new service, before redesigning a website, answer both questions:

**Who's it for?** Name a specific person. Not a demographic. A person with a specific role, a specific problem, a specific emotional state. "The overwhelmed founder who just hit $1M in revenue and realizes they've never had a real marketing strategy" is a who. "Small business owners" is not.

**What's it for?** Describe the change it creates. Not the deliverable — the transformation. "A 90-day coaching engagement" is a deliverable. "The confidence to raise prices without losing clients" is what it's for. "A content strategy audit" is a deliverable. "Knowing exactly why their content isn't converting and what to change first" is what it's for.

If you can answer both questions in one sentence each, your positioning is ready to test. If you can't — or if your answer to "who's it for" is "lots of different people" — the positioning work isn't done yet.

---

## Key Principle

Positioning is a choice, and it requires courage. The courage to say "not for you" to some prospects. The courage to name a specific person and risk being wrong about them. The courage to take a point of view that not everyone will agree with. The consultants and coaches who do this consistently become the ones everyone in their niche already knows.
