# Ann Handley: Storytelling for Service Businesses

*Source: Ann Handley — Everybody Writes, MarketingProfs, Total Annarchy newsletter*

---

## The Core Premise

Most service business owners think storytelling is something writers do. Ann Handley's argument is the opposite: storytelling is something businesses do when they finally get out of their own way. The problem isn't that your business doesn't have compelling stories — it's that you're telling them from the wrong angle, about the wrong character, using the wrong frame.

Her framework is practical, replicable, and designed specifically for people who don't think of themselves as writers.

---

## The Story Spine Technique

The story spine is a structural tool borrowed from improvisational theater (specifically Pixar's storytelling methodology, which Handley adapted for marketing contexts). It forces you to build narrative structure before you write a single polished sentence.

The template:

- **Once upon a time…** (establish the world before your client met you)
- **Every day…** (describe the problem they were living with)
- **Until one day…** (the moment they decided something had to change)
- **Because of that…** (what happened when they took action)
- **Because of that…** (what changed as a result — repeat as needed)
- **Until finally…** (the transformation)
- **And ever since then…** (the new normal)

Used on a client case study, this structure stops you from writing the kind of "we did X, then we did Y, results were Z" narrative that puts readers to sleep. Instead, it creates dramatic tension — there's a before and after, a moment of decision, a progression of consequence.

The spine is a drafting tool, not a template to publish verbatim. Once you fill it in, rewrite it in plain language. The structure stays; the fairy-tale phrasing goes.

## Your Customer Is the Hero — Not You

This is Handley's most important reframe for service businesses, and the one most consistently violated.

The typical service business website or case study positions the company as the hero: "We built a strategy. We executed the campaign. We delivered results." The customer appears as a passive recipient of your brilliance.

The problem: your prospect is not reading your content to hear about you. They're reading it to see themselves — to find a character who had the same problem they have and find out what happened.

**Hero positioning rules:**
1. Name the client's situation and pain first, before introducing yourself
2. Credit the client for the decision and the execution, even if you did the work
3. Frame your role as "guide," not "hero" — you're Yoda, not Luke
4. Lead with the client's transformation, not your methodology

A simple test: read your last case study or bio. Count the sentences that start with "We" or reference your firm versus the sentences that reference your client or their world. If the ratio is more than 1:1 in your favor, the story is about the wrong character.

## Case Studies as Storytelling Vehicles

Handley argues that case studies are the most underused storytelling asset in professional services. The reason they're usually terrible: they're written as performance reviews instead of stories.

A strong case study has these elements:
- **A relatable villain** — the problem, the competitor, the internal dysfunction, the missed opportunity. Name it specifically. "Low website traffic" is not a villain. "Losing deals to competitors who ranked for terms we didn't even know to target" is a villain.
- **Specific, surprising details** — not "revenue increased significantly" but "we went from 12 inbound leads a month to 47 in the first 90 days." Specificity is what makes a story feel true.
- **The moment of change** — what was the turning point? What did the client try first? What failed before the solution that worked?
- **A human voice** — include a quote that sounds like a person talking, not a press release. If the quote could appear in any case study for any company, it's not doing its job.

Format guidance from Handley: aim for 500–800 words for a standard case study. Anything shorter lacks narrative weight; anything longer loses the reader. Use subheads to allow scanning. Lead with the result (not the process) in the headline.

## The "So What" Test

Every piece of content you create — case study, email, article, LinkedIn post — needs to pass the "so what" test before it goes out.

After you write something, ask: if a reader finished this, so what? What would they think, feel, do, or believe differently than they did before reading it?

If the answer is "nothing, really," the content doesn't earn its place. It's content for the sake of content.

The "so what" isn't always a call to action. It might be:
- A new framing they hadn't considered
- A specific piece of information they can use today
- An emotional connection to a problem they've been quietly carrying
- A decision made easier because of something you clarified

Handley's rule: if you can't state the "so what" in one sentence before you write the piece, you don't know what you're writing yet. Start there.

## Finding Compelling Angles in Ordinary Stories

The most common excuse: "Our business isn't interesting enough for storytelling." Handley's response is that interesting isn't found — it's excavated.

Techniques for finding angles in ordinary businesses:

**The unexpected contrast.** What do people assume about your industry that's wrong? Lead with the assumption, then flip it. "Most people think hiring a bookkeeper saves time. Our clients find it saves their marriage."

**The specific moment.** Instead of "we help businesses grow," describe the exact meeting, email, or conversation where everything changed for one client.

**The counterintuitive result.** Did the client try to solve Problem A and accidentally solve Problem B? Did a perceived weakness turn out to be a strength? The twist is the story.

**The confession.** What mistake did you make early on that shaped how you do things now? Vulnerability with a lesson attached is extraordinarily engaging. Handley calls this the "path to earned wisdom" — you're not just sharing knowledge, you're showing that you paid for it.

**The small, specific detail.** Not "our client was stressed." But "she called me at 11pm on a Tuesday because she couldn't figure out why her margins had collapsed." Specificity creates believability. Believability creates trust.

---

## Key Principle

You don't need a dramatic story. You need a true one, told from the right angle, about the right character, with a clear reason for the reader to care. That combination is available to every service business that's ever helped anyone.
