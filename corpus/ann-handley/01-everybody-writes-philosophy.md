# The "Everybody Writes" Philosophy — Writing as a Core Business Skill

**Source Authority:** Ann Handley — Wall Street Journal bestselling author, Chief Content Officer at MarketingProfs, author of *Everybody Writes* (2nd Ed., 2022)
**Primary Sources:** annhandley.com/everybodywrites/, Seth Godin endorsement (public), Ann Handley keynotes and interviews

---

## The Core Premise

Ann Handley's central argument is simple and radical at the same time: **writing is not a talent reserved for "writers." It is a learnable business skill that every person in every organization must develop.**

In *Everybody Writes*, Handley opens with a declaration that makes marketers, business owners, and entrepreneurs uncomfortable: your website, your emails, your social posts, your proposals — they all speak for you before you get a chance to. Every sentence you publish is either earning trust or eroding it.

Seth Godin, in his endorsement of the book, frames it perfectly: *"Writing is a skill, not a talent. That's great news, because it means it can be learned."*

This distinction matters enormously. Talent is fixed. Skill is developed. If writing is a skill, then every business owner who says "I'm not a writer" is simply someone who hasn't practiced yet.

---

## Why This Matters for Service Businesses

For solopreneurs, consultants, coaches, and small agency owners, this philosophy has direct revenue implications.

When you write poorly — or avoid writing altogether — you cede the field to competitors who may be less skilled but more articulate. Your expertise stays locked inside your head. Potential clients can't see it. They can't trust what they can't perceive.

Handley's argument is that in the attention economy, **publishing is the proof of expertise.** The business that shows up consistently with clear, useful, human writing becomes the obvious choice — not because they're better, but because they're *visible in the right way.*

Think about the last time you hired a consultant, bought a course, or chose a service provider. Odds are you read something they wrote first. A blog post. An email. A LinkedIn article. That writing did the selling before the conversation ever started.

## The "Ridiculously Good Content" Standard

Handley doesn't just argue that you should write more — she argues you should write *better*. The full subtitle of her book is "Your New and Improved Go-To Guide to Creating Ridiculously Good Content."

What does "ridiculously good" mean in practice? Three things:

**1. Clear writing equals clear thinking.**
If your writing is muddy, your ideas are muddy. There's no such thing as "I know what I mean but can't express it." If you can't express it, you don't fully know it yet. Writing forces clarity.

**2. Good writing creates deeper customer relationships.**
This is Handley's stated goal: not just conversions, not just traffic, but *relationships*. Content that helps, teaches, entertains, or challenges someone builds the kind of trust that advertising can never buy.

**3. Ridiculously good is a competitive moat.**
Most business content is mediocre. Safe. Generic. It sounds like it was written by a committee of cautious people trying not to offend anyone. Handley's framework explicitly aims to help you stand *apart* — not blend in.

## The Writing Process Handley Recommends

Handley is emphatic that good writing is a *process*, not an act of inspiration. Her recommended approach, drawn from *Everybody Writes*:

**Step 1: Think before you write.**
What's the one thing you want your reader to walk away knowing, feeling, or doing? If you can't answer that in one sentence, you're not ready to write yet.

**Step 2: Write ugly.**
The first draft is not for quality. It's for existence. Get the ideas down without editing. The internal editor kills most writing before it starts. Give yourself permission to be bad on the first pass.

**Step 3: Rewrite ruthlessly.**
This is where writing actually happens. Reading back your draft, cutting everything that doesn't serve the reader, restructuring for clarity, and tightening every sentence.

**Step 4: Read it out loud.**
This is the single fastest way to catch awkward phrasing, run-on sentences, and places where your voice goes flat. If you stumble reading it, your reader will too.

**Step 5: Have someone else read it.**
Fresh eyes catch what familiarity hides. Even a one-sentence "does this make sense?" to a trusted colleague is better than publishing blind.

## What This Means for Growthwick Clients

For a service business owner who feels they "can't write," the Handley framework offers a reframe: **you don't need to be a gifted writer. You need a consistent practice and a commitment to your reader.**

Concrete starting points from the Everybody Writes framework:

- **Start with what you know.** Your expertise is the raw material. Writing isn't about inventing ideas — it's about organizing and sharing what you already know.
- **Write to one person.** Don't write to "my audience." Write to the specific person who most needs what you have. One reader, addressed directly, always reads better than vague broadcast.
- **Publish before you're ready.** Perfectionism is the enemy of progress. A 700-word blog post published every two weeks beats a perfect 3,000-word piece that never ships.
- **Treat every piece as a conversation.** Good business writing doesn't lecture. It invites response. End pieces with questions, invitations, or clear next steps.

## The Business Case in Numbers

Handley's broader body of work at MarketingProfs consistently reinforces the ROI case for writing:

- Businesses with active blogs generate significantly more inbound leads than those without
- Email newsletters with a distinctive voice have dramatically higher open rates than generic "company updates"
- Thought leadership content — essays, frameworks, point-of-view pieces — builds pipeline in ways that ads cannot replicate

The bottom line: **in a world of AI-generated sameness, your genuine human voice is a competitive advantage.** The "Everybody Writes" philosophy isn't just about getting better at grammar. It's about owning the most underutilized asset most service businesses already have — their expertise, expressed in their own words.
