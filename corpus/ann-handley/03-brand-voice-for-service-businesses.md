# Finding Your Brand Voice: Ann Handley's Framework for Service Businesses

**Source Authority:** Ann Handley — "Milking Brand Voice for All It's Worth" (Total Annarchy newsletter / annhandley.com); *Everybody Writes* Chapter on Voice
**Primary Sources:** annhandley.com/brand-voice/ (Oatly case study); annhandley.com/everybodywrites/

## The Voice Problem Most Businesses Have

Most service business websites sound identical. They use the same phrases: "results-driven," "client-focused," "passionate about excellence," "innovative solutions." Strip the logo off most consulting or coaching websites and you couldn't tell one from another.

Ann Handley's work on brand voice addresses exactly this problem — and her insight is counterintuitive: **voice isn't something you craft. It's something you reveal.**

The goal isn't to build a persona. It's to stop suppressing the personality that already exists.

---

## What Brand Voice Actually Is

Handley draws a critical distinction between *voice* and *tone*:

- **Voice** is your brand's consistent personality — who you are. It doesn't change.
- **Tone** is how you express that personality in a given context — it adapts. You might be warmer in a welcome email, more precise in a technical guide, and more urgent in a deadline-driven offer. But the underlying voice stays constant.

The analogy: you have the same personality whether you're at a job interview or dinner with old friends. But your tone shifts. Voice = who you are. Tone = how you show up in the room.

## The Oatly Case Study: What Fearless Brand Voice Looks Like

Handley interviewed Kevin Lynch, Creative Director at Oatly, for her Total Annarchy newsletter. The Oatly case study is one of the most instructive examples of committed brand voice in modern marketing.

Oatly named their email newsletter *Spam*. They promoted it on billboards in New York and in the London Underground. The campaign was called "Spam Week."

Kevin Lynch's explanation of their brand voice philosophy: *"We like to be 'consistently inconsistent.' And we try to make sure the brand acts 'like a human, not a company.'"*

When asked about criticism (one Reddit user called their advertising "grating and pathetic faux-cool bullshit"), Lynch responded: *"I really don't understand the level of fear marketers have about drawing criticism. Why not look at criticism as a sign that people care, and treat it as only the beginning of a conversation? We don't necessarily wish to be hated. But being ignored — now that's something to fear."*

Handley's takeaway for the rest of us: **Brand voice isn't about being safe. It's about being memorable.** And memorable content always risks making someone uncomfortable.

## Handley's Voice Framework: Four Dimensions

From *Everybody Writes* and her keynote work, Handley identifies four dimensions of authentic brand voice:

**1. Tonal range**
Where does your voice land on the spectrum from "formal and professional" to "casual and conversational"? Neither extreme is wrong — but you need to know where you sit and stay consistent. A solopreneur coach who is warm and direct in person but formal and stiff in their writing is creating a disconnect that erodes trust.

**2. Personality traits (with their limits)**
Handley recommends identifying 3-5 personality traits your voice embodies — and then adding the "but not" qualifier for each one. This prevents voice from becoming caricature:

- *Confident* — but not arrogant
- *Casual* — but not sloppy
- *Direct* — but not cold
- *Expert* — but not condescending
- *Warm* — but not saccharine

**3. Point of view**
A voice without opinions isn't a voice — it's a bulletin board. What does your brand actually believe? What do you stand for that makes you different? What would you argue against? Strong voice has a perspective. It believes things. It pushes back on conventional wisdom when conventional wisdom is wrong.

**4. Specific language choices**
Voice shows up in the tiny decisions: Do you use contractions or not? Do you use "I" or "we"? Do you use industry jargon, or do you translate it? Do you use humor, and if so, what kind? These micro-choices, made consistently, accumulate into a distinctive voice.

## The Warren Buffett Writing Principle Applied to Voice

In another Ann Handley piece analyzing Warren Buffett's annual shareholder letter, she captures a principle that applies directly to brand voice:

Buffett writes his letters as if writing to one person: his sister Doris, a Berkshire-Hathaway investor who is smart but has no patience for financial jargon. The result is the most readable corporate communication in financial history.

**The application:** Write to Doris. Not to "my audience" or "my target market" or "our ideal customer profile." To one real person, with a name and a face, who you know, who you want to help.

When you write to a specific person, your voice becomes specific too. The formality drops. The real you shows up. The wall between "business communication" and "human being" comes down.

Handley calls this her single most actionable writing tip: *Write to Doris.*

## Practical Steps: Finding Your Voice

**Step 1: Collect samples of your "real" communication.**
Pull together 5-10 emails or messages you've written to clients where you felt most like yourself — where you were helpful, direct, maybe a little funny. That's your voice in its natural state.

**Step 2: Identify the patterns.**
What recurring phrases do you use? What's the general rhythm — short, punchy sentences or longer, discursive ones? How do you open? How do you close? Do you use analogies? Stories? Lists?

**Step 3: Write your voice characteristics (with the "but not" pairs).**
Three to five traits. Be specific. "Professional" isn't a trait — it's a baseline. "Blunt about what doesn't work, but always constructive" is a trait.

**Step 4: Create a one-page voice guide.**
Not a 40-page brand manual. A one-page guide you can actually use. Include: your traits, the "but not" pairs, three word/phrase examples that sound like you, and three that don't.

**Step 5: Audit your website against it.**
Read your current website copy out loud against your voice guide. Mark every sentence that doesn't sound like you. Rewrite it until it does. This is usually the most illuminating exercise — and the most uncomfortable.

## The Unique Advantage Service Businesses Have

Product companies have to build brand voice from scratch — they need to construct a personality around an object. Service businesses have a secret weapon: **you are the brand.**

Your knowledge, your opinions, your way of explaining things, your frustrations with the industry, your genuine enthusiasm for your clients' outcomes — all of that is already the voice. The work is excavation, not construction.

Handley makes this point implicitly throughout *Everybody Writes*: the best content comes from telling stories that could only come from you. Your experience. Your perspective. Your specific take on what works and what doesn't.

For a consultant, coach, or small agency owner, that means your most distinctive content is often built on the thing you've seen go wrong a hundred times, or the counterintuitive approach that consistently gets results, or the client conversation that changed how you think about your work.

Those stories are yours. No competitor can copy them. That's brand voice.
