# The Never Ending Road To Product Market Fit

By Brian Balfour

## Key Framing

Most people think of product-market fit as a definitive point on the startup path. Brian thinks about it as a **series of tests and checkpoints** that increase in difficulty — but also in definitiveness.

The traction phase vs. growth phase distinction matters. Knowing which you're in helps you focus on the right goals, metrics, channels, and team structure. But knowing when to transition requires understanding where you are on the PMF spectrum.

---

## The 4 Product-Market Fit Checkpoints

### Checkpoint 1: The Leading Indicator Survey

Two common surveys:

**1a. Product/Market Fit Survey (Sean Ellis):** "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" → Success = 40%+ respond "Very Disappointed."

**1b. NPS (Net Promoter Score):** Measures customer happiness; used by many as a growth indicator.

**Pros:** Easy to deploy, requires least data, quick signal.

**Cons:**
- Highest chance of false positive
- Hard to answer "how big is the market?" — can't determine whether to step on the gas yet

Rule: Don't step on the gas based on surveys alone until supported by steps 2 or 3.

---

### Checkpoint 2: Leading Indicator Engagement Data

Support survey results with actual behavior data at small scale. What are users actually doing?

Examples:
- Photo sharing app: number of photos users share daily/weekly
- Messaging app: unique people messaged per day
- B2B SaaS invoicing: number of invoices processed per company

Data must align with:
- Events or actions (not views)
- The core purpose of the product

**Faults:**
1. It's probably at small scale
2. Easy to rationalize — you tend to find the data that supports what you want

This is a potential leading indicator, not proof of PMF.

---

### Checkpoint 3: The Retention Curve

Plot % active over time for various cohorts to create your retention curve.

**If it flattens at some point → you probably found product market fit for some market.**

The question then: what/who is that market, and how big is it?

**Segment the retention curve:**
- Key Demographics (Sex, Age, Location, Industry, Company Size)
- Time
- User Source
- Custom segments for your specific business

**Also use qualitative surveys** to identify differences between those who retained vs. those who didn't. If you can't explain the difference, you don't know your market.

**Cons:** Takes time to collect. B2C (messaging, gaming) → fastest. B2B SaaS → slowest (monthly churn timeline).

"Without retention, accelerating growth is meaningless. Your retention curve is the best proof."

---

### Checkpoint 4: The Trifecta

Example: SnapChat, a few months after launch:
- 200K downloads
- 100K daily active users (50% retention!)
- 1 million pictures sent per day (10/DAU)

That's **the trifecta:**
1. **Non-trivial top-line growth** (200K downloads)
2. **Retention** (50% of downloads were active daily)
3. **Meaningful usage** (sending 10 pictures/day on average)

When you have all three, you can say with close to 100% certainty that you have product-market fit among a meaningful market.

If PMF just smacks you in the face — do your happy dance (this rarely happens) and fast forward from traction to growth.

---

## Why It Never Ends

1. **Your market doesn't sit still.** Markets change at an accelerating pace. As your market moves, your product needs to move with it.
2. **Companies expand target audiences** to new segments, triggering the need to re-validate PMF for each new audience.

Large companies tend to lose sight of this. Either they don't move with the market, or they assume PMF when expanding, and end up pushing a product that ultimately fails. That creates opportunity for disruption and startups.

---

## PMF + Growth Balance

It's not an either/or between PMF and Growth — it's a progressing balance of each (per Rob Go of NextView Ventures). As you progress through checkpoints, the volume of users and data needed increases.
