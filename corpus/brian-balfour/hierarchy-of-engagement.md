## Brian Balfour's Engagement Philosophy: Why "One Metric" Thinking Fails

Brian Balfour (founder of Reforge, former VP Growth at HubSpot) has written extensively about why engagement measurement is more complex than most growth teams assume. His core argument is that engagement must be understood as a hierarchy — different layers of metrics that interact with and predict each other.

## The Constellation of Metrics Framework

From his essay "Don't Let Your North Star Metric Deceive You" (brianbalfour.com/essays/north-star-metric-growth):

### The Problem with OMTM (One Metric That Matters)
The "one metric that matters" philosophy (popularized in the early growth hacking era) is a dangerous oversimplification. Four reasons it fails:

**1. Good North Star metrics are output metrics, not input metrics**
- Output metrics = results (DAU, MRR, revenue) — they tell you the score
- Input metrics = actions (pageviews, activations, feature usage) — they tell you how to change the score
- You can't build experiments around output metrics — they're too broad and too distant from what your team can actually change
- Focusing on the output metric gives you the score but not the play

**2. Output metrics are lagging indicators**
- By the time a problem shows up in your north star metric, it's already been percolating in input metrics for weeks or months
- A subscription product's MRR can look healthy while a usage engagement cliff is forming underneath it
- By the time churn shows up in revenue retention, the customers are already gone

**3. A single metric only captures one dimension of the business**
At minimum, a healthy business requires metrics across three dimensions:
- **Breadth** — How many users are retained? (retention/churn)
- **Depth** — How much are retained users engaging? (usage, sessions, actions)
- **Monetization** — How much revenue is the product generating per user? (ARPU, LTV)

A product can look healthy on one dimension while deteriorating on another. Example: growing DAUs hiding collapsing session depth.

**4. North star metrics don't account for tradeoff metrics**
Some metrics move inversely — optimizing one harms another.
- Example: Inserting more ad spots in a feed increases ad revenue (monetization) but decreases engagement depth
- Example: Optimizing for new user signup rate by reducing friction can harm activation quality (users who sign up easily churn faster)
- Tradeoff metrics must be tracked together, not in isolation

## The Right Way to Set Growth Metrics (Balfour's 3-Step Framework)

**Step 1: Select a Constellation of Output Metrics**
Choose 3-5 output metrics that collectively capture all three dimensions of business health: breadth, depth, and monetization.

Examples by product type:
- **Collaboration tool (like Slack):** DAU (breadth) + messages sent per active user (depth) + paid team conversion rate (monetization)
- **B2B SaaS:** Active accounts (breadth) + key feature usage rate (depth) + net revenue retention (monetization)
- **Marketplace:** Active buyers AND sellers (breadth, both sides) + transaction frequency (depth) + GMV per user (monetization)

**Step 2: Break Output Metrics into Input Metrics**
For each output metric, drill down to the input metrics your team can directly influence.

Example (Spotify-style):
- Output metric: Total time listening
- Input metrics (layer 1): Repeat usage rate + time per session
- Input metrics (layer 2 — more actionable): Playlist creation rate + song skips + playlist follow rate

The goal: drill down until you reach metrics you can run experiments against. "Increase total time listening" is not an experiment — "increase playlist creation in first week" is.

**Step 3: Identify and Monitor Tradeoff Metrics**
For every metric you try to improve, ask: "What other metric might decline as a result?" Build monitoring for both sides of the tradeoff before running experiments.

## The Four Fits Framework (Related Engagement Concept)

From "Building a Growth Framework Towards a $100 Million Product" (fetched from brianbalfour.com):

Engagement doesn't exist in isolation — it depends on four interconnected "fits" all being aligned:

**1. Market ↔ Product Fit**
The right users are adopting the product. Balfour reframes this as "Market Product Fit" — starting with the market, not the product. The market defines the problem; the product solves it.

Indicator: Retention curves flatten (users keep coming back). A product without market-product fit shows a retention curve that decays to zero.

**2. Product ↔ Channel Fit**
Products are built to fit channels — channels don't mold to products. The properties of your product (viral, simple, enterprise, social) determine which acquisition channels will work.
- Simple, social, low-friction products → viral and paid acquisition
- Complex, high-value, enterprise products → content marketing and inside sales

**3. Channel ↔ Model Fit**
Your pricing model determines your allowable CAC, which determines your channel options.
- Low ARPU ($10/mo) → must use low-CAC channels (viral, SEO, paid with low CPAs)
- High ARPU ($10K/yr) → can afford high-CAC channels (inside sales, events, outbound)
- The "danger zone": ARPU too high for low-CAC channels but too low for high-CAC channels

**4. Model ↔ Market Fit**
Your monetization model needs to match the size and structure of your target market.
- Formula: ARPU × Achievable customers in market ≥ $100M (for VC-backed targets)
- If your ARPU is low, you need a massive market; if your ARPU is high, a smaller market can support a large business

## Key Quotes from Balfour's Public Writing

- "Blindly buying into the concept of the one metric that matters (OMTM) is a fatal oversimplification."
- "Output metrics help you set long-term goals. Input metrics represent the actions that influence the output metric. You can't focus exclusively on output metrics because they're not actionable."
- "A company is an ecosystem and your metrics are the various species that make up that ecosystem."
- "Products are built to fit with channels. Channels do not mold to products."
- "If you change one element of the Four Fits, you need to revisit and change the rest."

## The LinkedIn AI Growth Loop (Referenced in Balfour's Essays)

From his essay "A Breakdown of LinkedIn's AI Assisted Growth Loop" (brianbalfour.com):

This case study illustrates engagement loops in action:
- LinkedIn launched an AI feature (March timeframe) that generated AI-assisted content
- Within 3 months: 0 → 1M unique monthly visitors to the AI content
- The loop: users create AI content → content gets published on LinkedIn → content drives profile views → profile views create new users → new users create more content → loop continues

**Engagement loop pattern:**
1. User takes action (creates content)
2. Action creates artifact (published post)
3. Artifact attracts others (views, follows)
4. Others join the platform
5. New users create content → back to step 1

This is the same structure as viral loops, but accelerated by AI making content creation effortless.
