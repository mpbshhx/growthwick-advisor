# Don't Let Your North Star Metric Deceive You

By Brian Balfour, with Shaun Clowes (VP Product, Metromile) and Casey Winters (EIR, Greylock)

## The Problem With "One Metric That Matters" (OMTM)

We've all heard the rallying cry: choose your north star and focus. Grow 7% week over week. If you grow DAUs, the rest will follow.

Blindly buying into the concept of the One Metric That Matters (OMTM) is a fatal oversimplification.

Casey Winters (former Growth Lead at Pinterest and GrubHub): "The search for one key metric for a complex ecosystem like Pinterest over-simplifies how the ecosystem works and prevents anyone from focusing on understanding the different elements of that ecosystem."

---

## 4 Reasons OMTM Is Misleading

### 1. Good North Star Metrics Are Output Metrics — But You Need Input Metrics

**Output metrics** = results. Revenue, DAUs, WAUs, MRR.
**Input metrics** = the actions that influence outputs. Pageviews, registrations, free-to-paid upgrades.

Output metrics are important as a scoreboard — but you can't build experiments against them. They're too broad and not actionable. To win the game, you need to focus on the individual plays.

**Example — Spotify:**
- Output metric: Total time spent listening
- Problem: "Increase total time spent" isn't actionable
- Solution: Break it into inputs — bring users back more often (retention/reactivation) AND increase session length when they are there
- Then: Break those into even more granular inputs → build experiments against those

### 2. Output Metrics Are Lagging Indicators

By the time a problem surfaces in your north star metric, the damage is done. Inputs are leading indicators.

**B2B SaaS example (MRR as north star):**
"Revenue retention is the output of engaged users. The usage is the input. Revenue can hide what is going on under the hood with product usage. If you're trying to improve retention but only tracking revenue retention, the game is over before you've had a chance to play. Once a paying subscriber has churned, winning them back is almost impossible."

**Analogy — The Serengeti Ecosystem:**
If you're an ecologist tracking the health of the Serengeti and you focus only on lion population (your north star), you might miss that the wildebeest population (an input) is collapsing. By the time the lions start dying, it's too late to intervene.

### 3. A Single Metric Only Captures One Dimension of Your Business

Three key buckets that every product must measure at minimum:
- **Breadth of retention** (are users coming back?)
- **Depth of engagement** (are they getting real value when they do?)
- **Monetization** (are they paying, and expanding?)

Shaun Clowes (VP Product, Metromile / ex-Head of Growth at Atlassian): "There's always a constellation of input metrics that we swap in and out under the umbrella of our output metrics, based on what we're learning at the time."

**Slack example:**
- North star: DAU → captures retention, but says nothing about engagement depth or monetization
- DAU could be growing while monetization is stagnant

**HubSpot CRM example:**
- North star: Weekly Active Teams → captures retention, but not engagement depth or revenue

### 4. North Star Metrics Don't Account for Tradeoffs Between Metrics

**Pinterest "WARC" example:**
Pinterest tried to combine repinning + clicking into one metric (Weekly Active Repinner or Clicker = WARC).

Problem 1: Ignored the supply side — no team wanted to create new content when there was tried-and-true content that drove repins and clicks. This created content recycling and staleness.

Problem 2: "False rigor" — you can run an experiment that increases WARCs by trading repins for clicks (or vice versa) without realizing it, because the combined metric went up.

**LinkedIn ad revenue example:**
If your north star is ad revenue per user, you might increase ad slots in the news feed. This could increase ad revenue short-term while destroying retention and engagement long-term — but you won't see it until users churn months later.

---

## The Right Way to Set Growth Metrics

### Step 1: Select a Constellation of Metrics

Instead of one north star, choose a *constellation* of key output metrics that together capture:
- Retention (breadth)
- Engagement (depth)
- Monetization

Monitor the full scoreboard, not just one number.

### Step 2: Break Output Metrics Into Input Metrics

For each output metric, drill down until you have a set of actionable input metrics you can directly influence through experiments.

Be willing to discard inputs quickly if experiments don't move the output. The inputs that *do* move the output = your true leading indicators.

### Step 3: Understand and Monitor Tradeoff Metrics

For every metric you try to improve, identify where in the business you're likely to see a counter-reaction. Find the healthy balance between opposing metrics.

---

## Key Principle

"At the end of the day, this constellation of metrics reflects the health of your business, whether you track it or not. It's good to simplify and focus as much as you can, but remember you're never going to find a silver bullet metric."

Zoom in when experimenting. Zoom out when reviewing business health. Never confuse the two.
