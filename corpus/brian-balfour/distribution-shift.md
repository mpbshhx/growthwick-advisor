# The Next Great Distribution Shift

By Brian Balfour | blog.brianbalfour.com

---

## The Core Thesis

We're living through one of the most significant technology shifts in history, yet we're still distributing products like it's 2015.

The AI revolution has transformed how we build products, but there's a glaring problem — this technology shift has come without a distribution shift. In fact, AI is actively destroying the distribution channels we've relied on for decades:
- SEO traffic is plummeting as users shift to answer engine platforms
- Social algorithms are more unpredictable than ever
- The old playbooks are burning

But the next great distribution shift is coming, and it's going to reshape how products find users just as dramatically as search engines and app stores did before it.

---

## The Sequenced Pattern of Platform Shifts

Casey Winters (Dec 2023 essay "On Platform Shifts and AI") made the key distinction: what separates a major platform shift from a minor one isn't the technology itself — it's whether that shift enables **both new ways of building things AND new ways of reaching people**.

**Critical insight:** The technological and distribution shifts do NOT happen at the same time.

- **Internet:** Created websites in early 1990s. Google didn't emerge as dominant distribution until 1998. That's ~a decade between technology and distribution.
- **Mobile:** Apps launched with iPhone in 2008. The real distribution shift came 4 years later when Facebook launched mobile ads in 2012. Companies like King (Candy Crush) and Supercell (Clash of Clans) exploded.
- **AI:** Tech shift started gaining mainstream adoption late 2022 with ChatGPT. Following mobile timeline, we wouldn't expect distribution shift until ~2026-2027.

**But Brian believes it will happen faster this time.**

## The Three-Step Platform Playbook

Every major distribution platform in history has followed the same cycle:

### Step 1: Identify the Moat
Figure out what will make the platform unassailable.
- Facebook: the social graph (who knows whom)
- Google: search data (what people want)
- Apple: application ecosystem to attract premium device owners

### Step 2: Open the Gates
Create "open" ecosystems — free API access, viral growth mechanics, revenue sharing that seems too good to be true.

Why? The platform **needs you**. Your apps, content, data, and innovations feed the moat. Every developer who builds on the platform makes it stronger.

### Step 3: Close for Monetization
Once the moat achieves escape velocity, the rules change. What was free becomes paid. What was permitted becomes restricted. Platform competes with its own developers.

Three ways this happens systematically:
1. Build first-party versions of most popular third-party applications
2. Tax directly (take % of revenue)
3. Tax indirectly (reduce organic reach to push toward paid advertising)

**The timing is unpredictable but accelerating:** Facebook took ~2 years, Apple took ~4, Google stretched over 2 decades. In AI, players will be more ruthless because the stakes are larger.

## Platform Case Studies

### Facebook (2007-2012): The Template

**The problem:** Facebook had 25M users vs. MySpace at 107M, hi5 at 70M, Friendster at 50M.

**The moat:** Social graph — network of who knows whom. Classic direct network effect.

**Opening the gates (May 2007, f8 launch):**
- Canvas to build anything on the platform
- Keep 100% of app revenue
- Use viral channels freely
- Facebook only monetizes sidebar ads

**Results:** 7,000 apps by late 2007, 100 new apps/day. 33,000 apps and 400,000 developers by mid-2008. Facebook grew from 25M to 250M users in 2 years.

**The close:**
- 2009-2010: Viral channels disappear (notification limits, throttled invite systems)
- 2010-2011: Mandatory Facebook Credits, 30% transaction tax
- 2011-2012: Feature absorption — native Photos, Events, Groups, Location, Marketplace launched. BandPage lost 90% traffic overnight (35M → 3M users).

Mark Zuckerberg at f8: "Until now, social networks have been closed platforms. Today, we're going to end that." — the irony only became clear years later.

### Apple App Store (2008-Present)

**The problem:** In 2008, Apple was competing against Nokia (40% global share), BlackBerry (enterprise standard), Windows Mobile, and the just-launching Android.

**The moat:** Ecosystem — if the iPhone had the best apps you couldn't get elsewhere, switching costs become massive.

**Opening the gates:**
- 70/30 revenue split (developers keep 70%)
- Free app hosting and distribution
- Seamless payment processing, global reach

**Results:** "There's an app for that" became cultural phenomenon. By 2010: 225,000 apps, $2.5B in revenue generated.

**The close (slower than Facebook):**
- 2011: In-App Purchase Mandate — all digital content via Apple's payment system, 30% commission. Amazon Kindle forced to remove in-app store.
- 2012-2015: Restrictions on apps duplicating core functionality, Search Ads launched
- 2016-Present: App Tracking Transparency decimates ad-based models. Apple builds features killing popular apps. Today generating $100B+ annually from the 30% tax.

### Google Search (2000s-Present)

**The moat:** Data and content — circular flywheel: more searches → more data → better results → more searches.

**Opening the gates:** "We want to get you out of Google and to the right place as fast as possible." (Larry Page, 2004). SEO worked. Ranking #1 was the holy grail. No penalty for linking out.

**The close:**
- Phase 1 (2010-2015): Ads moved from sidebar to above organic results. Keyword data killed ("not provided").
- Phase 2 (2015-2020): Featured snippets, Knowledge Graph, "People Also Ask" — Google answers without clicks.
- Phase 3 (2020-Present): The Markup investigation showed 41% of mobile first screen is Google products, 63% of visible first page is Google-controlled.

Today: Travel queries go to Google Flights, product searches to Google Shopping, local to Google Maps. Companies that spent decades building SEO businesses watched traffic evaporate.

### LinkedIn (2020-Present): The Fastest Cycle Yet

**The moat:** Professional data — not just who you are, but what you do, care about, and how you behave professionally. Gold for marketers, recruiters, and salespeople.

**The problem:** LinkedIn was a static resume database. Needed dynamic behavioral data.

**Opening the gates (~2020):** Prioritized "creator" content in feed. Extraordinary organic reach. Creator Mode, newsletter functionality, live video. Some creators got 10,000+ impressions on posts that would get 100 elsewhere.

**The close (less than 4 years):**
- Late 2022: Declining reach begins. Social agencies report 50-75% decrease.
- June 2023: Thought Leader Ads launch — companies can sponsor organic posts from individuals, turning executives into ad inventory.

LinkedIn's formula:
- Create artificial scarcity (make organic reach feel special)
- Build creator dependence (encourage abandoning other channels)
- Flip the switch (once critical mass, monetize aggressively)
- Gaslight the community (claim algorithm changes are about "quality")

## Who's Next: ChatGPT as the Next Distribution Platform

**Brian's prediction:** ChatGPT will be the next distribution platform. Likely within 6 months of writing (and possibly faster).

**Why ChatGPT despite 700M users not yet being a distribution channel?**

ChatGPT's moat will be **context and memory** — not model quality (which changes monthly):

- An AI that knows you've been working on a product launch for 3 months, references previous discussions, remembers your preferences = infinitely more valuable than one starting from scratch
- Context is not portable via MCP. Memory is not portable. That's where the real lock-in lives.

**Early signals OpenAI is about to open the gates:**
- Recently launched connectors (Deep Research) to HubSpot, Box, GitHub
- Actively soliciting more platform integrations (MCP connector interest form)
- Hiring aggressively for Agent Platform/Infrastructure team: "building the platform and integrations to launch new agents to hundreds of millions of users worldwide"

**Why ChatGPT over Claude, Google Gemini, or Meta?**

The god metric is retention. ChatGPT has:
- Highest mean customer retention vs. all competitors
- Retention curves shifting UP over time
- The rare "smile curve" of retention (only other examples: Facebook, LinkedIn, Slack)

"It does not matter that Google/Meta have access to larger distribution if the retention & engagement disparity is this large."

## Counter-Arguments

**MCP Makes Context Portable:** Theory says yes. Reality says developers are resource-constrained and focus on the largest platform first. ChatGPT has 20X Claude's user base and growing faster. MCP only addresses context portability, not memory.

**Microsoft/Google/Apple Have More Distribution:** Remember — Facebook wasn't the largest social network. Apple wasn't the market leader. What matters is engagement depth, platform readiness, and trajectory. Depth beats breadth in platform dynamics.

**Regulation:** The wild card. Data privacy laws, AI safety regulations, antitrust enforcement could all derail. But betting against technology platforms finding ways around regulation has historically been a losing proposition.

## What You Should Do Now

1. **Understand where you are in the platform lifecycle.** Are the platforms you rely on in Step 2 (open) or Step 3 (closing)?

2. **Capture value during Step 2 while preparing for Step 3.** Extract while the window is open.

3. **Don't build your entire business on any single platform.** The walls always go up eventually.

4. **Watch ChatGPT's integration moves closely.** The window between Step 2 and Step 3 is the opportunity.

5. **Build for the context era.** Products that help ChatGPT accumulate user context will have leverage in the early platform phase.
