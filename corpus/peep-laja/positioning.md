## What is Positioning?

Positioning is the act of defining how your product/company occupies a specific place in the mind of your target customer — relative to alternatives.

**Peep's definition**: Positioning is the answer to "Why should I buy from you instead of [competitor]?" for a specific type of customer.

It's not a tagline. It's not a mission statement. It's a strategic decision about:
- WHO you serve (and who you don't)
- WHAT problem you solve
- HOW you're different from alternatives
- WHY those differences matter to that specific audience

## The April Dunford Framework (Peep Endorses)

Peep frequently references and teaches April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" positioning framework:

### The 5 Components of Positioning
1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers do if your product didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — What features/capabilities do you have that alternatives lack?
3. **Value that those attributes enable** — So what? What does the unique feature actually deliver?
4. **Target market** — Who specifically cares most about this value?
5. **Market category** — What "box" do customers put you in?

### Why Market Category Matters
The category you choose frames everything:
- It sets expectations
- It determines who you're compared against
- It shapes perceived pricing
- It affects discoverability

Choosing the wrong category is one of the most expensive mistakes a company can make.

## Common Positioning Mistakes (Peep's List)

### Mistake 1: Internal Positioning
Positioning created by the marketing team in a vacuum, without input from:
- Sales (what objections come up?)
- Customer success (why do customers stay or leave?)
- Actual customers (why did they choose you?)

### Mistake 2: Feature Positioning
Listing features and calling it positioning. Features are attributes; positioning is the meaning of those attributes for a specific buyer.

### Mistake 3: Claiming Every Benefit
Trying to appeal to everyone dilutes appeal to anyone. Effective positioning is often about what you're NOT, not just what you are.

### Mistake 4: Aspirational Positioning
Positioning to where you want to be, not where you actually are today. This destroys trust when customers discover the gap.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring the Competitive Context
Position doesn't exist in a vacuum — it exists relative to alternatives. Ignoring what competitors claim leads to undifferentiated positioning.

## The Positioning Process

**Step 1: Map competitive alternatives**
- Ask customers: "What would you use if we didn't exist?"
- Include non-obvious alternatives (spreadsheets, manual processes, do nothing)

**Step 2: Isolate genuine differentiators**
- What do you have that alternatives don't?
- Run win-loss analysis to confirm what actually matters

**Step 3: Map to buyer value**
- For each differentiator: why does it matter to buyers?
- What outcome does it enable? What pain does it remove?

**Step 4: Identify the highest-value segment**
- Who values your differentiators MOST?
- This is your beachhead market

**Step 5: Choose your category frame**
- What category makes your differentiation most obvious?
- Sometimes creating a new category is the right move

**Step 6: Test the positioning**
- Use Wynter (Peep's tool) to test messaging with actual buyers
- Does it resonate? Does it feel different? Does it create desire?

## Positioning and Messaging: The Distinction

**Positioning** = strategic (internal document, guides all communication)
**Messaging** = tactical (the words you actually use on the website, in ads, in sales)

Most companies confuse them. They write messaging without positioning, which is why messaging feels generic.

**Peep's flow**: Strong positioning → clear messaging → resonant copy → better conversion
