# Win-Loss Analysis — Peep Laja / CXL

## Why Win-Loss Analysis is Underused

It's the highest signal research a B2B company can do. Most companies don't do it because:
- It feels uncomfortable (especially loss interviews)
- Sales teams are protective of their deal data
- Marketing teams assume they know why they win/lose
- Nobody "owns" it organizationally

The result: companies make positioning, messaging, and product decisions based on guesses, not evidence.

## The Core Insight: The Decision Is Made Before Sales Talks to Them

Buyers do most of their research before ever talking to a vendor. By the time a prospect enters your pipeline, they likely have a mental shortlist and a pre-formed impression of each vendor.

This means:
- Your marketing IS your sales pitch for most of the buyer journey
- Your messaging either works or doesn't before a rep ever picks up the phone
- Win-loss analysis reveals problems that are pre-pipeline, not just in-pipeline

## Who to Interview

**Wins (recent customers, 30-90 days post-purchase):**
- Why they bought while memory is fresh
- What almost stopped them
- What surprised them

**Losses (recent prospects who chose a competitor or did nothing):**
- What happened in their evaluation
- Who else they considered and why they won
- What your company could have done differently

**Churned customers (bonus):**
- Why they left — this is the deepest signal about product-market fit gaps

**Ratio:** Interview more losses than wins. Wins confirm what's working. Losses reveal what to fix.

## The Interview Framework

### Setup
- Not sales. Not customer success. Ideally an independent researcher or a neutral third party.
- Buyers speak more honestly when they're not talking to someone who has a stake in the outcome.
- 30-45 minutes. Video preferred (you want to see facial expressions).
- Always record (with permission).

### Opening Questions (Context)
- What triggered your search for a solution like ours?
- How long were you evaluating?
- Who was involved in the decision?
- What were your must-haves?

### Evaluation Questions (The Meat)
- Walk me through how you evaluated your options.
- What were you looking for in the initial research phase?
- At what point did we enter your consideration set?
- What was your impression of us vs. alternatives?
- What were the key decision criteria you used?

### Decision Questions (The Signal)
- What was the final deciding factor?
- Was there a moment when the decision became clear?
- What would have changed the outcome?
- What did [winning competitor] do better than we did?

### Post-Decision Questions (The Honest Take)
- Looking back, was it the right decision?
- What's working well / not working?
- What would you tell a colleague who's evaluating us?

## Patterns to Look For

After 10-15 interviews, patterns emerge. Specifically:

**In wins:**
- Which message/claim resonated most?
- What was the "aha moment" that made us the obvious choice?
- Which competitor were we most often beating and why?

**In losses:**
- Which competitor won and what was their pitch?
- Was it product, pricing, process, or relationship?
- What objections did we fail to overcome?
- Was there a specific feature/capability gap?

**Across both:**
- How do buyers describe their problem? (Use this language in your messaging)
- What criteria do buyers use that you're not speaking to?
- What's the buying committee composition? (Who has veto power?)

## What to Do With the Data

### Immediate (Days)
- Fix the messaging gaps — if buyers consistently misunderstand what you do, fix the homepage
- Arm sales with the "loss narratives" — what objections come up and how to handle them
- Share the win patterns — what's working and why

### Short-Term (Weeks)
- Update your ICP definition based on win patterns
- Prioritize roadmap items that came up repeatedly in losses
- Create competitive battle cards based on what competitors are saying and doing

### Ongoing (Months)
- Make win-loss analysis continuous, not a one-time project
- Build it into the post-deal process: every closed-lost triggers an interview request
- Track trends over time — are you winning/losing on the same things? Are things improving?

## Common Mistakes

1. **Only interviewing wins** — self-congratulatory and low signal. The losses tell you more.
2. **Having sales do the interviews** — buyers won't be honest with the person they just turned down.
3. **Treating it as a sales post-mortem** — win-loss analysis is a marketing and product research tool, not a sales coaching tool.
4. **One-time project** — patterns shift as your market evolves. Make it continuous.
5. **Not sharing the insights** — the data is worthless if it sits in a report. Insights need to reach product, marketing, and sales.
