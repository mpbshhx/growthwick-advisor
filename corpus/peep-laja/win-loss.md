## Why Win-Loss Analysis Matters

Most companies have no idea why they actually win or lose deals. They rely on:
- Salespeople's self-serving explanations ("we lost because of price")
- CRM data that's incomplete and biased
- Internal speculation

**The reality**: Your sales team's explanations for losses are wrong ~50% of the time. Buyers give polite rejections ("budget," "timing") that obscure real reasons.

**Peep's core belief**: Win-loss analysis is the highest-signal input for positioning, messaging, and competitive strategy. It tells you what's actually happening in the market — not what you wish were happening.

## What Win-Loss Analysis Reveals

1. **True buying criteria** — What buyers actually care about (often different from what you think)
2. **Competitor strengths and weaknesses** — What buyers say competitors do better/worse
3. **Messaging gaps** — Where your value proposition isn't landing
4. **Sales process issues** — Where deals break down and why
5. **Positioning blind spots** — How buyers perceive you vs. how you see yourself
6. **ICP validation** — Whether you're winning in the segments you want to win in

## How to Run Win-Loss Analysis

### Step 1: Who to Interview
- Recent WINS (last 30-90 days)
- Recent LOSSES (last 30-90 days)  
- Churned customers (why did they leave?)

**Sample size**: 10-15 interviews per quarter to see reliable patterns

### Step 2: Who Should Conduct the Interviews
- **NOT the salesperson** who worked the deal (buyers won't be honest)
- **NOT the account executive** (same reason)
- Best options: Customer success, marketing, or a third-party firm
- Independence = honesty

### Step 3: Key Questions (Peep's Framework)

**For WINS:**
- Why did you choose us over alternatives?
- What alternatives did you seriously consider?
- What were your biggest concerns before buying?
- What tipped the decision in our favor?
- What did we do better than others?

**For LOSSES:**
- What alternatives did you choose and why?
- What was the deciding factor?
- What did the winner do better than us?
- What could we have done differently?
- What would need to change for you to reconsider us?

**For CHURNS:**
- What prompted you to leave?
- What were you hoping we'd be that we weren't?
- What is the solution doing better than us?

### Step 4: Analyze for Patterns
After 10-15 interviews:
- Theme clustering — what comes up repeatedly?
- Win patterns — what do wins have in common?
- Loss patterns — what's consistently the problem?
- Competitive patterns — what are each competitor's perceived strengths/weaknesses?

### Step 5: Feed Insights Into Strategy
Win-loss analysis should directly update:
- Positioning and messaging (if buyers don't understand your value)
- Sales enablement (common objections and how to handle them)
- Product roadmap (features consistently requested by churned customers)
- ICP definition (if wins cluster in unexpected segments)

## Common Win-Loss Findings (from Peep's experience)

**"We lost to price"** → Usually not the full story. Often means: value wasn't clearly communicated, buyer didn't trust your results, or competitor's packaging was clearer.

**"We lost because of missing features"** → Often means: the features were there but not positioned correctly, OR the real problem was onboarding/ease of use.

**"We win because of our support"** → Often the real differentiator even when the company doesn't lead with it in marketing.

**"We lose because buyers don't understand us"** → Positioning and messaging problem, not a product problem.

## Win-Loss at Scale

As companies grow, win-loss becomes formalized:
- Quarterly cadence of structured interviews
- Dedicated function (competitive intelligence or product marketing)
- Tools: Klue, Crayon, Kompyte for competitive tracking; Wynter for message testing

**Peep's view**: Any B2B company doing >$1M ARR should be doing systematic win-loss analysis. The companies that grow fastest are usually the ones that listen hardest.
