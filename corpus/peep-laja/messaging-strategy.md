# Messaging Strategy — Peep Laja / CXL + Wynter

## What Messaging Is (And Isn't)

**Messaging ≠ Positioning.** They are related but distinct.

- **Positioning** = the strategic choice of where you play and how you win (internal decision)
- **Messaging** = how you communicate that positioning to specific audiences (external execution)

You can have strong positioning and weak messaging. You can have strong messaging and weak positioning. Both need to work.

**Messaging ≠ Copy.** Copy is the execution of messaging in specific formats (ads, landing pages, emails). Messaging is the framework that guides all copy.

## The Core Problem with Most B2B Messaging

Most B2B messaging is written from the company's perspective, not the buyer's:
- Uses internal vocabulary, not buyer vocabulary
- Describes product capabilities, not buyer outcomes
- Leads with features, not problems solved
- Generic enough to fit any competitor in the space

**The test:** Could your top 3 competitors use your homepage headline? If yes → your messaging is not differentiated.

## The Buyer Language Principle (Peep's Core Teaching)

Your messaging should use the EXACT words your buyers use to describe their problem. Not cleaned-up, polished versions. The actual words.

Why this matters:
- Buyers self-select when they hear their own language
- It signals: "these people get us"
- It creates immediate resonance that generic messaging can't
- It dramatically improves conversion rates

**How to get buyer language:**
1. Record sales calls — what words do prospects use to describe their problem?
2. Read customer reviews (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot)
- The review language is buyer language
- Mining reviews for exact phrases is one of the highest-leverage research activities
3. Run win-loss interviews — ask "how would you describe the problem you were trying to solve?"
4. Use Wynter (Peep's company) — test your messaging directly against your ICP panels

## The Messaging Hierarchy

Every company needs a messaging hierarchy that cascades from top-level to execution:

### Level 1: Core Value Proposition
One sentence. The most important thing your company does for your best customer.

Format: "We help [ICP] [achieve outcome] [without/by] [key differentiator]."

### Level 2: Supporting Claims (3-5)
The specific proof points that make your value proposition credible.

Each supporting claim should:
- Be specific (numbers, outcomes, mechanisms)
- Be differentiating (something competitors can't easily claim)
- Connect to the value proposition

### Level 3: Proof
Evidence for each supporting claim:
- Customer stories (with specifics: "Company X reduced [metric] by Y% in Z weeks")
- Data from your product
- Third-party validation (awards, analyst recognition, review ratings)

### Level 4: Channel-Specific Adaptation
The core message adapted for specific contexts:
- Landing pages (more detail, more proof)
- Ads (extreme brevity, single hook)
- Sales decks (buyer journey context, objection handling)
- Email sequences (nurture, education, CTA progression)

## Wynter: Peep's Approach to Message Testing

Peep built Wynter specifically because B2B messaging is almost never validated before launch.

**The problem Wynter solves:** "We think this message will resonate. We're not sure. Let's launch and see." This is backwards.

**Wynter's approach:**
- Build a panel of your exact ICP (job title, industry, company size)
- Show them your messaging for 7 minutes
- Collect structured feedback: what's confusing? What resonates? What do they doubt? What made them want to learn more?
- Get results in 48 hours

The insight: **buyers can tell you exactly what's wrong with your messaging**. Most companies never ask.

## Common Messaging Failures

### 1. The "We're the Leading/Best/Most Powerful" Trap
Every company claims to be the leader. Buyers tune it out. It's noise. Leading → proof. Best → specific outcome. Most powerful → what problem does that power solve?

### 2. Category Descriptor Overload
"AI-powered" "cloud-native" "enterprise-grade" — these are table stakes descriptors, not differentiators. Remove them or make them specific.

### 3. Feature-Forward, Outcome-Backward
Leading with features makes buyers do the work of connecting features to their outcomes. They won't. You have to do it for them.

### 4. ICP Mismatch
Messaging written for your broadest possible audience will resonate with nobody. Narrow to your ICP. The message that converts your best customers will attract more of your best customers.

### 5. Jargon-Only Messaging
If your homepage requires a PhD in your industry to understand → you're writing for internal stakeholders, not buyers.

## The Messaging Audit (Quick Version)

Test your homepage against these questions:
1. Does it immediately communicate who this is for?
2. Does it communicate the primary outcome they'll get?
3. Is there anything here a competitor could copy word-for-word?
4. Are there specific claims (numbers, outcomes, mechanisms) or only generic assertions?
5. Does it use your buyer's vocabulary or your internal vocabulary?

Score 1 per "yes." 4-5 = solid. 2-3 = needs work. 0-1 = start over.
