# How to Rebuild Your Marketing Team for the Agentic Age — Kieran Flanagan

*Published May 29, 2026*

## The Core Mistake

Most marketing leaders are trying to **fit AI into their existing org** — new AI tools and agents, same old team boxes. This means bolting AI onto a structure designed to coordinate people.

The mistake: If you integrate AI into how a team works, you'll get minimal results. You have to **rethink how the team works, how it's structured, and what its core skills are**.

The org chart stops being a tree of functions. **It becomes a system that produces output.** A system needs a different shape than a hierarchy.

---

## The Model: 4 Layers

This isn't about replacing marketers with agents. AI should remove the **coordination tax** — status meetings, handoffs, "circling back" — and concentrate humans at the points where they actually add value.

Fewer people coordinating people. More marketers directing systems, agents, and practicing their craft.

Every layer reads from the layer below it, and every layer writes its learnings back into it. **It's a loop, not a ladder.**

---

## Layer 1: Context (The Intelligence Layer)

**The biggest unlock available to you right now — and the one almost nobody has built.**

Pre-AI, your intelligence lived in people's heads: what the market wants, how a campaign really gets shipped, which plays work. When someone left, it left with them.

**Context** is a system that captures that intelligence — market knowledge, how the team operates, what's working, what isn't — and makes it readable by every human and every agent above it. It updates automatically from the work you do.

### Key notes:
- **This is real work** — real data engineering: CRM, analytics, support tickets, Slack, docs, call transcripts, brand guidelines, stitched into something queryable. This is infrastructure, not a wiki.
- **Someone has to own it** — or it goes stale. Named owner: AI Ops or RevOps, with senior strategists curating what's true.
- **Has to be deterministic, not vibes** — if the same question returns different answers on different days, you don't have an intelligence layer; you have a slot machine.
- **Don't ingest everything** — start with your 3–5 best artifacts. Lean, trusted core beats a giant pile of noise.
- **Full version includes the whole company** — customer knowledge lives in sales and CS, product owns the roadmap, finance owns unit economics. Context is an enterprise asset, not a marketing corner project.

---

## Layer 2: Execution (Craftspeople + Agents)

**Where the work gets made.** A mix of agents running in parallel and genuine craftspeople.

Why "craftspeople" not "marketers"? Because **good enough won't cut it when everyone has the same models**. AI can draft most of a thing. The gap between forgettable and unmissable is taste, judgment, and real understanding of the customer — and that gap is widening.

### Key notes:
- **Humans own the brief and the standard** — agents do the volume in the middle. Put human judgment at the start and the end, not just the end.
- **Some work doesn't compress at all** — distribution through events, partnerships, influencers, community, the founder's brand. Physical touchpoints, activations. This work can't be automated.
- **Agents augment, not replace** — humans determine the prompts, set guardrails, and enrich the work.

---

## Layer 3: Orchestration (Running the System)

Someone has to run the system: make routing decisions, monitor what agents produce, ensure they're reading the right context, feed results back into Layer 1.

This is the **newest and most important role to get right**.

Likely two sub-roles:
- **Orchestrator** — designs the system, decides what flows where, which decisions stay human, which get agent-drafted for approval, which run autonomously
- **Operator** — lives in the system day-to-day, QAs agents, improves them

**The hard part of orchestration:** Delegation logic. What's allowed to run on its own? What gets escalated? What never leaves a human's hands? Deliberate design choice, not a default.

Who does this? RevOps lead, a GTM product manager from MarketingOps, or an AI generalist.

---

## Layer 4: Leadership (Human-Only Layer)

**The new leadership job:** Set direction for a system, refine it, and get output from it. You stop coordinating people and start steering an engine.

You decide what the system should optimize toward, make the judgment calls it can't, and feed strategy back into Context so the whole thing inherits your point of view.

**Why human-only?** Not because leaders shouldn't use agents (they absolutely should). But someone has to own the taste the system optimizes toward and carry the judgment calls when the model is confidently wrong. **You can automate the work. You can't automate accountability for it.**

### Hard question: Is the current CMO equipped to lead this?
Only ~15% of CEOs believe their CMO is AI-savvy. Most CMOs were selected for their ability to coordinate people and manage org charts. This model asks them to direct systems instead — a genuinely different skill.

For some, it's learnable. For some, it's a hiring reset.

---

## Critical Questions

**"Is this actually new — or is it just good marketing with better tools?"**

A lot of this is what great marketing has always demanded: clear thinking, real craft, tight feedback loops. What's genuinely new is the substrate underneath it — a shared intelligence layer that **survives turnover** was simply not possible before. Today, the most intelligent marketer makes the whole team smarter just by working.

**"Where do entry-level marketers learn the craft if the middle concentrates?"**

The entry path shifts from "do the task" to "operate the system and build the context." IC fluency with AI has to come before you redesign the org, not after. Anyone who says this is solved is guessing — it's one of the hard AI problems.
