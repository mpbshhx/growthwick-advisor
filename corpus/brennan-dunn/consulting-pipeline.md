# Brennan Dunn: Building a Consulting Pipeline

*Source: Brennan Dunn — Double Your Freelancing, RightMessage, and associated writing/courses*

---

## The Core Premise

Most consultants fail at business development because they treat it as a separate activity from their work. Brennan Dunn's framework treats pipeline-building as a system — something that runs continuously in the background, not something you scramble to do between projects. The goal is to never be in a position where you need the next client. You want to be choosing clients, not chasing them.

---

## Warm Outreach vs. Cold Outreach

Dunn's strong bias is toward warm outreach, and the distinction matters more than most consultants realize.

**Cold outreach** means contacting people who have no prior relationship with you — no referral, no content touchpoint, no mutual connection. It can work, but the conversion rates are brutal and it positions you as a vendor competing on price from the opening message. Cold outreach is a volume game that independent consultants almost never win against agencies or sales teams.

**Warm outreach** means reaching people who already know you exist and have some reason to trust you. This includes:
- Past clients you've done great work for
- People who've been on your email list for months
- People referred by someone they already trust
- Conference attendees who heard you speak
- Readers who've engaged with your content

The asymmetry is stark. A cold email to 100 strangers might yield 1-2 conversations. A warm email to 20 engaged list subscribers might yield 5-8. The math is not even close. Dunn builds his entire system around manufacturing warm relationships at scale before a prospect ever needs to hire someone.

## The Email List as a Consulting Asset

This is the centerpiece of Dunn's model. An email list of the right people — owners, operators, decision-makers in your niche — is worth more than any ad spend or cold outreach campaign.

The mechanism: when someone is ready to hire a consultant, they think of who they already trust. If you've been in their inbox every two weeks with genuinely useful thinking, you're already on the shortlist before the RFP even exists.

Building the list requires:
1. **A specific audience** — not "business owners," but "e-commerce founders doing $1M–$10M in revenue"
2. **Consistent, valuable content** — Dunn advocates for deeply personal, opinionated emails, not generic tips
3. **A clear reason to subscribe** — usually a lead magnet that signals your area of expertise

Dunn also emphasizes segmentation. Not everyone on your list is at the same buying stage. RightMessage (his SaaS product) was built to solve exactly this — showing different content and CTAs to different segments. At minimum, tag subscribers by their role, company size, and where they are in the awareness journey.

## Productized Lead Magnets

A lead magnet for a consultant shouldn't be a PDF checklist. It should demonstrate the value of working with you by delivering a version of that value for free.

Dunn's preferred formats:
- **Free audit** — a 15–30 minute review of their current situation (pricing, funnel, email sequence, whatever your specialty is) with specific observations. This creates a natural conversation starter because the next step is "want help implementing this?"
- **Diagnostic quiz or assessment** — asks the prospect about their situation, delivers a personalized score or recommendation, and segments them simultaneously
- **Templates with embedded thinking** — not blank templates, but pre-filled frameworks that show how you think. The template itself becomes a demonstration of your methodology.

The key is that a good lead magnet pre-sells your approach. By the time a prospect finishes it, they should think: "This person understands my problem. I wonder if they work with people like me."

## Referral Systems

Referrals are the highest-conversion lead source for consultants. Dunn's framework for making them systematic rather than accidental:

1. **Deliver a remarkable result, then name it.** Don't just do great work — articulate what you did in a sentence your client can repeat. "He helped us cut our sales cycle from 6 weeks to 10 days." That sentence travels.

2. **Ask at the right moment.** The best time to ask for a referral is immediately after a win — when the client has just seen results and goodwill is highest. Not at the end of the engagement.

3. **Make the ask specific.** "Do you know anyone who might need my help?" is too broad. "Do you know any other SaaS founders who struggle with email onboarding sequences?" is actionable. The specificity helps them match you to someone real.

4. **Build a referral partner network.** Identify adjacent service providers who serve the same clients but don't compete. A copywriter and a web designer serve the same clients. Build a genuine reciprocal referral relationship — not a formal affiliate arrangement, just mutual trust and awareness.

## Turning Prospects Into Clients

Dunn's conversion framework centers on what he calls the "roadmapping session" or "strategy session" — a paid diagnostic engagement that sits between "interested prospect" and "full engagement."

The logic: prospects are scared to commit to a large project with someone they don't fully trust yet. A small, low-risk paid engagement lets them experience working with you before committing to a large one. The roadmapping session (typically $500–$2,500) delivers a concrete strategy document and naturally leads to: "Here's what I'd recommend doing next, and here's how I can help."

Conversion from roadmap to full engagement is typically 60–80% for consultants who execute this well. The session also filters out tire-kickers — anyone unwilling to pay $1,000 for a strategy session is unlikely to pay $15,000 for an implementation project.

**The funnel in summary:**
1. Content / speaking / SEO → list subscriber
2. Lead magnet → segmented prospect
3. Email nurture → warm relationship
4. Referral or direct inquiry → consultation call
5. Roadmapping session → full engagement

---

## Key Principle

The entire system is designed so that by the time a prospect gets on a call with you, they already believe you can help them. The call exists to confirm fit and scope — not to convince them you're credible. That work has already been done.
