# Value-Based Pricing: Charge What Your Work Is Worth
**Source: Brennan Dunn / Double Your Freelancing**
*doubleyourfreelancing.com/rate/ | doubleyourfreelancing.com*

---

## The Core Principle

Value-based pricing is the practice of setting your price based on the *economic value your work delivers to the client* — not the number of hours it takes you to do it.

Brennan Dunn's Double Your Freelancing Rate methodology is built entirely on this premise. In his own words:

> "This service I'm going to provide for you is clearly going to make you oodles of money in the coming years, and I'm charging you a small fraction of the financial benefit that my service provides."

That framing — "a small fraction of the financial benefit" — is the entire value-pricing formula. The price is anchored to the client's gain, not your cost.

---

## Why Hourly Billing Is a Trap

Hourly billing is intuitive but structurally broken for knowledge workers.

**Problem 1: It rewards slowness.** The longer you take, the more you earn. The more efficient you get, the less you make. Your expertise works against you.

**Problem 2: It commoditizes you.** When clients compare hourly rates, they're comparing costs, not outcomes. You become a line item, not a partner.

**Problem 3: It caps your earning ceiling.** There are only so many hours in a week. Hourly billing means your income has a hard ceiling no matter how good you get.

**Problem 4: It misaligns incentives.** The client wants it done fast and cheap. You're (theoretically) paid to take time. This creates an adversarial dynamic.

Value-based pricing breaks all four of these problems simultaneously.

## The Value Calculation Process

Dunn teaches a specific process for calculating a value-anchored price. Here's the adapted framework:

### Step 1: Understand the Client's Business Problem
Before you can price on value, you need to understand what the problem is worth to them. Ask questions like:
- What is this project supposed to accomplish for your business?
- What happens if this problem doesn't get solved?
- What's the expected revenue impact if we succeed?
- What have you already spent trying to solve this?

You're not just gathering project requirements. You're doing a financial discovery that tells you what the engagement is *worth* before you name a price.

### Step 2: Quantify the Expected Gain
If a client is running a $2M/year business and your email marketing overhaul conservatively increases their revenue by 10%, that's $200,000 in new revenue. If you charge $20,000, you're charging 10 cents on the dollar for a guaranteed outcome. That's not expensive — it's a no-brainer.

The math:
- Client's annual revenue: $2,000,000
- Conservative lift from your work: 10%
- Dollar value of lift: $200,000
- Your price at 10% of value: $20,000
- Client's ROI: 10x

### Step 3: Enhance Your Perceived Value
This is where most people get stuck. You can't just declare a high price — you have to *earn* the client's belief that you're worth it. Dunn identifies several value enhancers:

- **Case studies with outcomes** — "I helped a similar business increase revenue by $150K in 90 days"
- **Niche expertise** — being the specialist signals lower risk and higher competence
- **Process and methodology** — having a defined approach signals professionalism
- **Social proof** — testimonials from recognizable clients or outcomes
- **Risk reduction** — satisfaction guarantees, milestone-based payment, money-back on roadmapping sessions

### Step 4: Price at a Fraction of Value
The rule of thumb: price at 10–20% of the measurable value you deliver. This gives the client a compelling ROI while ensuring you're well-compensated for expertise.

At $10K–$30K for an engagement, most B2B clients will say yes if they can see $100K–$300K in expected return. The challenge is making that ROI visible and believable.

## The Positioning Change That Makes Pricing Work

Raising your price without changing your value positioning will fail. This is the core warning in Dunn's methodology.

The problem is not your price. The problem is what the client believes they're buying.

If a client thinks they're buying "a website," $5,000 sounds expensive. If they think they're buying "a system that will convert 3x more visitors into paying customers," $15,000 sounds like a deal.

Same work. Completely different price tolerance.

The reframe: stop describing what you *do* and start describing what the client *gets*.

| Instead of this... | Say this... |
|---|---|
| "I build websites" | "I build websites that convert visitors into clients" |
| "I write email newsletters" | "I build automated email systems that nurture leads into buyers" |
| "I do strategy consulting" | "I help coaching businesses double their monthly retainer income" |

Every time you describe your work in deliverable terms (what you produce), you're inviting hourly-rate comparisons. Every time you describe it in outcome terms (what the client gains), you're having a value conversation.

## Overcoming Imposter Syndrome on Pricing

The most common reason consultants undercharge: they don't believe they're worth more.

Dunn addresses this directly. Arbitrarily raising your rates without a value framework feels dishonest — because it is. You're asking for more money without giving the client a reason to say yes.

But when you've done the value calculation — when you can show a client that your $15,000 engagement will generate $150,000 in new revenue — confidence comes naturally. You're not asking for more. You're *offering* them a good deal.

The internal shift: from "I hope they'll pay this" to "I'm offering them 10x ROI and charging a fair fraction of that."

This is why the system matters more than the mindset. Fix the framework first; the confidence follows.

## Practical Pricing Benchmarks

From Dunn's own experience and student results:

- **Starter productized offer**: $1,500–$3,000 (audit, roadmap, or discovery)
- **Mid-tier engagement**: $5,000–$15,000 (90-day project, defined deliverables)
- **Premium retainer**: $5,000–$20,000/month (ongoing advisory, high-touch)
- **One-day intensives/seminars**: $10,000–$25,000 (Dunn himself charges $20K/day)

Dunn notes: students who started at $1,500 packages routinely reach $15,000 for similar-scope projects by changing how they position and price — not by doing fundamentally different work.

## When Performance-Based Pricing Makes Sense

An advanced variant: tie a portion of your fee to results. If you deliver X outcome, you earn a bonus. This works when:
- You have strong data that predicts your impact
- The client can measure the outcome cleanly (revenue, leads, conversions)
- You want to signal extreme confidence in your results

Caution: performance pricing can backfire if you don't control all the variables. External factors (market conditions, client execution) can undermine results you would have otherwise delivered.

---

## The Summary Formula

**Value-Based Price = (Client's expected economic gain) × (10–20%)**

Do the math before every engagement. Know the number. Build your case. Charge accordingly.
