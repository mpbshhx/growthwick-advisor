# Expert Positioning: Niching Down and Building Authority
**Source: Brennan Dunn / Double Your Freelancing**
*doubleyourfreelancing.com/niche/*

## The Core Argument for Niching

Most consultants resist niching because they fear excluding potential clients. Brennan Dunn's answer: you're not excluding — you're attracting. Specificity is what makes a buyer stop scrolling and say "this person is for me."

He uses a simple thought experiment. You run a healthcare clinic and need a website. You find two candidates:

- **Bill**: Generic web designer. Portfolio of varied projects. Technologies listed prominently. Biography. Contact form.
- **Sarah**: "Websites for healthcare clinics who want more patients." Case studies using language like "HIPAA compliance," "patient acquisition," "appointment conversion."

Same technical skill. Completely different perceived value. Sarah wins the project — and commands a higher price — not because she's better, but because she's *legible* to the buyer.

## What a Niche Actually Is

Dunn defines a niche as the intersection of two factors:

**1. Specification** — a particular skill, method, or technology you've mastered
- A copywriter who specializes in high-converting sales pages
- A developer who specializes in query performance optimization
- A coach who specializes in revenue operations for SaaS founders

**2. Focus** — a particular industry, client type, or context you serve
- Healthcare clinics
- E-commerce brands under $5M revenue
- Solopreneur coaches building their first online product

The strongest niche combines both: *who* you serve + *what specific problem* you solve.

> Example: "I help independent financial advisors (focus) build automated email funnels (specification) that convert cold prospects into booked calls."

That's a sentence that makes a financial advisor's head turn. And it makes every other type of buyer self-select out — which is the goal.

## Why Niching Reduces Risk (For Everyone)

Clients hiring a consultant are making a high-stakes decision with imperfect information. They can't test-drive the service. They're betting their time and money on someone they may have just met.

A niche reduces that perceived risk because it signals:
- You've solved this before
- You understand our industry without a steep learning curve
- You speak our language
- We're not your guinea pig

The more familiar you look to the exact problem a buyer has, the more confident they feel paying a premium. Generalists look risky. Specialists look safe.

## The Allergy Clinic Case Study

Dunn cites marketing expert Sean D'Souza's case study about a clinic that helped with all types of allergies. The clinic's original website tried to speak to everyone — broad messaging, all allergies mentioned, nothing specific.

Traffic was poor. Conversions were worse.

D'Souza's advice: focus on one allergy. Make the front door narrow. What happened? The site dramatically increased traffic from people with that specific allergy — but also pulled in people with *other* allergies, because the specificity built trust.

The counterintuitive insight: **specificity attracts more people than generality.** When you demonstrate that you solve a specific problem excellently, people extrapolate that you probably solve adjacent problems excellently too.

Trying to speak to everyone means you're memorable to no one.

## How to Find Your Niche

Dunn recommends working from your actual track record, not from what you wish you were known for.

### Step 1: Catalog your past clients and problems
Go through every client you've worked with. For each:
- What kind of business did they run?
- What was the core problem they hired you to solve?
- What was the measurable outcome you delivered?

Don't think about the technical work you did. Think about the business outcome.

### Step 2: Normalize the list
Group similar problems together. Look for patterns. Most consultants find they've been solving variations of 2–3 core problems — often without naming them.

### Step 3: Identify your most successful case study
Which past engagement had the clearest, most measurable outcome? That outcome is the foundation of your niche positioning. It's the proof that you solve this problem at a high level.

### Step 4: Build the positioning statement
Formula: "I help [specific client type] [achieve specific outcome] using [your method/approach]."

Test it. If someone in your target market reads it and thinks "that's exactly my problem," you've got it.

## Authority Building: Becoming the Recognized Expert

Niching gets you visible to the right people. Authority building gets them to trust you before they've ever spoken to you.

Dunn's authority-building framework comes down to three pillars:

### Pillar 1: Published Thinking
Write about the problems your niche clients face. Not about your service — about *their* world, *their* challenges, *their* mistakes. Publish this consistently in places where your target clients spend time: LinkedIn, newsletters, your own blog.

The goal: when a prospect finds you, they should feel like you already understand their situation. The best way to achieve this is to have written about it in detail, with specificity.

### Pillar 2: Outcome-Anchored Case Studies
Every major client engagement should become a case study. Structure:
1. The client's situation before you
2. What problem they were facing and what it was costing them
3. Your approach (the process)
4. The measurable outcome after

Avoid vague case studies ("helped them grow their business"). Precise numbers build trust: "Helped increase email open rates from 18% to 34%, leading to $47K in additional revenue over 90 days."

### Pillar 3: The Niche Lead Magnet
A lead magnet for a generalist is a liability — it attracts everyone and no one. A niche-specific lead magnet attracts exactly the right people.

Examples:
- "The 5-Part Email Framework for Coaches Selling High-Ticket Programs" (not "5 Email Tips")
- "Why Most SaaS Onboarding Emails Fail (And What To Do Instead)" (for an onboarding specialist)

Dunn notes: the "for freelancers" qualifier on any lead magnet is essential because good lead magnet advice for product businesses looks totally different from what a service provider needs. The same logic applies to your niche.

## Positioning and the "Front Door" Strategy

D'Souza's concept of the "front door" is central to Dunn's positioning philosophy:

> "First get them through the front door. Then move the customers systematically through the rest of your products and services."

Your niche is the front door. It attracts the specific buyer you're best positioned to serve. Once they're in — once they trust you for the specific thing — they'll learn about everything else you do.

This is why niching feels scary but isn't: you don't lose the other opportunities. You just have a clearer entry point that makes it easier for the right people to say yes.

## The Positioning Ladder for Coaches and Consultants

From broad to specific — where you want to be:

| Level | Example | Why It's Weak/Strong |
|---|---|---|
| Too broad | "Business coach" | Every buyer self-qualifies out |
| Better | "Coach for entrepreneurs" | Still vague |
| Good | "Coach for online coaches building group programs" | Specific client type |
| Strong | "I help coaches launch their first group program and fill it in 90 days" | Specific outcome + timeline |
| Authority | "I've helped 40 coaches fill their group programs; here's the framework" | Proof of track record |

Move as far down this ladder as your proof supports. The more you can say "I've done this for people like you," the more powerful your positioning.
