## The Problem With "Just Raise Your Rates"

The most common advice freelancers and consultants receive is the most dangerous advice they receive: "Just raise your rates."

It's dangerous because it's incomplete. It tells you what to do without telling you *how* or *why*. And if you go raise your rates without changing anything else — without changing how you communicate your value — you will fail. You'll lose clients, feel like an imposter, or fold under the first negotiation pushback.

Brennan Dunn built the Double Your Freelancing Rate (DYFR) methodology for exactly this situation. It's a system — not a mindset hack. It's a process for calculating and communicating value in a way that makes higher prices feel fair and justified to the client.

Over 8,000 course alumni have used this methodology to double, triple, and in some cases 10x their rates.

## Why Your Current Rate Is What It Is

You're charging your current rate because, consciously or not, you believe it's what the market will bear.

This belief comes from somewhere:
- It's what you started with when you had no track record
- A client told you your previous price was too high
- You looked at what competitors charge
- You're scared to lose clients

None of these are anchored to the actual economic value you deliver. They're anchored to *your feelings* about what the market will accept. The DYFR methodology replaces those feelings with data.

## The Three-Stage Framework

### Stage 1: Understand the Client's World

Before you quote any number, you need to understand three things about the client:

**1. Their business problem** (not their project request)
Most clients come to you with a request: "I need a new website." The DYFR methodology teaches you to look behind the request to the underlying business problem: "I need more leads because my current site converts at 1% and my competitor's converts at 4%."

The problem — the 3% conversion gap — is what has economic value. The website is just the vehicle.

**2. The cost of inaction**
What happens to their business if they don't solve this? If the conversion gap is costing them $200K/year in lost revenue, the stakes are clear. If it's just a minor inconvenience, the budget will be limited.

**3. Their success criteria**
What does "solved" look like to them? In their words, in their metrics. "I want to go from 10 inquiries/month to 40." Now you have a specific, measurable outcome to price against.

**How to get this information:** Ask it directly, in the first discovery call. Dunn teaches a structured discovery script — questions that move from surface project requirements to underlying business context. Sample questions:
- "What made you decide now was the time to address this?"
- "What does success look like for you six months from now?"
- "What's the expected revenue impact if we nail this?"

Most consultants skip these questions and jump straight to scope. That's why most consultants undercharge.

---

### Stage 2: Calculate and Enhance Perceived Value

Once you understand the client's business problem and the economic stakes, you can calculate a value-anchored price. But before you name the price, you do the work of *enhancing* the client's perception of your value.

**Value Enhancement Levers:**

**A. Risk Reduction**
The biggest purchase barrier for consulting clients is uncertainty: "Will this actually work?" You reduce that fear by:
- Showing case studies with concrete outcomes (not just "helped them grow")
- Offering a satisfaction guarantee on discovery sessions
- Proposing milestone-based payments tied to deliverables
- Starting with a smaller paid engagement (the roadmap) before the big project

**B. Expertise Signals**
Niche positioning, published content, and outcome-based case studies all signal that you've solved this before. Each signal reduces the perceived risk of hiring you and increases the price they're willing to pay.

**C. Social Proof**
One well-placed testimonial from a recognizable name in their industry can shift the entire conversation. "Oh, you worked with [X]? Let's talk about budget."

**D. The Alternative Analysis**
Help the client understand what it costs NOT to solve the problem, or what it would cost to solve it another way (hiring internally, working with a larger agency). This anchors your price against real alternatives — and often makes you look remarkably affordable.

### Stage 3: Price Confidently and Defend It

The DYFR methodology culminates in a specific pricing practice:

**Name the price with context, not apology.**

Wrong: "So my rate is $150/hour... I know that might seem high but..."
Right: "Based on what you've shared — that you're losing roughly $30K/month from this conversion gap — I'm proposing a $12,000 engagement to fix it. That's less than half of one month's loss, and we'd aim to have this resolved in six weeks."

The difference: the second version anchors the price to the client's problem and makes the ROI math obvious. The client isn't evaluating whether $12,000 is expensive. They're evaluating whether recovering $30K/month is worth $12,000 upfront. Of course it is.

**When the client pushes back on price:**

Don't cave immediately. Dunn teaches a specific response:

1. Acknowledge the concern: "I hear you — that's a significant investment."
2. Reframe to value: "Walk me through what concerns you — is it the price itself, or uncertainty about the outcome?"
3. Offer a smaller entry point: "What if we started with a paid discovery session at $1,500? We'd validate the approach, and you'd have everything you need to make a confident decision about the full engagement."

The smaller entry point (roadmapping/paid discovery) is the ultimate objection handler. It's low risk for the client, paid work for you, and a filter that shows who's serious.

## The Rate Increase Plan for Existing Clients

One of the most practical pieces of DYFR: how to raise rates with clients who already know your old price.

**The forward-raising strategy:**
Don't retroactively raise rates on an active engagement. Wait until a natural inflection point — end of a project, a contract renewal, a new scope request.

Then say: "Before we kick off the next phase, I want to let you know my rates have changed since we started working together. I've refined my process significantly, and my new rate for this type of work is [X]. I want to be transparent about that now so there are no surprises."

You're giving them a choice. Clients who value what you do will accept the new rate. Clients who push back hard are clients who were treating you as a commodity — and those aren't the ones you want to build your business around anyway.

**The new client premium:**
Always charge new clients your target rate, not your legacy rate. It's much easier to raise rates with new clients (they have no anchor) than existing ones. Over time, as you cycle clients, your average rate rises without any dramatic confrontation.

## The Confidence Loop

Here's why the DYFR system works psychologically, not just strategically:

When you can genuinely show that your $15,000 engagement will generate $150,000 in client value, you stop feeling like you're asking for more than you deserve. You're not extracting — you're *offering a good deal*. That internal shift changes how you carry yourself in the proposal conversation. Clients feel the confidence.

Confidence is not what you develop before you raise your rates. It's what naturally emerges when you have a system that proves your price is fair.

## Quick-Reference: The DYFR Process in 6 Steps

1. **Discovery call**: Ask business-context questions, not just project questions
2. **Quantify the problem**: What is solving this worth to them?
3. **Enhance your value**: Case studies, niche signals, risk reducers
4. **Calculate your price**: 10–20% of measurable client value
5. **Propose with context**: State the price, state the ROI math
6. **Defend or defer to discovery**: If they push back, offer the roadmap
