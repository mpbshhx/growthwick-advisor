# Office Hours: Can You Actually Track AI Visibility?

By Rand Fishkin / SparkToro | March 11, 2026

## The Core Question

"Can you even track AI visibility?"

If you've used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Google AI Mode for more than five minutes, you know the problem: ask the same question twice, and you probably won't get the same answer twice. Different brands. Different order. Different framing. Sometimes a totally different list.

So when marketers started asking whether they should track "AI rankings," Rand got skeptical — "possibly 'this whole thing might be bullshit' skeptical."

SparkToro decided to test it (in partnership with Gumshoe.ai).

## The Answer: More Nuanced Than Either Extreme

- **AI "rankings"?** Probably nonsense.
- **AI visibility tracked across many prompts and many runs?** Surprisingly useful.

## What the Research Found

SparkToro + Gumshoe.ai ran hundreds of prompts across major AI tools. Key findings:

- AI tools return different brand recommendations for the **exact same prompt** across runs
- Asking ChatGPT once and screenshotting the answer tells you almost nothing
- Real people almost never phrase AI prompts the same way — even when they have the same goal
- **"Visibility percentage"** (how often you appear across many prompts/runs) may be more useful than rank position
- AI visibility tracking gets harder in broad, crowded categories
- PR, brand mentions, and consistent language may influence AI visibility over time

## What This Means for Marketers

**Don't:**
- Obsess over one-off AI answers
- Build dashboards around "AI rank" for individual queries
- Let executive anxiety about AI visibility drive measurement frameworks

**Do:**
- Track visibility percentage across many prompt variations
- Use PR and brand mention strategies to build consistent AI presence over time
- Understand that your category's size affects trackability — broader categories are harder to measure

## The Practical Implication

When your boss, client, or CMO asks why the brand "doesn't show up in AI," the answer is:
- Single-query AI answers are not stable enough to mean anything
- You need many prompts, many runs, tracked over time
- The right question is: "What percentage of relevant AI conversations include us?"
- That requires methodology, not a screenshot
