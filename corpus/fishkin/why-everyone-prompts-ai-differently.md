# Why Everyone Prompts AI Differently (Even When They Want the Same Thing)

**Author: Rand Fishkin / Amanda Natividad**

## The Experiment

Eight moms — similar socioeconomic backgrounds, kids around the same age — were asked to look up local basketball leagues for their kids. Only instruction: search as though they really wanted a good answer.

Every single one did it differently.

- One roleplayed: *"You are a well-known youth basketball coach in [region]... What are the best basketball leagues in my area? I want him to be nurtured and taught all the skills..."*
- One focused on location: *"Please find a basketball league for ages 7 to 10 near the [ZIP code] area."*
- One gave ChatGPT a name but forgot to specify her area: *"Hey Dolly, can you please recommend basketball leagues for my 7yr old daughter in my area?"*
- Author's prompt: *"Can you recommend some not competitive basketball clubs or leagues?"*

Same intent. Same life stage. Wildly different prompts.

## What SparkToro's Research Found

In the AI visibility study with Gumshoe, hundreds of people wrote prompts expressing the same underlying intent. Average semantic similarity score across human-written prompts: **0.081** — meaning the wording varied dramatically, even when everyone wanted the same type of answer.

This is normal human behavior. People don't reduce intent to a neat set of keywords. They explain. They qualify. They add context. They describe constraints. They personalize the question based on their own experience.

In other words: **people don't "search" anymore. They ask. And increasingly, they talk.**

## Same Intent, Different Words — Similar Results

AI visibility tools often unintentionally inherit assumptions from keyword-based search:
- There's a small, representative set of prompts worth tracking
- Phrasing is relatively stable
- Optimizing for a handful of queries gets you most of the way there

But the research shows why that assumption falls apart. If ten people with the same need phrase their question ten different ways, tracking visibility on one or two "ideal" prompts tells you very little about how often you're actually showing up for real people.

**The reassuring finding:** Despite massive variation in how humans wrote their prompts, AI tools often returned similar clusters of brands across those phrasings. The wording and/or order changed, but the set of recommendations frequently overlapped.

AI models are generally good at recognizing underlying intent, even when the surface language looks very different.

**The reframed question:** Not "which exact prompt should I optimize for?" but "**am I showing up reliably across the full semantic neighborhood of this intent?**"

## AI Visibility is an Audience Research Problem, Not a Measurement Problem

If you want to appear in AI answers, don't start by asking which prompt to track. Start by asking:

- How do people actually describe this problem?
- What constraints do they mention?
- What words do they use when they're unsure, overwhelmed, or comparing options?
- How does their language change based on experience level, confidence, or urgency?
- How am I providing answers to these questions, online, in language that matches how real people talk?

**SparkToro's approach:** Getting mentioned by LLMs wasn't the strategy. Educating people about audience research, making it easy for marketers, and getting them to associate audience research with SparkToro — through content creation, distribution, and PR — made AI visibility the *outcome*, not the target.

## The Structural Advantage

Teams who already invest in audience research — listening to real language in forums, reviews, social posts, support tickets, and conversations — and tie that knowledge to their marketing have a structural advantage.

They're not just building a better content strategy. They're building the kind of distributed, interconnected, real-language presence that AI models draw on when forming recommendations:
- Blog posts that link to each other
- Transcripts in YouTube videos
- Mentions in subreddits
- Customer support resources that are easy to find and parse
- Earned media that puts the brand in the right context

## What To Do With This

Once you've done the audience research, translate it into content. If you discover that your customers describe their problem as "I don't know where to start" rather than "I need a solution for X," that's not just a persona insight — that's a phrase to build content around.

Write the blog post. Answer the forum question. Create the comparison guide. Get that language indexed and crawled across the web in ways that position you as the obvious answer.

**The eight parents reflected slightly different mental models of the same task.** But they were all looking for an age-appropriate basketball league. They just had their slightly different filters and their own ways of communicating.

If your AI visibility strategy assumes people ask questions the same way, it's already misaligned with reality. But if you've spent real time understanding how your audience talks, you're building something that works with the way AI models actually operate.
