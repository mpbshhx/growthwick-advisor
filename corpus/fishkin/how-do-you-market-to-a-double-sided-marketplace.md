# How Do You Market to a Double-Sided Marketplace?

By Rand Fishkin / SparkToro

## The Core Problem

A double-sided marketplace (like Thumbtack or TheKnot) has two audiences — but essentially one trust problem:
- Homeowners need to trust they'll find a reliable pro
- Pros need to trust the platform will send them real jobs worth taking

## Insight #1: Homeowners Search by Problem, Not by Platform

Homeowners aren't thinking "I should browse a home services marketplace." They're thinking:
- My roof is leaking
- I need someone to install this ceiling fan
- How much should a kitchen remodel cost?

Top keywords: "hire a handyman," "average kitchen remodel cost," "roof leak repair," "licensed contractors near me," "ceiling fan installation near me"

Top AI prompt topics: "Home Renovation Tips," "Affordable Home Repairs," "Best Local Handymen," "Average Kitchen Remodel Cost"

**Marketing implication:** Don't start with "Use our trusted marketplace!" Start with the moment of need:
- "Need a ceiling fan installed?"
- "Trying to understand what a kitchen remodel should cost?"
- "Roof leaking?"

Homeowners are solving specific problems. Meet them there.

## Insight #2: Pros Want Leads, Not Another Profile to Manage

Provider-side searches are almost comically direct:
- "join thumbtack," "thumbtack pros," "get leads online," "find customers online"
- "grow service business," "find local clients," "how to get more jobs"
- "lead generation for contractors," "contractor marketing tools," "home service leads"

**The actual job to be done:** "Fill your schedule with real local jobs without becoming a full-time marketer."

Pros aren't looking for another dashboard or marketplace badge. They want customers. Ideally good ones — local, ready to hire, not just price-shopping.

Two pro-facing marketing paths:
1. **Lead generation:** Show pros how the platform gets them more of the right jobs
2. **Business growth:** Help them run a better service business (pricing, scheduling, customer communication, estimates, reviews, repeat bookings)

A good pro-side campaign feels less like "come join our app" and more like "here's how to build a healthier local service business."

## Insight #3: The Best Marketplace Marketing Helps Both Sides Trust Each Other

Both audiences share the same general home services ecosystem (Angi, HomeAdvisor, Porch, TaskRabbit, Homeyou, Fixr, etc.) but enter with different anxieties:

**Homeowners:** Can I trust this person? What should this cost? Am I going to get ripped off?

**Pros:** How do I avoid tire-kickers? Can I get better local clients? How do I grow without spending all day on marketing?

**The flywheel:** Homeowners need confidence they can find reliable pros → Pros need confidence the platform sends real demand → Best marketing makes each side more confident in the other.

### Dual-purpose marketing examples:
- "What should this cost?" guide → helps homeowners, signals platform attracts high-intent customers
- "How to win better local jobs" → helps pros, reassures homeowners the platform cares about pro quality
- "How to hire without getting burned" → attracts homeowners, sets expectations that help good pros win

## What We'd Actually Do

**For homeowners:** Content around specific jobs and trust moments
- "What should this cost?" / "When should you DIY vs. hire?"
- "How to spot a reliable pro" / "What to ask before hiring a contractor"
- Distribution: Search, YouTube, Reddit, home improvement media
- Key subreddits: r/HomeImprovement, r/DIY, r/InteriorDesign, r/PropertyManagement

**For pros:** Lead quality, local demand, and business growth
- "How to get more local clients" / "How to turn more estimates into booked jobs"
- "How to avoid bad-fit leads" / "How to price jobs profitably"
- Distribution: Contractor business podcasts — Service Business Mastery, Contractor Growth Network, Handyman Success Podcast

A double-sided marketplace doesn't just need more traffic — it needs confidence on both sides.
