# NEW Research: AIs are highly inconsistent when recommending brands or products; marketers should take care when tracking AI visibility

**Author: Rand Fishkin**

## The Problem

For the last few years, companies have been investing inordinate sums into AI tracking and AI visibility for their brands and products. $100M+/yr is already estimated to be spent on this new version of search analytics — and yet there was no research showing whether AI tools are consistent enough, when prompted for lists of brand/product recommendations, to produce valid visibility metrics.

There are loads of studies about AI accuracy, but when it came to answering the question of whether ChatGPT, Claude, or Google AI produce consistent enough lists of results to be usefully tracked… nothing.

## The Experiment

**Hypothesis:** AI tools produce such randomized lists of recommendations, and user prompts are so highly varied, that attempting to track rankings or visibility of a brand/product for a given topic space or user intent is pointless.

**Method:** 600 volunteers ran 12 different prompts through ChatGPT, Claude, and Google AI (AI Overview / AI Mode) a combined 2,961 times. They copied and pasted AI tool responses into survey forms, which were normalized into ordered product/brand results.

## Key Finding: AIs almost never give the same list twice

**If you ask an AI tool for brand/product recommendations a hundred times, nearly every response will be unique in three ways:**
- The list presented
- The order of the recommendations
- The number of items on that list (sometimes 2-3, equally often 10+)

**Statistical reality:**
- There's a <1 in 100 chance that ChatGPT or Google's AI, asked 100X, will give you the same list of brands in any two responses
- Claude is slightly more likely to give the same list twice but even less likely to do so in the same order
- When it comes to ordering, AI tool responses are so random it's more like 1 in 1,000 runs before you'd see two lists in the same order

**Bottom line:** AIs do not give consistent lists of brand or product recommendations. If you don't like an answer, or your brand doesn't show up where you want it to, just ask a few more times.

## Is There Any Way to Order This Randomized Chaos?

**Surprising answer: Yes.** The original hypothesis — that AI brand answer lists are so random as for tracking to be entirely useless — was probably wrong.

**Key insight:** Even if rankings are random to the point of near-uselessness, *appearances across dozens or hundreds of runs of the same prompt* indicates a set of brands that the AI's system generally associates more (or less) as a good answer for the prompt intent.

**Example:** When Google's AI was asked to recommend digital marketing consultants with expertise in e-commerce, the Smartsites agency appeared in 85 out of 95 responses. That's meaningful.

**Measuring visibility % across many prompt runs** is (probably) a reasonable way to know how prominent or invisible your entity is within the AI's consideration set.

**Category size matters:** How often a brand shows up may have less to do with relative prominence and more to do with how many potential recommendations the AI is choosing from. Cloud computing providers for SaaS startups = high pairwise correlation (small set). Recently-published SciFi novels = low pairwise correlation (huge set).

## The Prompt Variability Problem

**Huge problem for AI tracking:** Even if visibility % is a reasonable metric, the prompts themselves are wildly variable.

In an experiment, 142 people wrote prompts expressing the same underlying intent. **Average semantic similarity score: 0.081.** (Like the average pair of prompts were Kung Pao Chicken and Peanut Butter — key ingredients overlapped, but they're not especially close.)

People don't reduce intent to keywords. They explain. They qualify. They add context. They describe constraints. They personalize.

**BUT** — despite massive prompt variation, AI tools often returned similar clusters of brands across those phrasings. The wording and order changed, but the set of recommendations frequently overlapped.

**Example (headphones for traveling family member):** 994 AI responses across 142 human-crafted prompts → Bose, Sony, Sennheiser, and Apple showed up 55-77% of the time. Remarkably similar to results from tighter spaces.

## Research Conclusions

1. **AIs rarely give the same list of brands or recommendations twice** — stop screenshotting one ChatGPT answer and calling it data
2. **Ranking position in AI responses is nearly meaningless** — don't track it
3. **Visibility % across many prompt runs has statistical validity** — this is the metric worth tracking
4. **Prompt diversity doesn't break visibility tracking** — AI models recognize underlying intent despite wildly different phrasings
5. **Before spending on AI tracking tools:** Make sure your provider publishes transparent, publicly-reviewable research on their methodology

## What This Means for Marketers

- Don't obsess over "ranking" in AI the way you rank on Google
- Track visibility percentage across many prompts and many runs
- One-off screenshots are meaningless
- Consistent brand presence across varied prompts is what matters
- AI tracking vendors who don't show their math should not be trusted
