# Inimitable Product is the New "Make Great Content"

**Author: Rand Fishkin**

For 25 years, Google told websites to "just make great content," and they'd sort out the rest. This advice was incomplete, problematic, and reductive, but also… sorta true. If you made 10X Content in 1997, 2007, or 2017, it often worked (at least, so long as you *marketed* that 10X content).

But, no more.

## Google's Plan: Elegant, Simple, and Evil

Google's future is no longer indexing the web and making information universally useful and available — it's wholesale, intentional, unrepentant theft: the great digital enclosure of publishing.

Google's plan:
- Crawl and index the web's content
- Use their mafia-like power to force a prisoner's dilemma paradox (if you don't let them index your stuff, they'll just take it from someone else and reward them with the scraps of traffic)
- Use that content to train their models/power their answers
- Make your work a redundant commodity (everything published can now be found in a more convenient format inside Google's interface)
- Only send clicks when businesses either: A) Pay them for ads, or B) Offer something Google can't provide directly in their AI-powered answer interface

AI is statistically the least trusted, most hated technology of our lifetimes, but popular sentiment is doing nothing to halt its progress among the tech, business, and finance elite. Google no longer wants to be the gateway to the web. They want to be the web.

## Businesses and Creators Have But Two Solutions

**Solution 1 — Long-term:** Collective political action. Citizens supporting politicians who'd empower the DOJ and FTC, breaking up monopolies, making self-preferencing illegal. Rand cynically suspects this won't happen fast enough.

**Solution 2 — Short-term:** Build inimitable products.

Despite all the seeming wonders of Google's new AI-powered, agentic search engine, it cannot:
- Invent ultrasonic chef's knives
- Tailor a made-to-measure suit with an ocean of whimsical personality
- Thoughtfully curate gift boxes from local makers
- Lovingly prepare the meal of your life
- Refine pottery techniques for thousands of years to make the perfect vase
- Source WWI-era Armagnac to serve your 98-year-old grandfather something older than him
- Hand-carve and paint tiny creatures
- Mow your lawn or fix your roof or service your printer or manage your 401k

**To make inimitable products, you'll need to:**
1. Uncover the overlap in what you're passionately capable of creating, what other solutions in the market fail to offer, and what people in that market desperately want
2. Create that overlapping thing in a way that's designed for Zero Click Marketing: it can be shown via short-form video, amplified with just a few images, easily described by a rabid fan, and made enticing though a brief summary atop an AI answer
3. Get the word out through the channels where your audience pays attention: social media, PR, advertising, search engines and AI answers, and word-of-mouth

## So Content Marketing is Dead?

Not quite — but the writing's on the wall. Only a fool would double down on "make great content" (10X, 100X, or 10^100X) as the path forward.

**Nearly universal advice for the web's creators:**
- Ignore traffic
- Make inimitable products
- Shift priorities away from "great content" on your own site toward "great marketing" on the platforms where your audience pays attention

**The new funnel:**
- Influence is the new traffic
- Impressions and engagement are the new top of funnel
- Branded search volume is the new middle
- Sales remains the true measure of success

**The brutal takeaway:** Inimitable products may be defensible moats on which to build a web business, but inimitable content is not. Content as product is dying, and that death spiral is accelerating.

- The New York Times is just a games platform with a vestigial news appendage
- Bloomberg makes money on terminals and uses the profits to fund their content machine (which markets their inimitable product: the terminal)
- Not every Substack newsletter will die, not every Patreon creator will fail — but most will need to find an inimitable product to survive and thrive

## So What is Even the Point of Publishing Anymore?

Why Rand still publishes (sometimes), and why you might continue to do some (though probably much less):

- **To influence search engines and AI tools** — who still need sources to train off and pull from
- **To have a home base for an idea** — something that can be cited, quoted, screenshot'd, and referred back to as the original source
- **For the most loyal fans** — even if that group is 1/100th the size it once might have been
- **To distribute via email** — one of the last owned channels where big tech's power hasn't yet removed value
- **To exercise longer-form, more thoughtful creation** — illustrating that ability to those few people with longer attention spans and deeper care about the problem

"I expect this post to receive <5,000 visits, <500 from search, and <100 people who read every word. But if they're the right ones, and if the piece creates external influence beyond that, it may still be worthwhile. Especially since, living behind this content, funding its creation, is an inimitable product."
