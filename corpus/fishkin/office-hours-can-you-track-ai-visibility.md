# Office Hours: Can You Actually Track AI Visibility?

By Rand Fishkin, SparkToro (recorded March 11, 2026)

## The Question Behind This Session

"Can you even track AI visibility?"

Because if you've used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or Google AI Mode for more than five minutes, you already know the problem: ask the same question twice, and you probably won't get the same answer twice. Different brands. Different order. Different framing. Sometimes a totally different list.

So when marketers started asking whether they should track "AI rankings," Rand got skeptical. Very skeptical. Possibly "this whole thing might be bullshit" skeptical.

---

## What They Tested

SparkToro partnered with Gumshoe.ai to research AI answer consistency, brand visibility, and prompt variation across major AI tools. They ran hundreds of prompts across ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Google AI Mode.

---

## Key Findings and Takeaways

**AI "rankings" are usually the wrong thing to measure.**
Unlike Google search rankings — which are reasonably consistent for a given query — AI model outputs vary dramatically run-to-run and tool-to-tool.

**Real people almost never phrase AI prompts the same way** — even when they have the same underlying goal. This makes "rank tracking" for AI essentially meaningless.

**How often AI tools return different brand recommendations for the exact same prompt:**
Asking the same prompt twice produces different brand lists more often than expected. This variance is structurally built into how LLMs work (temperature, sampling, context).

**Screenshotting one AI answer tells you almost nothing.**
If you asked ChatGPT "what are the best project management tools?" and screenshotted the answer, you captured one random output from a probabilistic system — not a stable ranking.

**Visibility percentage may be more useful than rank position.**
Rather than "what rank is our brand," the better question: "across 100 similar prompts, what percentage of the time does our brand appear?" This shifts from rank-tracking to visibility rate — a much more meaningful and statistically stable metric.

**AI tracking gets harder in broad, crowded categories.**
In niche categories with few known players, AI visibility is more consistent. In broad categories (e.g., "best CRM"), the variance is extreme.

---

## What Marketers Should Do Instead

1. **Don't obsess over one-off AI answers.** They're noise, not signal.

2. **Focus on PR, brand mentions, and consistent language.** Over time, these likely influence AI visibility — AI models are trained on the web, so brands that appear authoritatively across many sources have better AI visibility.

3. **Track visibility percentage, not rankings.** Run many prompts across variations, measure brand appearance rate.

4. **Understand AI visibility tracking may mostly be executive anxiety in dashboard form.** Many tracking tools are selling comfort, not insight.

5. **The underlying principles remain:** Create remarkable content, build real audience relationships, and ensure your brand is mentioned by other trusted sources — these feed AI visibility the same way they fed SEO.

---

## Connecting to the Broader SparkToro Thesis

This session connects to Rand's core argument about the zero-click, AI-assisted web: the traditional "show up when searched" model is eroding. The future of marketing is about being present where your audience already is — trusted sources, other brands, communities — not just ranking in search results or AI outputs.
